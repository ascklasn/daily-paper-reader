Abstract 

Large language models(LLMs) increasingly match expert performance on licensing examinations, yet 

routine clinical use remains limited because governance requires auditable reasoning, safety and ethics 

alignment, and resilience to adversarial misuse. Here we present SafeMed-R1, trained with a traceable 

Clinical Trust Signals(CTS) pipeline that links each reasoning instance to clinician rubric scores and edit 

histories, and aligned through safety and ethics supervision and red team stress testing. SafeMed-R1 

attains a macro-averaged accuracy of 79.6% across clinical benchmarks. Under adversarial safety testing, 

it shows the lowest aggregated risk and reduces unsafe outputs by about 3 to 5% relative to its baseline. 

In a paired expert study of 30 medication safety vignettes, SafeMed-R1 matches PGY1 and PGY2 

residents on medical correctness and scores higher for medication safety, guideline consistency, and 

clinical usefulness. Collectively, these results suggest that clinician-audited supervision provenance, 

together with domain-tailored safety and ethics alignment, can strengthen governance-relevant evidence 

without relying on inference-time retrieval or citation grounding. 

Keywords: medical LLMs ; safety alignment; medical ethics; adversarial evaluation; medication safety 1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) and other generative artificial intelligence (AI) systems are rapidly 

expanding the frontier of clinical language understanding and generation, enabling applications such as 

clinical question answering, note and discharge summary drafting, triage support, patient education, 

and workflow automation 1-3 . Recent advances have also yielded strong performance on medical 

question answering, with multiple reports of expert-level accuracy on licensing-style examinations 4-8 .

Despite rapid progress on benchmarks, routine deployment at the level of health systems remains 

limited. The bottleneck is increasingly trustworthiness under clinical governance rather than raw 

capability: institutions require safety, accountability, and compliance under uncertainty, alongside clear 

responsibility when failures occur. Prior work has cautioned against “unrestricted and unmonitored ” AI 

experimentation in healthcare and argued for institutional oversight mechanisms spanning pre-review, 

transparency, and continuous monitoring s9-11 . Complementary perspectives emphasise that trust in 

AI-assisted care is a multi-party relationship that depends on independent validation, transparency, and 

shared accountability across patients, clinicians, and developers. Taken together, these views imply that 

deployment-readiness requires governance-grade evidence — auditable, traceable, and stress-tested 

assurances —not only benchmark performance. In parallel, open-source alternatives (e.g., the DeepSeek 

series) have gained momentum in China and are being explored for clinical decision support and 

specialty-specific workflows, further amplifying the need for deployment-oriented governance 

evidence 12-15 .

In this work, we focus on a specific —but practically central —form of auditability: the provenance of 

supervision. By traceability we refer to the ability to inspect how a model ’ s target behaviours were 

supervised and validated (e.g., clinician rubric scores, edits, and adjudication records attached to each 

training/evaluation item), rather than inference-time evidence attribution to external literature. 

Document-grounded answering with retrieval and citation verification is an important and 

complementary direction, but it is orthogonal to the present study. 

We therefore frame deployment-oriented medical LLM development around three governance-relevant 

requirements: (i) clinician-audited supervision provenance for reasoning-style outputs, (ii) 

ethics-and-safety alignment as a first-class objective, and (iii) robustness under worst-case misuse. This 

framing highlights three gaps that can hinder deployment readiness even when examination scores are 

high. 

Gap 1: Limited governance-grade supervision for reasoning-style outputs. Most datasets primarily 

assess whether the final answer is correct, providing limited information about how the reasoning 

content was reviewed, revised, and approved by clinicians. In real clinical settings, correctness alone is 

insufficient for governance: institutions must be able to audit how safety checks, contraindication 

considerations, and differential reasoning were supervised and to localize failure modes for 

remediation 16 . We address this gap by constructing a clinician-audited corpus in which each QA –reasoning instance is linked to structured rubric scores and edit histories, forming an auditable trail of 

supervision signals that supports training, audit, and remediation. 

Gap 2: Ethics and safety are rarely treated as first-class objectives. Existing evaluations often 

under-represent scenarios where ethical norms and institutional policies determine appropriate 

action —such as informed consent, privacy boundaries, vulnerable populations, and inappropriate 

requests that require refusal. Meanwhile, emerging guidance urges that ethical evaluation be 

transparent and verifiable, such as through structured reporting and governance-aligned checklists, 

instead of merely consisting of narrative claims. For clinical deployment, the desired behaviour is not 

only to refuse unsafe requests, but also to recognise high-risk contexts, explain why the request is 

unsafe, and provide safer, guideline-consistent alternatives 17 . Therefore, safety alignment must be 

measured as a positive, domain-specific capability rather than a generic refusal pattern. 

Gap 3: System-level robustness under adversarial misuse remains understudied. Prompt injection 

and jailbreak-style attacks can elicit unsafe outputs, and recent work has shown that such 

vulnerabilities can persist in medical multimodal settings, indicating that benign, exam-style testing 

may underestimate the real-world risk. Health systems therefore need evidence of worst-case behaviour 

through deliberate stress testing and red-teaming protocols before integrating LLMs into patient-facing 

or clinician-facing pathways 18 . In practice, a model ’s safety case must include adversarial evaluation 

that approximates how systems fail under pressure, not only how they perform under cooperative 

prompting. 

To address these gaps, we present a governance-oriented evidence package for deployment-oriented 

medical LLMs and instantiate it in SafeMed-R1, a Chinese clinical reasoning model built on 

Qwen3-32B with a data-centric alignment pipeline. Specifically, this work contributes: (i) Clinical 

Trust Signals (CTS), a clinician-audited supervision provenance scheme that links each QA –

reasoning item to rubric scores, edit histories, and adjudication decisions; (ii) an operationalization of 

ethics and safety as measurable, auditable objectives via MedSafety and MedEthics, together with 

worst-case robustness assessment via MedSafetyBench (MSB) and red-team stress testing; (iii) a

reference implementation, SafeMed-R1, evaluated across exam-style competence, safety/ethics, and 

adversarial misuse; and (iv) an integrated evidence package spanning capability, auditable safety/ethics 

performance, quantified worst-case risk, and clinician-rated vignette comparisons. 

2. Results 

We evaluate SafeMed-R1 against baselines on datasets (Table S1, Methods 4.3 –4.4). To demonstrate 

both the clinical competence and safety alignment of SafeMed-R1, we perform a comprehensive 

evaluation across several medical datasets, including knowledge tests and safety and ethics datasets. In 

total, we consider 4 clinical ability benchmarks and 3 safety benchmarks, covering high-risk licensed 

scenarios, multi-role clinical practice, pediatric medicine, and adversarial security attacks. A more detailed description of the SafeMed-R1 can be found in the Method section (Overview can be seen in 

Figure 1). 

2.1 Clinical capability as a prerequisite 

To assess clinical problem-solving ability, we evaluated SafeMed-R1 and baselines on four Chinese 

medical examination datasets: MedQA (Taiwan and Mainland subsets), CMExam (validation and test 

splits), Chinese-Exam (physician, pharmacist, and nurse tracks), and the multiple-choice subset of 

PediaBench (Figure 2, table 1). These datasets collectively cover cross-regional licensing examinations 

and role-specific professional assessments. Across all four benchmarks, SafeMed-R1 achieved the 

highest macro-average accuracy (79.6%) among 11 models, outperforming the base Qwen3-32B 

(77.7%), its supervised fine-tuned (SFT) variant (78.3%), and the strongest external medical model 

Baichuan-M2-32B (77.6%). These results indicate that SafeMed-R1 improves clinical examination 

performance beyond both its foundation model and strong medical LLM baselines. 

On the Mainland MedQA subset, SafeMed-R1 reached 85.5% accuracy, improving over Qwen3-32B 

(82.5%). On MedQA-TW, SafeMed-R1 achieved 85.1%, remaining competitive with high-performing 

systems while narrowing the gap to leading proprietary and distilled baselines (Table 1). On CMExam, 

SafeMed-R1 obtained 77.6% and 78.2% accuracy on the validation and test splits, respectively, again 

surpassing Qwen3-32B (75.4% and 76.4%), suggesting improved generalization across examination 

formats. On Chinese-Exam role-specific tracks (physician, pharmacist, and nurse), SafeMed-R1 

matched or closely approached the strongest baseline performance across roles, and on PediaBench it 

achieved 82.9% accuracy, supporting generalization to pediatric dosing and age-specific knowledge 

(Fig. 2c). High examination-style accuracy is a prerequisite, but it is insufficient for governance. We 

next evaluate whether SafeMed-R1 meets auditable safety-and-ethics criteria and remains safe under 

adversarial misuse. 

2.2 Auditable safety and ethics performance 

To support deployment in high-risk clinical environments, safety and ethics should be treated as 

auditable governance criteria rather than auxiliary performance metrics. Here, we define auditability as 

the ability for clinicians and governance teams to (i) verify that model responses adhere to explicit 

safety and ethics checks (e.g., medication-risk precautions, escalation thresholds, professional 

boundaries, and privacy considerations), (ii) localize failures to actionable risk categories for 

remediation, and (iii) quantify worst-case unsafe behavior under adversarial misuse. We operationalize 

these criteria using MedSafety and MedEthics as structured audits of safety- and ethics-grounded 

clinical behavior, and MedSafetyBench (MSB) as a stress test of misuse resilience. 

Across the two governance-oriented benchmarks, SafeMed-R1 demonstrates strong safety and ethics 

audit performance, scoring 81.3 on MedSafety and 80.7 on MedEthics. These results indicate that the 

model more consistently satisfies checklist-like safety requirements (e.g., avoiding high-risk dosing 

errors and unsafe recommendations) while maintaining professional, ethics-grounded reasoning under clinical constraints. Importantly, these aggregate scores are interpretable as auditable evidence because 

they reflect performance under explicitly defined safety and ethics criteria rather than open-ended 

helpfulness. 

We further assess adversarially induced risk using MSB. Under this adversarial audit, SafeMed-R1 

achieves the lowest overall average risk among evaluated systems and reduces unsafe behavior by 

approximately 3 – 5% relative to its base model under adversarial attacks. Together, 

MedSafety/MedEthics and MSB provide complementary governance evidence: the former quantify 

consistent execution of domain safety/ethics checks, while the latter characterizes a conservative upper 

bound on unsafe behavior under realistic misuse patterns (Table 2). 

Collectively, these benchmarks function as auditable evidence: MedSafety/MedEthics indicate more 

consistent execution of domain-specific safety/ethics checks, while MSB characterizes worst-case 

failure under misuse. 

2.3 Adversarial testability and risk decomposition across safety/ethics subdomains 

Aggregate safety and ethics scores can mask clinically meaningful failure modes. To improve 

governance interpretability, we perform risk decomposition across the subdomains defined by 

MedSafety and MedEthics, which localizes model behavior to actionable categories and facilitates 

targeted remediation. We summarize SafeMed-R1 ’s fine-grained profiles using radar charts (Figure 3), 

enabling an at-a-glance audit of whether improvements are broad-based or concentrated in a small 

subset of domains. 

Across both datasets, SafeMed-R1 consistently occupies the outer periphery of the radar plots, 

achieving the highest scores in nearly all subdomains. This pattern indicates that its safety and ethics 

performance is broadly balanced rather than driven by isolated strengths in a few areas. In contrast, 

both general LLMs and medical LLMs exhibit pronounced “spikes and dips ”: their performance 

approaches that of SafeMed-R1 in more textbook-oriented domains such as medical regulations and 

advanced health management ethics, yet lags markedly in domains closely linked to real-world medical 

events and value-sensitive care. Specifically, the operational safety subdomains include medication 

safety, infection control, critical laboratory indicator escalation, adverse event reporting, and medical 

information security, whereas the normative ethics subdomains cover end-of-life care, mental health 

ethics, ethical norms during major epidemics and public health emergencies, and ethics in traditional 

Chinese medicine (TCM), which is shaped by local practice norms. Finally, the subdomain view 

provides a natural bridge to adversarial evaluation: categories exhibiting larger gaps or instability can 

be prioritized for targeted red-teaming and policy refinement, while categories with uniformly strong 

performance serve as evidence of reliable safety/ethics check execution under the benchmark ’ s

governance criteria. 

2.4 Clinician-audited supervision provenance (CTS) and rubric distributions We constructed a clinician-audited corpus with CTS provenance records that link each QA –reasoning 

item to rubric scores, edits, and adjudication outcomes using a multi-stage CTS curation pipeline. 

Starting from 362,130 candidate QA –chain-of-thought (CoT) pairs, multi-stage clinician review 

retained 311,088 items after rubric scoring, adversarial re-answering with escalation, and expert 

adjudication by 160 physicians spanning 14 specialties over 2 months. Among retained items, 81% 

required no edits, 16% were revised, and 3% were removed due to irreparable issues. All retained items 

were approved by at least two licensed clinicians, yielding a large, auditable supervision corpus to 

support both training and downstream error analysis .

Within the set with complete rubric records, Medical Accuracy showed a small error tail (Score 0: 

0.44%), whereas Terminology Expression and Clinical Value exhibited near-ceiling performance 

(Score 2: 92.1% and 96.4%), indicating stable terminology and practical utility. In contrast, Reasoning 

Structure and Information Completeness clustered at Score 1 (94.2% and 94.6%; Score 0: 5.8% and 

5.4%), suggesting that large-scale clinical auditing did not primarily reveal deficits in usability, but 

rather localized the key, governable bottlenecks to structural coherence of the reasoning chain and 

systematic completion of essential information — thereby converting improvement from heuristic 

judgment into traceable, quantitative targets (Figure 4). 

2.5 Human-AI comparison on medication-safety vignettes 

We conducted a paired expert rating study on 30 medication-safety vignettes to compare SafeMed-R1 

with junior clinicians (PGY1-PGY2). An expert panel (two senior clinicians and one clinical 

pharmacist) rated anonymized, randomized responses on four dimensions: medical correctness, 

medication-safety adequacy, guideline consistency, and clinical usefulness (Figure 5). 

Correctness ratings were high for both groups (median 5/5), and we observed no statistically significant 

difference in correctness under this vignette set (mean 4.73 ±0.83 vs. 4.53 ±0.68; Wilcoxon signed-rank 

p=0.23). In contrast, SafeMed-R1 received higher ratings on the three deployment-relevant dimensions: 

medication-safety adequacy, guideline consistency, and clinical usefulness (median 5.0 vs. 4.0), 

outperforming residents in more than 70% of vignettes with large effect sizes (r ≈0.73; all p<0.001). 

This paired clinician rating provides human-audited governance evidence that SafeMed-R1 more 

consistently meets medication-safety adequacy and guideline-concordance expectations without 

sacrificing correctness. 

2.6 Case study 

Finally, we present two qualitative case studies illustrating SafeMed-R1 ’s behavior in value-sensitive, 

high-risk scenarios (see Figure 6). These cases demonstrate the model ’s ability to refuse harmful 

instructions, offer evidence-based alternatives, communicate risk, and maintain ethical consistency. 

In the first case, a patient requested an “extreme fasting and alternative therapy ” regimen to treat 

metabolic disorders. SafeMed-R1 identified the substantial risks associated with extreme dietary restrictions and unvalidated therapies, explicitly stating that such approaches could cause nutritional 

imbalances, exacerbate chronic diseases, and conflict with World Health Organization public health 

principles. Rather than accommodating the request, the model declined to provide dangerous advice 

and instead proposed safer, evidence-based options, including standardized lifestyle interventions, 

balanced dietary strategies, and consultation with healthcare professionals. Expert reviewers concluded 

that the refusal was justified, explanations were clear, risk warnings were appropriate, and the response 

aligned with clinical ethics and guideline principles. 

In the second case, a patient with mild, medication-controlled cardiac symptoms inquired about the 

necessity of invasive cardiac surgery. SafeMed-R1 clearly stated that such mild cases do not meet 

surgical indications and emphasized that invasive treatments must adhere to the principle that expected 

benefits outweigh procedural risks. The model further described key surgical risks (e.g., 

anaesthesia-related complications, bleeding, infection) and suggested alternatives such as optimization 

of medical therapy, symptom monitoring, and appropriate ancillary testing. Clinical experts judged that 

the response accurately reflected real-world decision-making processes and demonstrated strong risk 

communication and ethical consistency. Together, these case studies suggest that SafeMed-R1 can 

maintain safe, robust, and explainable behaviour when faced with complex, high-stakes patient 

requests. 

3. Discussion 

We present a governance-oriented evidence package for deployment-oriented medical LLMs and 

instantiate it in SafeMed-R1. SafeMed-R1 achieves the highest macro-average exam-style accuracy 

(79.6%) among 11 evaluated models, while also leading governance-oriented evaluations with ≈81.3 

on MedSafety and ≈80.7 on MedEthics. Under adversarial stress testing on MedSafetyBench (MSB), 

SafeMed-R1 exhibits the lowest aggregated risk and reduces unsafe behaviour by approximately 3–

5% relative to its baseline. Together, these results support an evidence stack that couples capability 

with auditable safety/ethics criteria and quantified worst-case risk under misuse. 

A recurring limitation in the medical LLM literature is an emphasis on exam-style accuracy and static 

benchmarks, which is informative but insufficient for institutional governance and deployment 

decision-making. Meanwhile, general safety reinforcement learning (RL) approaches may not encode 

clinical risk taxonomies or produce clinician-audited evidence that can be inspected, challenged, and 

acted upon by health systems 19 . A distinctive feature of SafeMed-R1 is therefore its auditable evidence 

chain: each question –answer item with CoT reasoning is linked to structured expert rubric scores and 

traceable edit decisions, enabling transparent inspection of reasoning quality and failure modes. 

Robustness was further strengthened using a five-retry screening protocol (re-answering with 

DeepSeek-R1 across multiple decoding seeds), treating inconsistency as an explicit quality-control 

trigger rather than an anecdotal limitation; items failing all five attempts (6.4%) were escalated for 

expert adjudication. In downstream behaviour, SafeMed-R1 more consistently surfaced risk-mitigation strategies (e.g., monitoring plans and contraindication checks) and provided mechanistic rationales 

aligned with its higher clinical-usefulness ratings. By operationalising ethical principles as measurable 

behaviours and reusable evaluation outcomes, SafeMed-R1 aligns with structured ethical assessment 

frameworks such as the TREGAI checklist 17 and with health-system perspectives that emphasise trust 

as shared, transparent commitments spanning safety, privacy, and fairness 10,20 .

Recent work has cautioned against “unrestricted and unmonitored ” AI experimentation in healthcare 

and has argued for institutional oversight mechanisms spanning pre-review, transparency, and 

continuous monitoring. SafeMed-R1 is designed for this governance reality by providing a minimum 

evidence package across (i) competence (benchmark performance), (ii) ethics/safety alignment 

(MedSafety/MedEthics), and (iii) worst-case robustness (MSB plus red-teaming, i.e., adversarial 

stress-testing). Practically, health-system partners could reuse the benchmarks and CTS-style rubrics to 

audit third-party models, compare vendors under a consistent protocol, and monitor drift as workflows 

and prompts evolve —an approach intended to be compatible with risk-based regulatory landscapes and 

trust-centred health-system perspectives 21,22 .This study is primarily text-centric and does not yet 

evaluate multimodal clinical inputs (e.g., imaging or physiological waveforms) or cross-modal safety 

failure modes. Although we include clinician comparisons, they rely on reconstructed, de-identified 

vignettes rather than prospective, in-workflow deployment; accordingly, we do not yet quantify 

real-world impacts such as time saved, error interception rates, over-reliance dynamics, or downstream 

patient outcomes. In addition, while MSB is scored using a multi-judge ensemble to reduce 

single-scorer bias, broader human adjudication and external validation across institutions will be 

important for strengthening the safety case. Future work should prioritise multilingual and multimodal 

extensions, workflow-integrated evaluation with online monitoring, and longitudinal incident tracking 

to characterise residual risks and mitigation effectiveness under real-world clinical constraints. Our 

current traceability is supervision-level provenance rather than inference-time evidence attribution. 

Incorporating document-grounded reasoning (e.g., guideline-conditioned QA with citation verification) 

is a natural next step and complementary to our governance-oriented supervision design. 

4. Methods 

This study develops SafeMed-R1, a trust-centred medical AI system built via a human –AI 

co-construction pipeline to support medical validity, transparent reasoning, and ethics –safety alignment. 

The workflow comprises three stages: (i) data construction and quality control, (ii) instruction 

fine-tuning (SFT) to integrate clinical knowledge and behavioural guardrails, and (iii) risk-sensitive 

alignment via reinforcement optimisation. 

4.1 Data Construction and Quality Control 

4.1.1 Clinical Reasoning Dataset The clinical reasoning dataset provides the primary supervised training corpus and comprises 320,710 

multiple-choice QA items. Each item contains a question (Chinese), 4-5 answer options, the correct 

answer, and a chain-of-thought (CoT) explanation. In aggregate, the dataset is structured to support 

both answer correctness and traceable clinical reasoning. 

To ensure broad clinical coverage, we curated questions spanning core medical fields and procedural 

specialties. Internal medicine contributes the largest share (including cardiology, pulmonology, 

gastroenterology, and related areas), while surgery accounts for 22.2%, endocrinology 8.2%, 

rheumatology 8.2%, oncology 8.0%, with additional representation across dozens of subtopics (Figure 

7). This specialty distribution is intended to reduce narrow-domain overfitting and improve 

generalisability across clinical departments. 

We also included heterogeneous item formats to capture both factual recall and applied reasoning. 

Some questions target straightforward knowledge retrieval (e.g., diagnostic criteria and drug 

mechanisms), whereas others are vignette-style items requiring application to a patient scenario. 

During expert validation, we categorised questions by cognitive level (recall, interpretation, 

problem-solving) and by difficulty (easy, moderate, hard) as judged by clinicians. Together, these 

design choices yield a clinically broad and difficulty-stratified reasoning dataset suitable for supervised 

learning and audit-oriented error analysis (rather than inference-time evidence attribution). 

4.1.2 Ethics and Safety Datasets 

To enable safety-aligned training and ethical risk control, we constructed an Ethics and Safety Dataset 

Suite that integrates multi-dimensional risk factors, regulatory principles, and scenario-based 

assessments. The suite is organised into ethical and clinical safety domains, reflecting core governance 

requirements in medical practice. It encompasses 11 ethical dimensions and 9 safety dimensions within 

medical ethics and risk management, including informed consent, bias, data privacy, patient autonomy, 

and misleading representations, and is grounded in World Health Organization guidelines and national 

healthcare policy documents (see Table S2 ). This structure provides explicit supervision targets for 

both normative and operational safety behaviours. 

An expert panel annotated each item using a structured scoring system that assesses legal risks, 

potential patient harm, and institutional compliance gaps based on the severity and type of ethical 

violations or safety lapses. The resulting corpus specifies expected behaviors for SafeMed-R1, 

including responding accurately within the appropriate professional scope, recognizing and mitigating 

high-risk situations, providing guideline-consistent advice, rejecting unsafe requests when necessary, 

and presenting reasoning in a traceable manner suitable for regulated medical use. 

4.1.3 Red-Teaming Dataset To improve robustness against unsafe user behaviours, we constructed a red-teaming dataset of 

approximately 10,000 adversarial, open-ended Chinese medical queries. These prompts were designed 

to probe safety-critical failure modes and reflect realistic misuse patterns observed in open 

clinical-assistant settings. They target unauthorised diagnostic or prescribing requests, 

guideline-violating treatments, misleading harmful instructions, and role-induction attacks. Unlike 

normative benchmarks, these items capture unstructured risks that arise during real user interactions. 

Integrated into the risk-sensitive alignment stage, they provide supervisory signals intended to reinforce 

conservative, guideline-consistent behaviour and strengthen intent recognition and refusal under attack 

(Supplementary Methods S3). 

4.1.4 Expert auditing and CTS provenance records 

CTS records supervision provenance (rubric scores, edits, adjudication) and does not provide 

retrieval-based grounding at inference time. To ensure clinical validity, reasoning quality, and 

educational usefulness, we implemented a multi-stage validation pipeline that combines structured 

clinician review, LLM-based adversarial re-answering, and panel adjudication. Over 2 months, 160 

physicians from 14 medical specialties reviewed tasks under a standard protocol (Supplementary 

Methods S4). All participants were independent practitioners or staff physicians; 10% served as chief 

medical officers, 43% were residents in their third year or beyond, and 47% were residents in their first 

or second year. All physicians received compensation. 

Each QA-CoT pair is accompanied by five-dimensional rubric scores and an edit history, forming 

traceable CTS that support supervised training, evaluation, and error analysis. In the first pass, 

physicians reviewed each QA pair for relevance, correctness, and sufficiency. In the second pass, 

licensed experts evaluated the associated CoT explanations using a formal annotation guide 

(Supplementary Methods S5-7 ), scoring five dimensions on a 0-2 scale; items judged incorrect or 

incomplete were revised or flagged for deeper inspection. 

To stress-test item quality, each question was re-answered by DeepSeek-R1 up to five times. Items that 

the model failed five times (6.4%) were escalated to an expert panel for detailed review; each escalated 

item was independently evaluated by at least two licensed clinicians following the quality standard, and 

items lacking expert consensus were removed or rewritten. 

4.2 Model Architecture and Training 

4.2.1 Experiment Setup 

We used Qwen3-32B as the base model and performed full-parameter supervised fine-tuning (SFT) 

using the curated clinical reasoning, safety, and ethics datasets. Training ran on 8×H200 GPUs for 3

epochs, and the resulting checkpoint was deployed with the vLLM inference engine for 

high-throughput evaluation. To enhance safety and adversarial robustness, we further applied a risk-sensitive reinforcement 

learning stage using the 10k red-teaming dataset. We optimised the model with Group Relative Policy 

Optimization (GRPO). A reward model scored sampled responses against medical-ethics criteria 

together with a structural format constraint. This alignment procedure is intended to encourage 

conservative, guideline-consistent behaviour while preserving clinical reasoning capability. 

4.2.2 Testing Setup 

During testing, we fixed decoding parameters to enforce determinism (temperature=0). We used two 

evaluation modes: Direct Evaluation and Robust Evaluation. Robust Evaluation requires consistent 

correctness under multiple option perturbations to reduce positional bias and accidental accuracy in 

multiple-choice tasks. For open-ended safety assessments (MedSafetyBench; MSB), we used a

multi-judge ensemble (gpt-4o, qwen3-235B-A22B, DeepSeek-V3) to reduce scorer-specific bias. 

4.3 Evaluation Datasets 

We evaluated SafeMed-R1 on a multi-layered suite of clinical knowledge, safety, ethics, and 

adversarial benchmarks (Table S1). Clinical competence was assessed using MedQA 23 ,

CMExam 8,Chinese-Exam, and PediaBench 24 , covering licensing-level knowledge, structured 

diagnostic reasoning, role-specific workflows, and paediatric scenarios. Safety-aligned behaviour was 

examined using MedSafety (nine-dimensional operational risk benchmark) and MedEthics (eleven 

domains of biomedical and professional ethics, including autonomy, privacy, and end-of-life 

decision-making). Robustness under real-world misuse patterns was measured using MSB 25 scored by 

the multi-judge ensemble. 

4.4 Baselines 

We compared SafeMed-R1 with a diverse set of LLMs spanning general-purpose and 

medical-specialised systems. General LLM baselines included Qwen3-32B 26 , Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct, 

Qwen3-30B-A3B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B 27 , gpt-5-mini, gemini-2.5-flash, and 

google-medgemma-27b, while medical LLM baselines included Baichuan-M2-32B 28 ,

HuatuoGPT-o1-70B 29 , and Lingshu-32B 30 . All models were evaluated under identical inference 

settings and the same perturbation-based robustness protocol for multiple-choice tasks, with 

open-ended safety tasks assessed using the multi-judge ensemble. Collectively, this baseline suite 

covers general reasoning, medical specialisation, and existing safety-aligned systems under a unified 

protocol. 

4.5 Human-AI comparative evaluation on medication-safety vignettes 

4.5.1 Case Construction We constructed 30 clinical vignettes by reconstructing typical EMR-style cases from cardiology, 

pulmonology, rheumatology, nephrology, geriatrics, and surgical specialties. Each vignette included 

demographics, chief complaint, history of present illness, key physical examination findings, essential 

laboratory and imaging summaries, current medications, and potential distractors. All vignettes 

contained at least one medication-safety pitfall (e.g., toxic drug levels, dosing errors, contraindicated 

combinations, renal-dose mismatch, or complex drug –drug interactions). No content was taken from 

hospital EMRs; all cases were fully de-identified and clinician-reconstructed. 

4.5.2 Scenario-based comparison between foundation models and junior clinicians 

We recruited 5 junior clinicians (PGY1 –PGY2) to answer the same vignette-based medication-safety 

and diagnostic-reasoning tasks as the models. Each vignette consisted of a reconstructed EMR-style 

case paired with a structured prompt, and responses from clinicians and models were anonymised and 

randomised prior to evaluation. This design reduces source-identification bias and enables paired 

comparisons on the same clinical inputs. 

An expert panel (two senior clinicians and one clinical pharmacist) independently rated each response 

on a 1-5 Likert scale across four dimensions: medical correctness, medication safety, guideline 

consistency, and clinical usefulness (Supplementary Methods S8 ). We compared SafeMed-R1 with 

junior clinicians using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired vignette-level ratings. Overall, the 

vignette evaluation provides expert-rated, paired evidence on whether model outputs meet clinical 

safety and guideline expectations relative to early-stage clinicians. 

5. Statistical Analysis 

We computed performance on multiple-choice tasks using accuracy under the option-perturbation 

consistency protocol. Open-ended safety tasks were scored by the multi-judge ensemble, and human 

Likert ratings were averaged across evaluators. We used paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests 

depending on distributional assumptions, defining statistical significance as p < 0.05. We obtained 

confidence intervals (CIs) via bootstrap resampling.

# Supplementary. Normative sources underpinning the Ethics and Safety Dataset Suite 

To ground the Ethics and Safety Dataset Suite in authoritative, auditable governance requirements, we 

anchored each ethical and clinical-safety dimension to a curated set of international guidelines and national policy/legal documents. The selected sources were chosen based on: (i) normative authority 

and broad adoption in health governance, (ii) explicit relevance to AI/digital health ethics, patient 

safety, and data governance, and (iii) direct translatability into supervision targets (i.e., observable 

behaviors and refusal/escalation patterns) for safety-aligned training.

# Supplementary Methods S3. Red-Teaming Dataset and AMA-aligned Safety Judge 

# Prompts 

# We constructed a red-teaming dataset (~10,000 items) of adversarial, open-ended Chinese 

# medical queries to probe safety-critical failure modes under realistic misuse patterns. Prompts 

# cover unauthorized diagnosis/prescribing requests, guideline-violating treatments, harmful or 

# misleading instructions, and role-induction (e.g., “act as a doctor rules ”) attacks. Unlike 

# normative benchmarks, these items emphasize unstructured, interaction-driven risks in real 

# clinical-assistant settings and were integrated into the risk-sensitive alignment stage to 

# reinforce conservative, guideline-consistent behavior, intent recognition, and refusal under 

# attack. Supplementary Methods S4. Clinician recruitment and reviewer composition 

Over a 2-month period, we recruited 160 physicians spanning 14 medical specialties to conduct 

structured review under a standardized protocol. All participants were independent practitioners or staff 

physicians; 10% served as chief medical officers, 43% were residents in their third year or beyond, and 

47% were residents in their first or second year. All participating physicians received compensation. Recruitment and eligibility criteria emphasized clinical training and the ability to evaluate medical 

correctness, relevance, and clinical acceptability. Reviewers were required to (a) have formal clinical 

medicine training (bachelor ’s degree or above), and (b) be capable of identifying incorrect options, 

logic errors, and insufficient or misleading content in clinical QA items. (See the original internal 

rubric documents for the operational criteria.) 

Supplementary Methods S5. First-pass QA-pair review rubric 

In the first pass, clinicians evaluated each QA pair (question + answer) with a binary rubric focused on 

clinical usability: relevance to the question, medical factual correctness, and sufficiency/acceptability 

of the answer in a clinical setting.

Supplementary Methods S7. Quality control, deliverables, and batch acceptance 

Annotations were delivered as structured spreadsheets with standardized headers. Quality assurance 

required that rubric scoring be free of errors and that all items flagged for revision/discard were 

appropriately handled. A batch-level acceptance threshold of ≥95% qualified items was applied, with 

batch rework triggered when audit sampling failed to meet the threshold. 

Supplementary Methods S8: Rating rubric 

Rating rubric for human –AI comparison For each vignette, the expert panel used a structured rating form with four dimensions scored on a 1–5

Likert scale (1 = poor, 5 = excellent): (1) medical correctness (accuracy and completeness of the 

diagnosis and causal reasoning), (2) medication-safety adequacy (identification and management of 

high-risk drugs, interactions, and monitoring needs), (3) guideline consistency (alignment with 

contemporary clinical practice guidelines or consensus statements), and (4) clinical usefulness (clarity, 

specificity, and practicality of the advice for frontline clinicians).
