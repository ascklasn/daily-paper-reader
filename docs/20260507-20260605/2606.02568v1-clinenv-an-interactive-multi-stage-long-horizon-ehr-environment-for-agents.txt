Abstract 

Clinical practice is not the selection of an an-swer from enumerated options: a physician gathers heterogeneous information incremen-tally and commits to sequential, irreversible decisions under uncertainty. Static benchmarks cannot probe and existing interactive medical benchmarks each compromise on at least one of them. We present C LIN ENV , an interac-tive benchmark that evaluates LLMs as attend-ing physicians over real inpatient admissions under a paradigm we term Longitudinal Inpa-tient Simulation. Each case is automatically constructed into an ordered sequence of de-cision stages; at every stage the model must actively query four specialized agents before committing to medications, procedures, and diagnoses. CLIN ENV scores both what the model decides, through deterministic ontology-grounded matching, and how it gathers infor-mation. Across seven models, the strongest reaches only 0.31 decision F1, and outcome quality is sharply decoupled from process qual-ity. Difficulty concentrates in management decisions and later stages, where models re-cover discharge diagnoses far more reliably than management actions (0.51 vs. 0.17 F1) and continue to issue redundant queries as cases progress. C LIN ENV makes this information-acquisition gap, invisible to outcome-only eval-uation, directly measurable. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models now approach or exceed expert performance on medical licensing examina-tions (Singhal et al., 2023; Nori et al., 2023) and structured clinical question answering (Jin et al., 2021). Yet real inpatient practice is not the selec-tion of an answer from enumerated options. An attending physician navigates a hospitalization in which information is incomplete and must be ac-tively sought, decisions are sequential and irre-versible, and each decision spans medications, pro-cedures, and diagnoses that are eventually veri-fied against the patient‚Äôs recorded course. Static multiple-choice benchmarks cannot probe any of these properties. Coding agents illustrate how evaluation infras-tructure drives progress, moving from static func-tional tests (Chen et al., 2021) to executable in-teractive environments (Jimenez et al., 2024) that double as training signals. Medical evaluation has begun a similar shift, but existing interactive bench-marks each make a structural compromise. Data-interaction benchmarks (Lee et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2025) treat the EHR as a queryable database and score atomic operations, measuring data-access skill rather than clinical reasoning. Diagnostic-conversation benchmarks (Schmidgall et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2026) rely on LLM-simulated patients, bounding ground truth to short exam vignettes and scoring via LLM-as-judge. Coding-style ex-ecutable benchmarks (Xu et al., 2025; Mao et al., 2025) reduce clinical reasoning to code or formulas the model must produce. None evaluates an LLM as an attending physician managing a real, multi-stage hospitalization with deterministic verification of its decisions. We introduce C LIN ENV , which evaluates LLMs as attending physicians navigating real admissions through three components (Figure 1). An auto-mated pipeline converts raw admissions into or-dered multi-stage cases with structured ground-truth decisions extracted from the EHR timeline and discharge documentation, requiring no manual annotation. An interactive multi-agent environment withholds clinical information until requested: at each stage the model must query four specialized agents (patient, nurse, laboratory, history) before committing to decisions. A dual evaluation frame-work scores both what the model decides, via de-terministic ontology-grounded matching (ATC for medications, hierarchical ICD F1 for diagnoses and procedures), and how it gathers information,

via process metrics for coverage and laboratory and medication cost efficiency. We refer to the paradigm defined by these properties, real EHR trajectories, multi-stage decisions, active informa-tion acquisition, and deterministic process-aware scoring, as Longitudinal Inpatient Simulation .Across seven LLMs, we find that even the strongest reaches only 0.31 decision F1 and recovers diag-noses far more reliably than management (0.51 vs. 0.17 F1), suggesting that outcome-only bench-marks may substantially overestimate clinical readi-ness and that information seeking is a distinct, mea-surable dimension of clinical competence. Our contributions are: (i) An automated case construction pipeline that converts raw admissions into multi-stage cases without manual annotation (Section 3.2); (ii) An interactive multi-agent clinical environ-ment requiring active information acquisition be-fore each decision (Section 3.3, 4); (iii) A dual evaluation framework covering decision accuracy and process quality (Section 5). 

2 Related Work 

We organize prior medical LLM benchmarks by the capability they evaluate (Table 1). 

Knowledge benchmarks. PubMedQA (Jin et al., 2019), MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022), MedQA (Jin et al., 2021), the medical subsets of MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), and the aggre-gated MultiMedQA (Singhal et al., 2023) probe factual recall through specified multiple-choice questions. This family equates clinical competence with knowledge retrieval, with no information acquisition or commitment under uncertainty. 

Data-interaction benchmarks. EHRSQL (Lee et al., 2022), MIMIC-SQL (Wang et al., 2020), and FHIR-AgentBench (Lee et al., 2025) treat the EHR as a queryable artifact accessed via SQL or APIs; MedAgentBench (Jiang et al., 2025) extends this with physician-authored interactive tasks on 100 patient profiles. These benchmarks measure proficiency at executing well-specified operations against patient data. Their tasks are atomic and externally defined, so they assess data-access skill rather than the clinical reasoning that determines which data matters or what action it warrants.

CLIN ENV shares this spirit but targets clinical deci-sions themselves (which drug to start, which diag-nosis to commit to), verified against medical ontolo-gies physicians use, rather than code or formulas the model must produce.

3 CLIN ENV Construction 

3.1 Data Preprocessing 

CLIN ENV is built from MIMIC-IV v3.1 (Johnson et al., 2023b) and MIMIC-IV-Note v2.2 (Johnson et al., 2023a). For each hospitalization, we com-bine structured EHR tables with clinical notes into an admission-level timeline by subject_id and 

hadm_id . Structured sources include admissions, laboratory and microbiology results, medication or-ders and administrations, ICU charted events, fluid input/output events, procedure events, and ICD-coded diagnoses and procedures; note sources in-clude discharge notes and radiology reports. Each retained row becomes a provenance-bearing event with a canonical event_time , source_table , and the original table-specific clinical fields. Events are sorted into one timeline per admission. Notes supply narrative context for decision extraction and history access, while structured records provide timestamped observations and coded ground truth for evaluation. Appendix B describes the prepro-cessing procedure and examples. 

3.2 Automated Case Construction 

Each admission is processed by a four-phase pipeline that segments the patient timeline into an ordered sequence of clinical stages, with ground-truth decision sets derived entirely from structured records and discharge documentation. Claude-Sonnet-4.6 is used as the planning backbone throughout. 

Phase A: Decision Extraction. The planner reads the discharge note to extract every attending-level physician decision made during the hospi-talization. When a full free-text discharge note is available it is used directly; otherwise, four sections are stitched together as a fallback: Chief Complaint, History of Present Illness, Brief Hospital Course, and Discharge Diagnosis. Each decision is anno-tated with a type (medication, procedure, or plan).

3.3 Multi-Source Clinical Environment 

The evaluation environment exposes clinical infor-mation through four specialized agents (Table 2), each powered by GPT-5.4-mini and restricted to a designated readview Va, which is a filtered projec-tion of the EHR events relevant to that role. The patient agent answers in lay language from demographics and clinical history; the nurse agent reports bedside vital signs, fluid balance, and medication administration; the lab agent resolves natural-language test queries against the stage‚Äôs available results via semantic matching, returning nothing for tests not on record; and the history agent supplies prior discharge summaries when ear-lier admissions exist. Table 2 lists each readview, and Appendix C.4 gives the full agent specifica-tions.

6.4 Diagnosis Recovered; Management Not 

The stage analysis points to decision type as the underlying axis of difficulty, which Table 6 makes explicit. On diagnosis-only stages, models recover the discharge diagnoses reasonably well (0.40 pooled, 0.51 for GPT-5.4), but on management stages F1 collapses to 0.13. The gap is roughly threefold and holds for every model: recognizing what the patient has is far easier than committing to what to do about it. This also explains why the ap-pended diagnosis stage inflates whole-case scores and why, once it is removed, the management tra-jectory in Section 6.2 falls so steeply. 7 Conclusion 

We presented C LIN ENV , which evaluates LLMs as attending physicians over real, multi-stage admis-sions. Under its Longitudinal Inpatient Simulation paradigm, cases are built automatically from EHR timelines, clinical information is withheld until the model actively queries one of four agents, and ev-ery decision is scored deterministically alongside process metrics for coverage and cost. Across the evaluated models, the strongest attains only mod-est decision F1, and outcome quality is decoupled from how efficiently models gather information. Difficulty concentrates in management decisions and later stages, where accuracy collapses even as coverage holds, indicating that clinical reasoning, not information access, is the binding constraint. CLIN ENV makes this gap, invisible to outcome-only evaluation, directly measurable. 8 Limitations 

Because C LIN ENV grounds every decision in the management actually recorded for each admission, it measures concordance with real clinical practice rather than against a single optimal answer; a clini-cally reasonable alternative may therefore score as a miss. This property is intrinsic to any benchmark derived from real EHR trajectories and bounds how scores should be read, but does not affect the rela-tive comparison of models on the same fixed cases. Cases come from one US academic medical cen-ter and use English notes with US coding and pric-ing systems (ICD, ATC, the CMS Clinical Labora-tory Fee Schedule, and NADAC); generalization to other settings, languages, and coding standards is left to future work. Finally, construction and some scoring steps use LLMs, but all structured ground truth (drug names, ICD codes) is derived determin-istically from EHRs rather than from model output, which keeps the evaluation targets independent of the models under test. 

9 Ethical Considerations 

CLIN ENV is derived from MIMIC-IV and MIMIC-IV-Note, which are de-identified and distributed through PhysioNet under a credentialed data use agreement; we comply with those terms, expose no protected health information, and require users to hold the appropriate PhysioNet credentials. C LI -

> N

ENV is a research benchmark for measuring LLM behavior, not a clinical decision-support tool: the models evaluated here fall far short of safe clinical use, and strong benchmark scores do not imply safe or equitable behavior in practice, particularly given the single-center source. We intend the benchmark to expose capability gaps and thereby discourage premature claims of clinical readiness.

C.3 Planner Safeguards 

The planner includes several implementation safe-guards that are easy to miss from the main descrip-tion: ‚Ä¢ Sequential anchoring: Phase B uses a forward search over 20-event windows. The next deci-sion begins after the previous accepted anchor, preserving the temporal order extracted from the discharge note. ‚Ä¢ Leakage expansion: after a single anchor is found, a local scan examines up to 10 events be-fore and after the anchor. Neighboring records for the same drug, procedure, or action are ab-sorbed into the anchor range so that these records are not included as pre-decision evidence. ‚Ä¢ Continuation filtering: medication decisions are skipped when the same drug already appears as active medication in the proposed context win-dow, preventing routine continuation orders from becoming artificial decisions. ‚Ä¢ Small-context merging: stages with fewer than 10 context events are merged into an adjacent stage when possible, reducing nearly empty deci-sion points that would be hard to evaluate mean-ingfully. ‚Ä¢ Groundable diagnoses: diagnosis ground truth is filtered through a separate sliding-window scan over the event timeline using 100-event windows and 50-event stride; only ICD codes judged ev-idenced or managed in at least one window are retained. After stages are constructed, Phase D converts raw decisions to structured gts . Medication fields such as drug name, dose, route, frequency, product strength, and order status are copied from prescrip-tion, pharmacy, or eMAR records. Procedure and diagnosis fields are copied from ICD-coded rows, including code, version, and long-title description. LLM calls classify decision type and medication action, but the final evaluable fields are copied from structured source records. 

C.4 Information Agent Specifications 

Each information agent is powered by GPT-5.4-mini and restricted to a designated readview Va, a filtered projection of the EHR events relevant to that role. 

Patient Agent. The patient agent simulates sub-jective reporting from the patient‚Äôs perspective. Given a query, it draws from demographics, chief complaint (CC), history of present illness (HPI), and past medical history (PMH) to produce lay-language responses that reflect how a real patient might describe their symptoms and background, without access to objective clinical measurements. Nurse Agent. The nurse agent reports bedside observations from the nursing record. It has access to vital signs, fluid balance, and medication admin-istration records (MAR), and responds to queries about the patient‚Äôs current physiological status and treatment as observed at the bedside. 

Lab Agent. The lab agent does not expose re-sults passively. Given a natural language query 

q and the set of available test labels Lk in stage 

Sk, it identifies the matching subset L‚Ä≤ ‚äÜ L k via LLM-based semantic matching that handles abbre-viations, panel expansions (e.g., CBC, BMP), and synonyms, then returns value, unit, and reference range for each matched item. Tests not in Lk return a null response. 

History Agent. The history agent supplies longi-tudinal context from prior discharge summaries. It is activated only when the patient has at least one prior admission on record, preventing uninforma-tive interactions when no historical data exists. 

Agent Activation. Agent a is activated in stage 

Sk only if its readview Va intersects the current context window, preventing uninformative interac-tions with agents that have no relevant data for the stage. The history agent is additionally gated on whether the patient has any prior admissions. 

C.5 Readview Construction and Tool Exposure 

Readviews are cumulative within an admission: at stage k, each role can see records from event index 0 through the stage end, filtered to that role‚Äôs al-lowed source tables. The patient readview contains static demographics, chief complaint, history of present illness, and past medical history. The nurse readview contains bedside observations, charted events, fluid input and output, administered medica-tions, pharmacy and prescription records, radiology summaries, and procedure events. The lab read-view contains laboratory and microbiology events, excluding degenerate test labels shorter than two characters. Tool exposure is stage-specific. The patient and history roles are available by default when their data exist, while nurse and lab tools are acti-vated only when the current stage window contains matching events. During interactive evaluation, only submit tools corresponding to ground-truth types in the current stage are exposed. Duplicate submissions with the same type, value, and medi-cation action are ignored by the runtime. 

C.6 Runtime State Across Stages 

The interactive runtime allows at most 60 turns per stage and permits one tool call per turn. To keep the longitudinal state clinically coherent, each new stage receives filtered prior-stage context: information-seeking dialogue is retained, while prior submit and finalize calls are removed. The environment then injects the previous stage‚Äôs con-firmed ground-truth decisions into the context, so later stages condition on what actually happened in the hospitalization rather than on earlier model errors. In direct mode, the same information is shown as prior realized events. 

C.7 Evaluation Implementation Details 

Outcome evaluation uses type-wise Hungarian matching. Diagnosis and procedure submissions are mapped to ICD codes by embedding re-trieval over ICD-9-CM, ICD-10-CM, ICD-9-PCS, and ICD-10-PCS vocabularies followed by LLM reranking, then scored with hierarchical F1 over code ancestors. Medication submissions are action-gated before drug similarity is computed: mis-matched actions score zero, while action-consistent pairs receive ATC-hierarchy partial credit. For 

adjust decisions, opposing explicit directions halve the drug score. Process evaluation is computed from the saved dialogue trace. Information coverage is judged separately for patient, nurse, and lab responses by comparing retrieved dialogue content against each role‚Äôs complete readview, then macro-averaged across roles. The implemented efficiency metric is the coverage value multiplied by N/ (N + K),where N is the number of judged key facts and K

is the number of information-gathering tool calls. Laboratory cost scoring parses lab tool outputs, counts unresolved or unavailable lab requests as wasted queries, and estimates test cost by matching ordered tests to the CMS Clinical Laboratory Fee Schedule. Medication-cost scoring resolves sub-mitted drugs through RxNorm, maps to NDC unit prices from NADAC, obtains defined daily doses from the WHO ATC/DDD index when possible, and reports estimated daily drug acquisition cost for submitted medication decisions. D CLIN ENV Statistics 

This appendix details the sampling protocol and construction-quality checks for C LIN ENV ; its com-position is summarized in Table 4 (Section 4). 

D.1 Sampling Protocol 

CLIN ENV contains 3,509 cases, one per admission, drawn from 1,809 patients. We discard admissions that yield no valid decision stage after construction, then sample the remainder to span the full range of case horizons, from single-stage admissions to trajectories of up to nine stages. Admissions with no usable discharge note, or with no groundable decisions after Phase C enrichment, are excluded. 

D.2 Construction Quality 

CLIN ENV is constructed without manual annota-tion, so the integrity of each stage rests on three properties that are guaranteed by construction and remain verifiable against the source record: ‚Ä¢ Decision precision: every extracted decision cor-responds to an attending-level action actually recorded during the admission (a prescription, procedure, or coded discharge diagnosis), rather than a hallucinated or non-decision item. ‚Ä¢ Anchor validity: each decision carries the time-line event index it was extracted from, placing it at the point in the admission where the action was taken. ‚Ä¢ Enrichment accuracy: the structured ground-truth fields (drug name, action type, dose, ICD code) are copied directly from the corresponding table row, not generated. Because every stage is built deterministically from logged events, the ground truth can always be traced back to the originating row. Appendix E presents a complete worked example for a single admission, showing the information environment the model observes and the held-out ground truth at each stage. 

D.3 Clinical entities follow common inpatient patterns. 

Figure 4 shows the most frequent diagnosis, med-ication, and procedure entities in the benchmark. Chronic inpatient comorbidities such as essential hypertension, hyperlipidemia, GERD, and diabetes dominate the diagnosis distribution, while medica-tion and procedure decisions are more dispersed. 

D.4 Longer timelines are less decision-dense. 

Figure 5 plots each case‚Äôs timeline event span against its decision density, colored by case horizon. Decision density declines as timelines lengthen (Pearson r = ‚àí0.365 , p < 0.001 ), showing that longer admissions add more context per decision rather than simply more decisions.

Decision type remains the dominant accuracy gap. Averaged across models, diagnosis match-ing is much stronger than management matching: mean diagnosis score is 0.412, mean procedure score is 0.284, and mean medication score is only 0.086. Even the best medication score is 0.101 (Llama-3.1-70B), far below the weakest diagnosis score (0.362). Medication action selection alone does not explain the gap: Llama-3.1-8B and Llama-3.1-70B have the highest medication-action accura-cies among submitted medication decisions (0.733 and 0.704), but still obtain low medication match scores. The bottleneck is therefore not only choos-ing whether to start, stop, switch, or adjust treat-ment; it is selecting the correct drug identity. 

Process metrics separate useful interaction from volume. Across the seven model-level points in Table 5, information coverage is strongly correlated with decision F1 (Pearson r = 0 .865 ), moderately anticorrelated with aggregate laboratory waste ratio (r = ‚àí0.496 ), and almost uncorrelated with mean laboratory spending ( r = 0 .103 ). Turn count is negatively correlated with coverage ( r = ‚àí0.404 )and positively correlated with waste ( r = 0 .644 ). Thus, longer dialogues do not automatically re-trieve more useful information; the relevant distinc-tion is whether the model asks clinically targeted questions and orders tests that the ground-truth clin-ical picture supports.
