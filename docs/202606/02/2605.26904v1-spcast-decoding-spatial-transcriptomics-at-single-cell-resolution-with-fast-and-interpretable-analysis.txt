## Abstract 

Single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics profiles gene expression at cellular locations in native tissues, yet accurate cell-type annotation remains challenging: imaging-based platforms are constrained by targeted gene panels, whereas sequencing-based platforms often suffer from sparse molecular capture and dropout. Reliable transfer of cell-type labels from single-cell RNA sequencing references is therefore critical for interpreting targeted and sparse spatial datasets. Here, we present SpCAST, a Kolmogorov–Arnold network-based framework for reference-guided spatial transcriptomics analysis. SpCAST captures nonlinear mappings between reference and spatial expression profiles and uses feature attribution to prioritize genes supporting cell-type predictions. Within a unified framework, SpCAST performs cell-type label transfer, spatial gene-expression reconstruction and marker-gene candidate prioritization. We benchmarked SpCAST on 53 datasets comprising 413,376 spatial cells across five technologies and diverse tissue contexts. SpCAST achieved competitive annotation performance with reduced runtime relative to representative existing methods. Case studies demonstrated that SpCAST supports cross-species label transfer and candidate assignment of originally unlabeled cells. It also reconstructs marker-gene expression patterns with improved spatial concordance and prioritizes cell-type-associated marker genes. Together, these results support SpCAST as an efficient and interpretable framework for extracting cell-type and gene-level information from targeted and sparse single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics data.

# Introduction 

Single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics (scST) enables the measurement of gene expression together with spatial location in native tissue contexts, providing important opportunities to resolve cellular heterogeneity, tissue architecture and spatially organized biological processes at cellular resolution[ 45 , 52 , 59 , 60 ]. Current scST platforms can be broadly grouped into imaging-based and sequencing-based technologies. Imaging-based platforms, including MERFISH[ 12 ], seqFISH[ 36 ], seqFISH+[ 18 ], CosMx SMI[ 23 ] and Xenium[ 28 ], can measure spatially resolved expression at single-cell scale but are typically constrained by predefined targeted gene panels, typically covering only hundreds to several thousand genes. Sequencing-based platforms, including Slide-tags[ 54 ] and Stereo-seq[ 10 ], expand the scope of spatial transcriptomic profiling but often suffer from sparse molecular capture and dropout. Thus, across platforms, a gap remains between spatially resolved expression measurements and reliable cell-type identity. At the same time, spatial transcriptomics experiments are rapidly increasing in the number of profiled cells, tissue area and gene-panel size per experiment, making robust and efficient transfer of cell-type labels from single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) references a central task in scST analysis[13]. A variety of computational methods have been developed to integrate scRNA-seq and spatial transcriptomics data. These approaches broadly include model-based mapping or deconvolution methods and deep learning-based representation learning methods. Model-based approaches typically rely on Bayesian inference, regression, cell-to-location mapping, alignment or related optimization strategies to estimate cell-type abundance, infer spatial localization or map single cells to spatial measurements [ 6, 30 ]. Although these methods have been widely adopted, their reliance on iterative optimization or complex probabilistic inference can become computationally demanding for large-scale scST datasets, particularly when applied to massive numbers of spatial cells or spots. Deep learning-based methods further improve reference-query integration by learning nonlinear representations between scRNA-seq and spatial expression profiles using transfer learning, deep generative models or neural-network-based annotation frameworks [ 22 , 57 , 65 , 74 ]. However, many of these models still lack intrinsic interpretability, making it difficult to identify the genes and nonlinear transcriptomic relationships that drive cell-type predictions. Consequently, when unexpected or uncertain annotations arise, it can be difficult to determine whether they reflect reference-query mismatch, insufficient marker coverage, platform-specific bias or limitations of the model itself. This limitation is particularly important in spatial transcriptomics because cell-type annotation is often used not only to assign cellular identities but also as the basis for marker-gene analysis, spatial expression reconstruction and tissue-structure interpretation. Moreover, most existing methods are designed around a primary task, such as cell-type annotation, cell-type abundance estimation, single-cell mapping or expression imputation, whereas few provide a unified, efficient and interpretable framework that jointly supports label transfer, marker-gene prioritization and spatial expression reconstruction. To address these challenges, we developed SpCAST, a fast framework for reference-guided scST analysis based on Kolmogorov–Arnold networks (KANs), which represent nonlinear relationships through learnable univariate functions on network edges rather than relying only on fixed activation functions at nodes [ 35 ]. This architecture is well suited for modeling nonlinear correspondences between scRNA-seq references and spatial transcriptomics queries. To connect model predictions with gene-level evidence, SpCAST combines KAN-based representation learning with SHAP-based attribution [ 37 ], enabling predicted cell identities to be examined together with marker-gene candidate evidence and cell-type-associated transcriptional programs. Within a unified framework, SpCAST integrates cell-type label transfer, reconstruction-aware spatial expression modeling and marker-gene candidate prioritization. By jointly learning cell-type-discriminative, cross-modal-aligned and reconstruction-aware representations, SpCAST links predicted cell identities to marker-gene evidence and spatial expression patterns. We systematically evaluated SpCAST on 53 publicly available scST datasets comprising 413,376 spatial cells or spots across five technologies, including seqFISH+, MERFISH, CosMx SMI, Slide-tags and Xenium, as well as multiple species and tissue contexts [18 , 23 , 28 , 43 , 54 ]. In this multi-dataset benchmark, SpCAST achieved competitive or improved annotation performance in our evaluation setting while reducing runtime relative to representative baseline methods, supporting its application to large-scale scST analysis. To examine whether these predictions translated into biologically meaningful outputs, we further applied SpCAST to case studies spanning cortical laminar organization, ortholog-constrained cross-species transfer, QC-guided candidate assignment of originally unlabeled Xenium cells, marker-gene candidate prioritization and reconstruction-supported spatial marker analysis. These analyses showed that SpCAST not only enables efficient reference-guided cell-type label transfer, but also links predictions to gene-level attribution and reconstructed spatial expression patterns, providing an integrated framework for interpreting targeted and sparse scST data. 

# Materials and methods 

## Data description 

We used 53 single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics datasets comprising 413,376 spatial cells or spots to validate and benchmark SpCAST. These datasets were generated by five spatial transcriptomics technologies, including seqFISH+[ 18 ], MERFISH[ 12 ], CosMx SMI[ 23 ], Slide-tags[ 53 ] and Xenium[ 28 ], and covered both imaging-based and sequencing-based platforms. The datasets represented diverse biological systems, including mouse brain tissues and human cancer tissues such as non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). For model evaluation and benchmarking, cell-type annotations provided by the original studies were used as benchmark reference labels. Corresponding scRNA-seq reference datasets were curated for cross-modal cell-type transfer, including datasets generated by droplet-based scRNA-seq platforms such as 10x Chromium and Drop-seq [40, 80]. 

## Data preprocessing 

All datasets were preprocessed using Scanpy[ 66 ] to ensure consistent handling of count matrices and preparation for Journal Title Here, YEAR, Volume XX, Issue x 3

spatial and single-cell integration. The preprocessing workflow included quality control, normalization, feature selection and cross-modality gene alignment. 

Normalization. For each dataset, raw count matrices were normalized to a total count of 10,000 per cell or spatial unit, followed by log1p transformation to stabilize variance across genes. 

scRNA-seq preprocessing. For scRNA-seq reference datasets, quality control was first performed to remove low-quality genes and cells. Genes expressed in fewer than three cells were excluded. Cells with fewer than 300 total counts or fewer than 600 detected genes were removed. Highly variable genes (HVGs) were then selected from the filtered expression matrix for downstream integration and model training. 

Spatial transcriptomics preprocessing. Spatial transcriptomics datasets were processed using gene-level filtering and normalization procedures. Genes expressed in fewer than three spatial cells or spots were removed, followed by HVG selection. Because sequencing-based spatial transcriptomics technologies often produce highly sparse expression matrices, we applied a spatial data enhancement preprocessing step to sequencing-based datasets, including Slide-tags, to reduce sparsity before cross-modal integration, as described below. 

Dataset integration. For each spatial dataset and its corresponding scRNA-seq reference, shared genes across the two modalities were identified and used for alignment. After intersecting the gene sets, HVGs were selected from the shared genes, and the top 2,000 HVGs were retained as the final feature set for downstream model training. 

## Spatial data enhancement 

Sequencing-based spatial transcriptomics datasets often exhibit high sparsity in their gene expression matrices. To reduce sparsity while preserving local spatial structure, we applied a spatial data enhancement procedure that integrated spatial proximity with gene expression similarity. 

K-nearest neighbor graph construction. For each spatial cell or spot, its k-nearest neighbors were identified based on two-dimensional spatial coordinates, resulting in a spatial proximity matrix A.

Expression similarity estimation. Gene expression profiles were first reduced using principal component analysis (PCA)[ 39 ]. Pairwise Pearson correlation coefficients were then computed between spatial cells or spots based on the reduced expression profiles to estimate transcriptomic similarity[ 3], resulting in an expression similarity matrix R.

Combined coefficient matrix. The spatial proximity matrix and expression similarity matrix were multiplied element-wise to construct a combined coefficient matrix. Negative correlation values were set to zero, and the resulting matrix was row-normalized to ensure comparable enhancement strength across spatial cells or spots: 

C = RowNorm [ A ⊙ max( R, 0)] , (1) 

where ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication. 

Enhanced expression matrix. The enhanced expression matrix was computed as follows: 

X′ = X + αCX , (2) 

where X denotes the original expression matrix, C denotes the row-normalized combined coefficient matrix, and α denotes the enhancement coefficient controlling the contribution of the neighborhood-derived expression term. This procedure was applied only to sequencing-based spatial transcriptomics datasets to mitigate expression sparsity while preserving local spatial expression patterns. The default value of α was determined through an ablation study on Slide-tags cortex datasets, in which the enhancement module was either disabled or α was varied from 0.0 to 0.9. This analysis showed that α = 0 .2 yielded the best overall performance in terms of accuracy, weighted F1 score, weighted precision, and weighted recall across datasets and random seeds, whereas α = 0 .0 was equivalent to disabling the enhancement term (Supplementary Table S1). Accordingly, 

α = 0 .2 was adopted as the default enhancement coefficient for sequencing-based scST datasets. 

## Overview of SpCAST 

SpCAST addresses cell-type annotation in single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics (scST) as a reference-guided cross-modal transfer problem. Given an annotated single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) reference and an unannotated scST query dataset, SpCAST learns to transfer cell-type information from reference transcriptomic profiles to spatially resolved molecular measurements while preserving spatial tissue context (Fig. 1A). After preprocessing and gene alignment, let Xsc ∈ RNsc ×G

denote the scRNA-seq reference expression matrix and Xst ∈

RNst ×G denote the scST expression matrix defined on the same set of shared genes. Here, Nsc and Nst denote the numbers of reference cells and spatial cells or spots, respectively, and G

denotes the number of shared genes. The reference annotations are represented as a one-hot label matrix Ysc ∈ { 0, 1}Nsc ×K ,where K is the number of annotated cell types. SpCAST uses a Kolmogorov–Arnold network (KAN)-based encoder to map scRNA-seq and scST profiles into a shared latent space[ 35 ], producing latent representations Hsc and Hst . Built on this shared representation, a classification branch transfers cell-type labels from the annotated scRNA-seq reference to spatial cells or spots, whereas a reconstruction branch decodes the spatial latent representation to generate reconstruction-aware spatial expression profiles. Gene-level contribution scores are further estimated from the trained model to support SHAP-based attribution, marker-gene candidate prioritization and downstream biological interpretation (Fig. 1A).

## Benchmarking and baseline methods 

SpCAST was benchmarked against six representative spatial transcriptomics annotation methods, including cell2location[ 30 ], SPANN[ 74 ], Spatial-ID[ 57 ], Tangram[ 6], STEM[ 22 ] and SpatialScope[ 65 ]. All methods were evaluated on the same paired scRNA-seq reference and spatial transcriptomics datasets whenever applicable. For each dataset, the original cell-type annotations provided by the corresponding study were used as benchmark reference labels. To evaluate model stability, experiments were repeated across multiple random seeds, and the resulting predictions and performance metrics were summarized across runs.

Runtime was evaluated to assess computational efficiency across datasets and methods. # Results 

## SpCAST enables accurate and efficient cell-type annotation 

We first evaluated SpCAST in a comprehensive benchmark for robust cell-type annotation across scST technologies, tissue contexts and dataset scales. To ensure that the benchmark captured methodological diversity rather than a simple comparison with arbitrarily selected tools, we compared SpCAST with six representative baseline methods spanning major computational paradigms for reference-based spatial cell-type annotation and single-cell spatial transcriptomics (scST) integration, including cell2location[ 30 ], SPANN[ 74 ], Spatial-ID[ 57 ], Tangram[ 6], STEM[ 22 ] and SpatialScope[ 65 ]. Specifically, cell2location represents probabilistic Bayesian cell-type mapping and deconvolution; Tangram represents a deep-learning-based spatial alignment framework that optimizes probabilistic single-cell-to-space mappings using shared gene-expression patterns; SPANN represents variational representation learning with prototype-based optimal transport and spatial regularization; Spatial-ID combines supervised transfer learning with spatial embedding; STEM learns spatially aware shared embeddings through deep transfer learning; and SpatialScope leverages deep generative modeling for single-cell and spatial transcriptomics integration. We evaluated annotation performance using complementary metrics: accuracy quantified the overall correctness of cell-type predictions, whereas weighted F1, weighted precision and weighted recall summarized class-wise performance while accounting for differences in cell-type abundance. Robustness to random initialization is essential for interpreting performance differences in multi-dataset benchmarks. The seed-level weighted F1 analysis showed that SpCAST produced consistent performance distributions across repeated runs and maintained low dataset-level standard deviation across seeds. In contrast, several baseline methods showed more pronounced seed-to-seed variability in a subset of datasets, with Spatial-ID displaying particularly large dataset-level variability in some cases. Similar trends were observed for accuracy, weighted precision and weighted recall, demonstrating stable annotation performance of SpCAST under independent random initializations (Fig. 2A and Supplementary Fig. S1). After establishing seed-level stability, we examined dataset-level absolute performance to evaluate whether SpCAST generalized across heterogeneous spatial transcriptomics data types. The circular weighted F1 summary included 53 datasets across five technologies: CosMx SMI, MERFISH, Slide-tags, seqFISH+ and Xenium. These platforms differ in molecular capture strategy, gene-panel design, tissue context and expression sparsity, providing a heterogeneous test of cross-platform annotation performance. Across this dataset-level summary, SpCAST maintained high weighted F1 values for most datasets and generally traced the outer performance envelope among the compared methods, indicating that its advantage was not confined to a single platform or a small subset of datasets. Similar dataset-level patterns were observed for accuracy, weighted precision and weighted recall (Fig. 2C and Supplementary Fig. S2). We next compared SpCAST with baseline methods from the perspectives of relative performance and rank-based aggregation. In the rank-score analysis based on accuracy and weighted F1, SpCAST achieved the highest mean rank scores among all evaluated methods and remained clearly separated from the stronger baseline methods in weighted F1. To further characterize method-specific differences at the dataset level, we calculated performance deltas between SpCAST and each baseline and summarized the corresponding win rates across metrics. The delta analyses showed predominantly positive differences for SpCAST relative to most baselines in accuracy, weighted precision and weighted recall. The win-rate summary showed that SpCAST exceeded Tangram and STEM across all evaluated metrics and maintained high, metric-dependent win rates against cell2location, SPANN, Spatial-ID and SpatialScope. In addition to annotation accuracy, computational efficiency is an important practical criterion for applying cell-type annotation methods to large-scale scST datasets. This consideration is becoming increasingly important as high-throughput spatial platforms with single-cell resolution, such as Xenium, now generate datasets containing hundreds of thousands of cells from a single tissue section. At this scale, computational efficiency affects not only runtime but also whether an annotation 8 Journal Title Here, YEAR, Volume XX, Issue x 

method can complete the analysis within available memory. We therefore measured the wall-clock runtime of each method under a unified benchmarking environment to assess the practical computational cost of the complete annotation pipeline. Runtime distributions varied across scST technologies, consistent with differences in platform-specific gene panels, cell numbers and data sparsity. When average weighted F1 was jointly considered with average runtime, SpCAST occupied the high-performance and low-runtime region, whereas several baseline methods required substantially longer runtime to achieve comparable or lower annotation performance. In contrast, Tangram and STEM showed lower mean weighted F1 or less favorable efficiency positions in this benchmark. Moreover, on larger Xenium datasets, methods that rely on explicit single-cell-to-space mapping matrices can become memory-limited, whereas SpCAST successfully completed the analysis, further demonstrating its scalability and practical utility for large-scale scST analysis. These results indicate that SpCAST provides practical pipeline-level computational efficiency in the evaluated benchmark setting while maintaining robust annotation performance (Fig. 2D and E). 

## SpCAST recapitulates laminar excitatory neuron organization 

Beyond global benchmark performance, spatial annotation methods should preserve biological spatial patterns in tissues with defined organization. We therefore evaluated SpCAST in mouse visual cortex seqFISH+ data, where cortical laminar architecture and excitatory neuron subtypes are well characterized [ 2, 18 ,61 , 62 , 73 ]. This dataset therefore represents a stringent evaluation setting, as excitatory L2/3, L4 and L5/6 neurons are transcriptionally related and spatially adjacent across cortical layers, making subtype-level separation a major source of annotation uncertainty. Consistent with this challenge, overall accuracies remained moderate across methods. Nevertheless, SpCAST achieved the highest accuracy, reaching 0.690 compared with 0.630 for cell2location, 0.620 for SpatialScope and 0.533 for SPANN (Fig. 3A), whereas STEM, Spatial-ID and Tangram showed lower accuracies in the supplementary comparison (Supplementary Fig. S3). Label-transition analysis and complete confusion matrices further showed that the remaining errors of SpCAST were largely confined to related excitatory subtypes, rather than broadly distributed across unrelated cell classes (Fig. 3E and Supplementary Fig. S5). Beyond aggregate accuracy, recovery of laminar spatial organization provided a direct assessment of biological plausibility. In the ground-truth annotations, excitatory L2/3, L4 and L5/6 neurons formed spatial domains corresponding to cortical layers. SpCAST better preserved the relative positioning of these excitatory subtypes, with predicted L2/3, L4 and L5/6 regions following a laminar arrangement similar to the ground truth. In contrast, several baseline methods showed more pronounced subtype mixing, local loss of subtype-specific domains or weaker recovery of layer-associated spatial structure (Fig. 3C). UMAP analysis also indicated that SpCAST maintained the correspondence between predicted and ground-truth labels while preserving the major structure of neuronal and non-neuronal populations (Fig. 3B) [41]. Subtype marker genes provided molecular evidence for the recovered laminar organization. Consistent with previous mouse cortical transcriptomic and laminar atlases, Cux2 and Ngb were associated with superficial excitatory L2/3 cells, Rorb and Scnn1a 

with excitatory L4 cells, and Fezf2 and Foxp2 with deeper excitatory L5/6 cells [ 2, 14 , 15 , 29 , 42 , 44 , 47 , 61 , 62 , 73 ]. The spatial domains predicted by SpCAST showed closer concordance with these marker expression patterns than those produced by several baseline methods, which exhibited more evident marker-domain shifts or subtype mixing in some regions (Fig. 3D). Additional marker analyses incorporating Syt17 , Rspo1 , Tle4 ,

Tbr1 and Sulf1 further showed consistent marker-set enrichment in the corresponding excitatory neuron subtype regions for SpCAST (Supplementary Fig. S4), supporting its recovery of marker-supported laminar excitatory neuron organization. 

## SpCAST supports stable cross-species transfer 

Cross-species spatial transcriptomic annotation provides acomputational entry point for comparing conserved and divergent cellular organization across species, and for examining evolutionary conservation and species-specific divergence in cell-type composition, tissue spatial organization and transcriptional programs [ 25 , 52 ]. To evaluate SpCAST in this more challenging setting, we used Slide-tags data to construct a species-matched reference-transfer comparison and a mouse-to-human cross-species transfer analysis [ 54 ]. In the species-matched human-reference transfer, a human prefrontal cortex snRNA-seq reference was used to annotate the human scST query [ 24 ]; in the mouse-to-human cross-species transfer, a mouse prefrontal cortex scRNA-seq reference was used to annotate the same human scST query [ 5]. Compared with species-matched reference transfer, cross-species transfer is affected by both technical differences between scRNA-seq and scST data and biological differences in gene content, ortholog mapping, cell-type taxonomy and species-specific expression programs [ 48 , 77 ]. The human and mouse datasets contained 13,281 orthologous genes, whereas 15,287 human genes and 6,236 mouse genes had no matched orthologs in the opposite species, indicating that this task was performed in a substantially restricted orthologous feature space. Thus, this analysis was not intended to establish universal cross-species annotation capability, but rather to test whether SpCAST could maintain stable label-transfer performance in an ortholog-constrained Slide-tags mouse-to-human transfer setting (Fig. 4C). We first used species-matched human-reference transfer as the performance reference for cross-species degradation. In this reference task, both the scRNA-seq reference and the scST query were human-derived, so the comparison primarily reflected cross-modal differences between scRNA-seq and scST data without introducing additional species-associated expression divergence. Several methods achieved high annotation accuracy in this task, indicating relatively stable label transfer when the cell-type taxonomy and gene feature space were largely matched. SpCAST reached an accuracy of 0.975, comparable to cell2location at 0.971 and higher than SpatialScope and Spatial-ID at 0.939 and 0.913, respectively. ## SpCAST prioritizes candidate identities for unlabeled Xenium cells 

Spatial annotation workflows often leave a subset of cells without reliable labels, particularly when transcript counts are low, marker evidence is ambiguous or cells reside in complex tumor microenvironments. We used a public human breast cancer Xenium dataset to test whether SpCAST could distinguish low-signal unlabeled cells from analyzable unlabeled cells and provide marker-supported candidate identities for the latter [ 28 ]. Breast cancer tissues contain complex epithelial, stromal, immune, vascular and tumor-associated cellular compartments, providing a challenging context for candidate assignment of difficult-to-annotate cells [ 4, 27 , 31 , 46 , 50 , 68 , 70 ]. In this dataset, 8,554 of 167,780 cells were labeled as Unlabeled, representing 5.1% of all cells (Fig. 5A,B). After transcript-signal quality control, 4,573 originally Unlabeled cells were retained for downstream candidate-label assignment, corresponding to 53.5% of the original Unlabeled population; the remaining 3,981 low-signal cells were excluded from downstream label assignment to avoid over-interpreting cells with insufficient transcript evidence (Supplementary Fig. S9). Before assigning candidate identities to originally Unlabeled cells, we first evaluated each method on cells with existing labels to establish whether the Xenium dataset contained sufficient reference signal for label transfer. This task was non-trivial because breast cancer tissues include multiple cellular states and tumor-microenvironment compartments that can produce related or partially overlapping transcriptional profiles [ 1, 27 , 68 ]. In the non-Unlabeled subset, SpCAST achieved an accuracy of 0.659, exceeding cell2location and slightly outperforming SpatialScope and Spatial-ID, which reached 0.280, 0.639 and 0.630, respectively (Fig. 5A,B). STEM and SPANN showed lower accuracies in the supplementary comparison (Supplementary Fig. S10). Spatial maps and UMAP visualizations indicated that SpCAST better preserved the major cell-type spatial distributions and embedding structure than several baseline methods, supporting its use for candidate assignment of QC-passed originally Unlabeled cells. For the 4,573 QC-passed originally Unlabeled cells, SpCAST generated candidate cell-type predictions. The predicted composition showed that these cells were not collapsed into a single dominant class, but were distributed across stromal, immune, myoepithelial, endothelial, perivascular-like and tumor-associated categories that are consistent with the cellular complexity of breast cancer tissue (Fig. 5C,E) [ 20 , 31 , 50 , 68 ]. In contrast, several baseline methods showed more concentrated class assignments or more evident shifts in spatial and UMAP distributions for QC-passed originally Unlabeled cells. This pattern indicates that the original Unlabeled population contained an analyzable subset with interpretable molecular signal, for which SpCAST provided biologically plausible candidate identities. Marker-gene analyses provided molecular support for the SpCAST-assigned candidate identities of originally Unlabeled cells. The marker heatmap recovered expected transcriptional programs across breast cancer cell classes, including stromal, immune, myoepithelial, endothelial and perivascular-like marker patterns (Fig. 5D) [ 4, 26 , 31 , 46 , 50 , 68 , 70 , 78 ]. Among representative SpCAST-assigned categories, stromal-associated cells expressed LUM , POSTN and SFRP4 ; Myoepi ACTA2+ cells expressed ACTA2 , MYLK and KRT14 ; and B cells expressed MS4A1 , CD79A and CD79B (Fig. 5F) [ 4, 46 , 71 ]. Target-versus-other comparisons further showed higher expression of the corresponding marker genes in the predicted target groups. Additional spatial and differential-expression analyses for macrophage, endothelial, perivascular-like and CD8+ T cell assignments were consistent with the corresponding SpCAST labels (Supplementary Fig. S11) [ 63 ]. Together, these marker-based analyses indicate that SpCAST provided molecularly supported candidate identities for QC-passed originally Unlabeled Xenium cells with sufficient transcript signal.

## SpCAST improves spatial marker coherence 

Finally, we assessed whether the reconstruction branch of SpCAST enhanced the concordance between marker-gene expression and the spatial organization of corresponding cell types. This analysis was performed using a human non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC) CosMx SMI dataset, which provides spatially resolved tumor-microenvironment information and cell-type distributions spanning tumor, fibroblast, immune, endothelial and epithelial compartments [ 23 , 32 , 34 , 38 , 55 , 58 , 64 ]. Because the spatial organization of tumor, stromal and immune compartments is closely linked to tumor-microenvironment structure in NSCLC, this dataset provides a suitable context for evaluating whether reconstructed marker signals are more concordant with the corresponding cell-type regions. Spatial maps and UMAP visualizations first showed that SpCAST preserved the major cell-type organization and embedding structure in the CosMx SMI dataset, providing spatial references for downstream marker–cell-type concordance analysis (Fig. 7A,B). In particular, tumor and fibroblast cells formed recognizable spatial domains in the tissue, enabling evaluation of tumor-associated and fibroblast-associated marker genes against their corresponding cell-type spatial references [ 11 , 16 , 23 , 32 ,34, 38, 55, 67]. Comparison of original and reconstructed expression showed that SpCAST reconstruction increased the spatial continuity of representative marker signals and improved their concordance with the corresponding cell-type regions. For tumor-associated marker genes, the Spearman similarities between original expression and the tumor spatial reference were 0.087 for 

KRT6B and 0.030 for CEACAM6 ; after reconstruction, these values increased to 0.530 and 0.424, respectively. For the fibroblast-associated marker LUM , similarity increased from 0.150 in the original expression to 0.311 after reconstruction (Fig. 7C). Spatial maps showed that the original marker signals were sparse and fragmented, whereas reconstructed expression patterns more closely followed the tumor or fibroblast spatial domains. These results indicate that SpCAST reconstruction provides information beyond discrete cell-type labels by improving the spatial concordance between marker expression and tissue organization. To test whether this improvement was limited to a few example genes, we further summarized gene-level spatial similarity across multiple tumor-associated and fibroblast-associated marker genes. In addition to KRT6B , CEACAM6 and LUM shown in the main spatial maps, KRT5 , KRT18 , KRT19 , EPCAM and 

DCN also showed higher spatial similarity after reconstruction (Supplementary Fig. S15) [ 9, 11 , 34 , 38 , 69 ]. The gene-level summary showed increased marker–cell-type spatial similarity for all evaluated markers, with larger gains for several tumor-associated genes and more modest but directionally consistent improvement for fibroblast-associated genes (Fig. 7D). This gene-specific pattern suggests that SpCAST reconstruction did not simply apply uniform smoothing to all expression fields; instead, it improved marker–cell-type spatial concordance in a gene-dependent manner across multiple marker genes. Because the spatial organization of tumor epithelial and stromal compartments is central to interpreting the NSCLC tumor microenvironment, these results suggest that reconstruction-aware spatial expression modeling in SpCAST can provide a complementary analysis beyond cell-type annotation [ 11 , 16 ,32 , 34 , 38 , 49 , 55 , 58 , 64 ]. Overall, the CosMx SMI analysis supports the ability of SpCAST reconstruction to enhance spatial coherence in sparse or locally fragmented marker expression. This conclusion should be interpreted specifically as an improvement in marker–cell-type spatial concordance, rather than as direct validation that reconstructed expression recovers the unobserved true expression field. 

# Discussion 

In this study, we developed SpCAST, a Kolmogorov–Arnold network (KAN)-based framework for reference-guided analysis of single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics (scST) data [ 35 ]. The main contribution of SpCAST is the integration of cell-type label transfer, gene-level attribution evidence and reconstruction-supported spatial marker analysis within a single model. Compared with workflows that only output discrete cell-type labels or treat annotation, interpretation and expression reconstruction as separate steps, SpCAST provides an integrated framework in which predicted cell identities, gene features supporting those predictions and the concordance between marker expression and cell-type spatial domains can be evaluated together. The methodological design of SpCAST combines nonlinear cross-modal representation learning, distribution alignment, supervised label transfer and expression reconstruction. The KAN-based encoder models nonlinear correspondences between scRNA-seq reference profiles and scST measurements, whereas the MMD-based alignment and supervised classification objectives reduce cross-modal distributional mismatch while preserving cell-type-discriminative information from the annotated reference [ 21 ]. By combining the trained nonlinear prediction function with SHAP-based attribution, SpCAST links model outputs to input-feature contributions and provides model-associated evidence for marker-gene candidate prioritization [37]. Across 53 publicly available datasets comprising 413,376 spatial cells or spots and spanning five spatial transcriptomics technologies, SpCAST achieved competitive or improved annotation performance in the evaluated benchmark setting while maintaining favorable computational efficiency. Crucially, this performance extended beyond aggregate metrics; SpCAST demonstrated consistent stability across imaging-based and sequencing-based platforms, different gene panels, diverse tissue contexts and varying dataset scales. As high-throughput single-cell-resolution spatial platforms such as Xenium and CosMx SMI continue to generate increasingly large datasets, runtime efficiency and memory scalability have become increasingly important practical requirements for spatial annotation methods [23, 28]. Beyond global benchmarking, the case studies further demonstrated the biological utility of SpCAST in complex tissue analysis. In mouse visual cortex data, SpCAST better preserved the laminar organization of excitatory neuron subtypes and showed concordance with spatial expression patterns of known subtype-associated marker genes [ 2, 18 , 61 , 62 ]. When evaluated in an ortholog-constrained mouse-to-human transfer setting using Slide-tags data, SpCAST maintained relatively stable label-transfer performance, providing a concrete example for exploring conserved cellular organization across species [ 25 , 52 , 54 ]. Furthermore, applied to human breast cancer Xenium data, SpCAST stratified originally Unlabeled cells into low-signal and 16 Journal Title Here, YEAR, Volume XX, Issue x 

QC-passed analyzable populations, providing marker-supported candidate identities for the latter [ 27 , 28 , 31 , 68 ]. Together, these analyses indicate that SpCAST is useful not only for label transfer in large-scale benchmarks, but also for tissue-structure interpretation and annotation refinement in complex spatial datasets. The gene-level attribution and reconstruction-supported marker analyses further extend the interpretability of SpCAST outputs. Through cell-type-specific SHAP attribution, SpCAST prioritizes marker-gene candidates associated with each predicted cell type [ 37 ]. In mouse hypothalamus MERFISH data, high-scoring genes were consistent with known transcriptional programs and spatial expression patterns of major neuronal and non-neuronal populations [ 8, 26 , 43 , 75 ]. In lung cancer CosMx SMI data, reconstructed tumor-associated and fibroblast-associated marker signals showed higher concordance with the corresponding cell-type spatial domains [ 23 , 32 , 58 , 64 ]. These results show that SpCAST outputs can extend beyond discrete labels to include gene-level evidence and spatial marker coherence, providing richer interpretive support for cell-type predictions. The interpretation of SpCAST outputs should nevertheless remain aligned with their intended analytical roles. SHAP-derived marker scores are best understood as input-feature attributions of the trained prediction function and are useful for marker-gene candidate prioritization, while conventional differential expression analysis remains important for independent marker validation [ 37 ,51 ]. Reconstructed expression is most appropriately used to evaluate marker–cell-type spatial concordance rather than as an independent ground-truth expression measurement. Similarly, cross-species transfer and candidate assignment of originally Unlabeled cells should be interpreted within the corresponding data conditions, ortholog coverage and marker-evidence context [ 25 , 48 , 52 , 77 ]. These boundaries do not diminish the practical utility of SpCAST; rather, they clarify which downstream analyses are best supported by each model output. Several methodological limitations remain. First, SpCAST relies on annotated scRNA-seq or single-nucleus RNA-seq references for supervised label transfer. Its performance therefore depends on reference quality, cell-type coverage, batch effects and annotation resolution. Second, transcriptionally similar and spatially adjacent cell types remain difficult to distinguish, as observed for related L2/3, L4 and L5/6 excitatory neuron subtypes in mouse visual cortex [ 61 , 62 , 73 ]. Third, gene-panel design and detection efficiency in targeted spatial platforms constrain the available molecular signal, making cells with extremely low transcript coverage or lacking informative markers difficult to annotate or interpret reliably [ 23 , 28 ]. Finally, although SpCAST showed favorable runtime in the current benchmark, atlas-scale datasets may still impose substantial memory and computational demands. Future work could extend SpCAST in several directions. Incorporating complementary modalities, such as histology, protein measurements, cellular morphology or spatial neighborhood information, may improve robustness in low-signal regions, complex tumor tissues and rare cell states [ 27 , 28 , 49 , 79 ]. Pretraining, approximate inference and more efficient mini-batch optimization strategies may further improve scalability for atlas-scale spatial transcriptomics datasets. In addition, reconstructed expression and attribution-derived marker scores should be evaluated across more tissues, gene panels and technology platforms to further clarify the downstream analyses best supported by these outputs. In conclusion, SpCAST provides an efficient and scalable reference-guided framework for scST analysis with gene-level attribution capability. By integrating cell-type label transfer, marker-gene candidate prioritization and reconstruction-supported spatial marker analysis within a unified model, SpCAST links scRNA-seq references with spatial transcriptomics data and provides a computational route for cell-identity annotation, tissue-structure interpretation and marker-gene evidence analysis in complex tissues. Overall, this study supports SpCAST as a practical framework for sparse and targeted single-cell-resolution spatial transcriptomics data, enabling cell identities, gene-level evidence and spatial marker organization to be connected across diverse platforms, tissues and challenging biological and technical contexts.
