Abstract 4

Pathway analysis is a critical step for translating gene-level omics results into biological mechanisms, yet existing workflows often leave researchers with long lists of statistically significant pathways that are difficult to interpret, validate, and connect to experimental context. We developed PAG-Agent, a biologist-oriented virtual research assistant that integrates pathway-level statistical analysis, context-aware biological interpretation, literature-supported reasoning, and scientific writing support within a unified workflow. PAG-Agent supports bulk and single-cell transcriptomic data and enables users to perform data preprocessing, differential expression analysis, pathway analysis, pathway-level consensus analysis, and pathway-level meta-analysis through click-based and chat-based interactions. Unlike conventional pathway-analysis tools that analyze gene sets largely in isolation, PAG-Agent incorporates experimental conditions and research objectives to prioritize biologically relevant pathways and generate interpretable hypotheses. The system also provides gene and pathway annotation, citation retrieval, visualization, and writing refinement functions. In Alzheimer’s disease case studies using three transcriptomic datasets, PAG-Agent consistently identified neurodegeneration-related pathways across multiple analysis methods and datasets. In citation-retrieval benchmarking, PAG-Agent outperformed six competing LLMs across five common literature-support scenarios, demonstrating improved ability to provide contextually relevant and valid references. Overall, PAG-Agent lowers technical barriers for pathway-level analysis and helps researchers move from transcriptomic data to biologically grounded interpretation, hypothesis generation, and scientific communication.

## 1 Introduction 6

Advances in high-throughput omics technologies have enabled researchers to profile molecular changes 7

across diverse biological systems and disease conditions. These studies routinely generate lists of differ-8

entially expressed genes or gene products from bulk and single-cell experiments. Although informative, 9

gene-level results alone rarely explain the biological mechanisms underlying the observed phenotypes. Path-10 

way analysis (PA) therefore serves as a key step for translating molecular changes into biological processes 11 

and functional modules using curated knowledge bases such as KEGG and Gene Ontology (GO) [1–3]. 12 

However, PA often produces large collections of statistically significant pathways. Determining which 13 

pathways are most relevant to the biological question and integrating them into a coherent interpretation 14 

remains a substantial challenge for many researchers. 15 

A wide range of pathway-analysis platforms have been developed to support functional interpretation 16 

of both bulk and single-cell omics data. These tools provide capabilities such as differential expression 17 

analysis (DEA), PA, consensus analysis, meta-analysis, and visualization [4–17]. Despite these advances, 18 

the burden of interpretation remains largely on the user. Researchers must decide which analytical methods 19 

to apply, compare outputs across methods or datasets, examine biological relevance, and connect significant 20 

pathways to the studied phenotype. These tasks often require substantial domain expertise and manual 21 

effort, particularly for early-career researchers and experimental biologists with limited computational 22 

backgrounds [18–21]. As a result, obtaining statistically significant pathways does not necessarily translate 23 

into efficient biological interpretation. 24 

Large language models (LLMs) have emerged as promising tools for biological interpretation, literature 25 

synthesis, and hypothesis generation. Recent studies have demonstrated that LLMs can assist gene-set 26 

enrichment analysis by transforming molecular signatures into biologically meaningful functional anno-27 

tations [22, 23]. However, these approaches typically ignore experimental conditions, research objectives, 28 

and other contextual information that motivate the study from the outset. In addition, they provide 29 

limited support for the broader workflow that researchers routinely follow to move from omics data to 30 

biological insight. Beyond pathway analysis, researchers often need to explore differentially expressed 31 

genes, pathways, and biological terms, validate findings through the scientific literature, generate hypothe-32 

ses, integrate evidence across datasets, and communicate results. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) 33 

systems have been proposed to improve factual accuracy by incorporating external knowledge during infer-34 

ence [24, 25]. However, these approaches often require substantial computational resources and continuous 35 

maintenance of large knowledge repositories. A practical framework that combines pathway-level analysis, 36 

context-aware biological interpretation, and reliable literature support within a unified workflow remains 37 

lacking. 38 

Here, we present PAG-Agent, a virtual research assistant for pathway-level analysis that integrates 39 

statistical analysis, context-aware biological interpretation, literature-grounded reasoning, and research 40 

support within a unified workflow. Unlike existing approaches that analyze gene sets in isolation, PAG-41 

Agent incorporates experimental conditions and research objectives throughout the analytical process to 42 

prioritize pathways and biological findings that are most relevant to the study context. PAG-Agent sup-43 

ports both bulk and single-cell transcriptomic data and enables researchers to perform data preprocessing, 44 

DEA, PA, gene- and pathway-level consensus analysis, and meta-analysis through a user-friendly inter-45 

face. Beyond statistical analyses, PAG-Agent assists users in exploring genes, pathways, and biological 46 

terms, retrieving supporting literature, generating hypotheses, and refining scientific writing. To improve 47 

the reliability of literature-supported responses, PAG-Agent incorporates lightweight in-context learning 48 

strategies that reduce hallucinations without the computational overhead associated with large retrieval 49 

systems. Together, these capabilities enable researchers to move more efficiently from omics data to bio-50 

logical insight. 51

Detailed annotations for genes or pathways provided critical biological context for interpreting results and 153 

selecting candidates for follow-up studies. Traditionally, computational scientists performing DEA and PA 154 

would deliver lists of significant genes or pathways to biologists without additional context. Consequently, 155 

experimentalists had to identify relevant knowledge bases, extract, and analyze the required information to 156 

address their queries. Common questions included understanding the biological role of a gene or pathway, 157 

identifying related pathways, determining which drugs targeted a specific gene or pathway, and exploring 158 

associations between genes or pathways and the studied phenotypes. This process was particularly chal-159 

lenging for novice biologists unfamiliar with bioinformatics. For example, in Fig. 2b, ALB (Entrez ID 213) 160 

ranked high on the DEA results, while PAG-Agent identified metabolic pathways (hsa01100) as significant 161 

(Fig. 3). However, even domain experts struggled to immediately provide accurate biological context or 162 

justify associations with Alzheimer’s disease. PAG-Agent, leveraging the combined power of GPT-4o-mini 163 

and in-context learning techniques (Methods), rapidly generated detailed and accurate annotations of these 164 

genes and pathways as well as made hypothesis on their relation with the studied conditions, as shown 165 

in Supplementary Fig. 27 and 28. These AI-generated hypotheses provided biologists with interpretable 166 

results or actionable starting points for further investigation. Notably, we encouraged LLMs to generate 167 

creative and novel hypotheses, exploring plausible connections between discoveries and phenotypes. We 168 

tolerated outputs containing hallucinations when they introduced potential insights that had not been 169 

previously considered. 170 

A key aspect of comparative studies involved determining whether any previous articles reported simi-171 

lar findings. Supplementary Fig. 29 illustrates PAG-Agent, integrated with a search engine (see Methods), 172 

retrieved relevant references linking Oxidative Phosphorylation to Alzheimer’s disease. It further summa-173 

rized these references and presented them in an academic format suitable for immediate use. To benchmark 174 

its performance, we compared PAG-Agent against six competing LLMs across five common scenarios (see 175 

Methods and Supplementary Table 2). Importantly, we intentionally downgraded GPT-4o-mini (version 176 

‘gpt-4o-mini’) to GPT-3.5 Turbo (version ‘gpt-3.5-turbo-0125’) as the backend for PAG-Agent to highlight 177

## 3 Discussion 199 

Pathway-level analysis often extends beyond statistical enrichment. Researchers must interpret biological 200 

findings, evaluate their relevance to the studied phenotype, validate observations through the literature, 201 

integrate evidence across datasets, and communicate results. Existing pathway-analysis platforms pro-202 

vide many of the statistical components required for these tasks, whereas recent LLM-based approaches 203 

facilitate biological interpretation. However, these capabilities are typically distributed across separate 204 

tools and workflows. PAG-Agent addresses this gap by integrating statistical analysis, context-aware 205 

pathway interpretation, literature-supported reasoning, and research assistance within a single platform. 206 

The system supports both bulk and single-cell transcriptomic data and provides a unified workflow span-207 

ning differential expression analysis, pathway analysis, consensus analysis, meta-analysis, and post-analysis 208 

activities. In addition, PAG-Agent incorporates experimental conditions and research objectives into path-209 

way interpretation. The favorable Accuracy, HIT rate, and F1 score achieved across 65 benchmark studies 210 

suggest that contextual information can improve the prioritization of biologically relevant pathways beyond 211 

conventional enrichment statistics alone. 212 

A central design objective of PAG-Agent is to improve the reliability of literature-supported responses 213 

without relying on large, manually maintained knowledge repositories. Retrieval-augmented generation 214 

(RAG) systems have emerged as a common strategy for reducing hallucinations by incorporating external 215 

knowledge during inference. However, maintaining large collections of scientific materials requires continu-216 

ous updates and additional computational resources. PAG-Agent instead combines lightweight information 217 

retrieval with in-context learning. Relevant references are dynamically retrieved from external sources and 218 

incorporated into prompts as contextual evidence for response generation. This strategy enables access 219 

to recent scientific findings while maintaining a lightweight architecture. Consistent with this design, 220 

PAG-Agent outperformed six competing LLMs in retrieving contextually relevant references across five 221 

evaluation scenarios. 222 

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, our demonstration focused primarily on three 223 

Alzheimer’s disease datasets and may not capture the full diversity of biological applications supported 224 

by the platform. Second, although the benchmark results support the utility of context-aware pathway 225 

prioritization, the benchmark dataset used for evaluation remains relatively modest in size. Future stud-226 

ies will expand benchmarking across additional diseases, organisms, and experimental settings. Future 227 

work will also investigate how experimental conditions and research objectives can be incorporated more 228 

systematically into pathway interpretation and biological hypothesis generation. 229 

Overall, PAG-Agent lowers the technical barriers associated with pathway-level analysis and inter-230 

pretation. By combining statistical analyses, biological reasoning, literature support, and scientific com-231 

munication within a unified workflow, the platform enables researchers to move more efficiently from 232 

transcriptomic data to biological insight. 233

4.2 Prompt engineering 244 

The collaborative functioning of eight engineered prompts underpins the smooth operation of PAG-Agent 245 

(Supplementary Fig. 31). These prompts included: (i) User Intent Detection : This prompt detects 246 

user intents, each related to a specific task. (ii) Pathway Analysis Method Detection : This prompt 247 

identifies the pathway analysis method requested by the user. (iii) Meta-analysis Method Detection :248 

This prompt determines the meta-analysis method requested by the user. (iv) Gene Annotation : This 249 

prompt places a gene to its biological context. (v) KEGG Pathway Annotation : This prompt provides 250 

information related to a specific KEGG pathway. (vi) Gene Ontology Term Annotation : This prompt 251 

provides information about a specific Gene Ontology (GO) term. (vii) Citation Retrieval : This prompt 252 

finds relevant and valid citations based on user-provided context. (viii) Writing Refinement : This 253 

prompt refines user-submitted academic writing. 254 

We engineer these prompts to identify predefined keywords that indicate user intents for PAG-Agent. 255 

User Intent Detection includes 10 preset user intents, each linked to a list of keywords. For exam-256 

ple, the pathway analysis intent includes keywords: “Users mention the following keywords: “pathway 257 

analysis”, “enrichment analysis”, “functional analysis”, “gene set analysis”, “gene set enrichment anal-258 

ysis”, “fgsea”, “ora”, “over-representation analysis”, “ks”, “ks-test”, “ks test”, “kolmogorov-smirnov”, 259 

“kolmogorov-smirnov test”, “wilcox”, “wilcox-test”, “wilcox test”, “wilcoxon”, “wilcoxon-test”, “wilcoxon 260 

test”, “hypergeometric test”, “hypergeometric”, “fisher”, “fisher’s exact test”, “fisher’s exact”, or “fisher 261 

exact test” ”. When a user submits a query like “ Can you perform ora for me? ”, PAG-Agent detects 262 

the keyword “ora” and maps it to the PA intent. The system then proceeds to the Pathway Analy-263 

sis Method Detection prompt to determine the specific method (over-representation analysis in this 264 

example). Supplementary Fig. 31 outlines the hierarchical organization of these prompts. 265 

To minimize hallucinations in responses from the Gene Annotation , KEGG Pathway Annotation ,266

4.6 Data preprocessing 334 

Data preprocessing is a critical step for all statistical analyses. Zhang et al. [46] have demonstrated 335 

that pathway analysis is highly influenced by the normalization procedures applied to gene expression 336 

measurements. PAG-Agent provides three preprocessing options depending on input data. 337 

Imputation methods, including mean, median, and zero imputation, are available to handle missing 338 

values by replacing them with the average, median, or zero value of the corresponding gene across all 339 

samples. Users should select the most appropriate method based on the dataset characteristics. Mean 340 

or median imputation is generally suitable for Affymetrix and Agilent data (continuous data) to account 341 

for random missing values or reduce skewness. Zero imputation, in contrast, is often more suitable for 342 

count-based transcriptomic data, including bulk RNA-seq and scRNA-seq data, where zero values may 343 

reflect biological absence or technical sparsity. 344 

For bulk transcriptomic data, normalization options include TPM normalization and quantile normal-345 

ization. TPM normalization is recommended for bulk RNA-seq data, whereas quantile normalization is 346 

recommended for microarray data. For single-cell RNA-seq data, PAG-Agent provides quality-control fil-347 

tering based on the number of detected genes, total UMI counts, and mitochondrial gene fraction. After 348 

filtering, the platform applies library-size normalization followed by natural-log transformation to improve 349 

comparability across cells. 350 

For bulk transcriptomic data, log2 transformation is applied to stabilize variance and reduce skew-351 

ness. For single-cell RNA-seq data, PAG-Agent applies natural-log transformation following library-size 352 

normalization as part of the standard preprocessing workflow. 353 

4.7 Parameter setting for differential expression analysis 354 

Differential expression analysis (DEA) is a fundamental approach for identifying genes that are differentially 355 

expressed between distinct conditions or phenotypes. Numerous robust techniques have been developed 356 

for differential analysis [47–49]. In this work, PAG-Agent supports multiple differential expression analysis 357 

methods tailored to the characteristics of bulk and single-cell transcriptomic data. For bulk transcriptomic 358 

datasets, the platform provides limma [33], DESeq2 [48], and edgeR [50]. For scRNA-seq datasets, PAG-359 

Agent supports the Wilcoxon rank-sum test, MAST [51], logistic regression, and DESeq2. 360 

Users can select the most appropriate method based on the assay technology and data representation. 361 

Limma is typically applied to continuous data, such as microarray expression or TPM-normalized bulk 362 

RNA-seq data, whereas DESeq2 and edgeR are suited for count-based bulk RNA-seq data or scRNA-363 

seq data. For single-cell RNA-seq analyses, the Wilcoxon rank-sum test and logistic regression provide 364 

robust non-parametric and discriminative alternatives, while MAST explicitly models zero inflation and 365 

cellular detection rates commonly observed in single-cell measurements. The primary output of these 366 

methods includes a list of genes with their corresponding fold-changes, p-values, adjusted p-values. Ad-367 

justed p-values, calculated using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure [35], are used to identify significantly 368 

differentially expressed genes. 369

4.8 Parameter setting for pathway-level analyses 370 

The platform supports several widely used pathway analysis (PA) methods, including ORA, fGSEA [34] 371 

(default), the KS test, and the Wilcoxon test, each designed to detect different data patterns. In this work, 372 

the default method refers to cases where the User Intent Detection prompt identifies a user intent for PA, 373 

but the Pathway Analysis Method Detection prompt cannot determine the preferred method (e.g., due to 374 

typos or unspecified method names). In such cases, PAG-Agent defaults to using fGSEA. 375 

PAG-Agent currently supports 10 organisms: H. sapiens , M. musculus , R. norvegicus , D. rerio , D. 376 

melanogaster , C. elegans , S. cerevisiae , A. thaliana , S. pombe , and P. falciparum . Gene sets for these 377 

species from KEGG (Release 112.1) and GO (2024-11-03) are integrated into the platform. Previous 378 

studies have highlighted the adverse impact of smaller gene sets on pathway analysis performance [46, 52]. 379 

Damian and Gorfine [53] attributed this to larger gene variances in smaller gene sets, which affect the 380 

accuracy of test statistics for enrichment. To address this, PAG-Agent excludes gene sets containing fewer 381 

than 10 genes. 382 

Existing web-based tools for pathway analysis face several limitations: (i) they cannot combine, com-383 

pare, or contrast results from multiple methods, (ii) they lack the ability to integrate results across datasets, 384 

and (iii) they do not support comprehensive visualization of gene networks and expression changes simul-385 

taneously. PAG-Agent addresses these shortcomings through two key strategies: pathway-level consensus 386 

analysis and pathway-level meta-analysis. 387 

Pathway-level consensus analysis, also known as multi-method analysis, combines results from different 388 

PA methods, such as the KS test and fGSEA, applied to a single gene expression dataset. This approach 389 

enhances robustness by identifying pathways consistently enriched across methods. PAG-Agent provides 390 

two methods for consensus analysis: weighted Z mean (default) and robust rank aggregation (RRA) [54]. 391 

Pathway-level meta-analysis, also known as multi-method and multi-cohort analysis, integrates pathway-392 

or gene-level results across multiple datasets, offering a unified perspective and improving reliability. 393 

Pathway-level results are generated from (i) a single dataset using different combinations of DEA and PA 394 

methods or (ii) multiple datasets from different experiments analyzed with similar or varied approaches. 395 

Similarly, gene-level results combines DEA results within the same context. PAG-Agent supports sev-396 

eral established meta-analysis methods: Fisher’s method [55], Stouffer’s method [37] (default), minimum 397 

p-value [56], geometric mean, addCLT [57], and restricted (residual) maximum likelihood (REML) [58]. 398 

These methods are chosen for their ability to account for heterogeneity across studies, yielding more 399 

accurate combined statistics. 400 

Importantly, identification through these integrative analyses does not necessarily imply greater bio-401 

logical significance for a specific gene or pathway. Instead, these methods provide a robust foundation for 402 

generating hypotheses that can be explored further in experimental studies. 403 

4.9 cGSA-like methodology 404 

4.9.1 Methodology 405 

To improve the biological relevance of pathway interpretation, PAG-Agent incorporates a context-aware 406 

pathway prioritization workflow. Starting from a set of differentially expressed genes derived from bulk or 407 

single-cell transcriptomic analyses, we first construct a weighted gene–gene interaction network using the 408 

HAPPI2 database. Each edge is weighted by the corresponding HAPPI confidence score, resulting in a 409 

weighted adjacency matrix that captures both the connectivity and confidence of known functional rela-410 

tionships among genes. Next, we apply the oCEM framework to identify overlapping gene modules from 411 

the weighted adjacency matrix. Specifically, oCEM uses independent component analysis to decompose the 412 

interaction network into partially overlapping functional modules, allowing genes to participate in multiple 413

It is made The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 6, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.64898/2026.06.02.729674 doi: bioRxiv preprint

biological processes. This property better reflects the pleiotropic nature of biological systems compared 414 

with non-overlapping clustering approaches. For each identified module, we perform ORA against path-415 

way databases to obtain candidate enriched pathways. Because multiple enriched pathways may represent 416 

related or partially redundant biological processes, PAG-Agent further prioritizes these candidates using 417 

contextual information available from the original study. Specifically, PAG-Agent leverages experimental 418 

conditions to estimate the contextual relevance of each candidate pathway using GPT-4o-mini with a tem-419 

perature of zero. The model assigns a relevance score that reflects the consistency between the pathway 420 

and the experimental context. Subsequently, PAG-Agent integrates three complementary criteria when 421 

selecting a representative pathway for each module: (i) pathway statistical significance derived from ORA, 422 

weighted by module quality through the CoCo score; (ii) contextual relevance estimated from the exper-423 

imental conditions; and (iii) consistency with the stated research objectives. Based on these criteria, the 424 

model selects a representative pathway that best explains the biological question under investigation. 425 

4.9.2 Benchmark dataset construction 426 

Evaluating context-aware pathway prioritization requires benchmark datasets in which biologically rele-427 

vant pathways can be linked to experimentally derived gene sets. Existing pathway-analysis benchmarks 428 

primarily focus on statistical enrichment performance and rarely provide pathway annotations that can 429 

serve as reference interpretations for evaluating context-aware reasoning. To address this limitation, we 430 

constructed a benchmark dataset from RummaGEO ( https://rummageo.com/ ), a large collection of gene 431 

sets derived from publicly available transcriptomic studies. For each study, we collected the differentially 432 

expressed gene set together with its corresponding pathway annotations reported in the original source. 433 

We then applied a series of quality-control criteria to retain only studies with sufficient information for 434 

evaluating pathway prioritization (see Supplementary Information Section 5). Briefly, studies were re-435 

quired to provide both gene sets and associated pathway annotations, enabling direct comparison between 436 

predicted pathways and study-reported biological findings. After filtering, the final benchmark consisted 437 

of 65 independent studies spanning diverse biological conditions and experimental settings (Supplementary 438 

Table 3). For each study, the differentially expressed genes served as the input to PAG-Agent, while the 439 

pathway annotations reported by the original study were treated as reference pathways for evaluation. 440 

This benchmark enabled systematic assessment of PAG-Agent’s ability to prioritize biologically relevant 441 

pathways using experimental conditions and research objectives. 442
