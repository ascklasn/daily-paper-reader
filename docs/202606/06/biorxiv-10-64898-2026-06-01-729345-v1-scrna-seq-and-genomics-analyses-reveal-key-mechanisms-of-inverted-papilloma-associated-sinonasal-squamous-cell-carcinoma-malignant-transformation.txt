Abstract 46 

Sinonasal inverted papilloma (IP) carries a 10% risk of malignant transformation to IP-associated 47 

sinonasal squamous cell carcinoma (IP-SNSCC), yet the molecular and immune drivers of this 48 

progression remain poorly defined. This study integrates single-cell RNA sequencing, 49 

multiplexed spatial proteomics, whole-exome sequencing, and functional assays across IP and 50 

IP-SNSCC cohorts to define mechanisms of malignant transformation. Respiratory epithelial 51 

basal cells are identified as the putative cell of origin, with progression marked by recurrent 52 

CDKN2A loss and TP53 mutations. Spatial profiling reveals immune reorganization at the 53 

lesional interface in IP-SNSCC, characterized by enrichment of alternatively activated M2 54 

macrophages. CXCL14 is shown to directly induce an immunosuppressive myeloid phenotype 55 

that suppresses T-cell IFN γ production through an IDO-pathway-dependent mechanism. 56 

Integration of these multimodal datasets defines a previously unrecognized CXCL14-IDO 57 

mechanism that constrains anti-tumor immunity at the tumor-stroma interface. These findings 58 

establish IDO-targeted immunomodulation as a rational adjuvant strategy and provide a 59 

comprehensive molecular framework for understanding IP-SNSCC pathogenesis. 60

Introduction 61 

Sinonasal squamous cell carcinoma (SNSCC) is the most common sinonasal malignancy, 62 

accounting for ~60-75% of sinonasal cancers 1-3 . Despite improvements in outcomes for patients 63 

with squamous cell carcinomas arising at other head and neck cancer subsites over the last three 64 

decades, the prognosis for patients with SNSCC has remained largely unchanged, with 5-year 65 

overall survival hovering around 50% 1 . This stagnation reflects a limited understanding of the 66 

biological mechanisms underlying SNSCC pathogenesis. Surgical resection with curative intent 67 

remains the primary treatment modality, and although advances in endoscopic approaches have 68 

reduced treatment-related morbidity 4, surgery is frequently constrained by the close proximity of 69 

sinonasal tumors to critical neurovascular structures 1 . Consequently, there is a pressing need for 70 

effective adjunct and alternative therapeutic strategies. 71 

Sinonasal inverted papilloma (IP) is one of the most frequently observed benign sinonasal 72 

neoplasms, with malignant transformation to IP-derived sinonasal squamous cell carcinoma (IP-73 

SNSCC) occurring in up to 10% of patients 5,6 . The mechanisms underlying progression from IP 74 

to IP-SNSCC remain poorly understood, and molecular features that distinguish IP-SNSCC from 75 

IP are incompletely defined. EGFR exon 20 activating mutations or low-risk human 76 

papillomavirus (HPV) serotypes have been detected in both IP and IP-SNSCC tumors 7-9 ,77 

supporting the concept that these lesions represent a continuum of neoplastic evolution 9 .78 

Moreover, CDKN2A mutations have been observed in IP-SNSCC tumors but are absent in 79 

matched benign precursor lesions, suggesting that these alterations may represent early 80 

molecular events in malignant progression 10 .81 

Beyond epithelial cell-intrinsic alterations, malignant progression is also likely to depend 82 

on remodeling of the local immune microenvironment 11 . Spatially organized myeloid 83 

immunoregulatory states, including those shaped by chemokine signaling and metabolic immune 84

Here, we performed the first single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) of IP and IP-88 

SNSCC tumors using 10x Genomics 5′ gene expression profiling. We further integrated whole-89 

exome sequencing, spatial proteomic profiling, and functional assays to characterize IP and IP-90 

SNSCC and define molecular and immune features associated with disease progression. Through 91 

this multi-modal approach, we identify candidate mechanisms linking malignant transformation 92 

to local immunoregulation and, to our knowledge, provide the first integrated molecular analysis 93 

of neoplastic transformation from IP to IP-SNSCC. 94

Results 96 

Cohort characteristics 97 

After quality assessment, 21 specimens collected from 19 patients (15 with benign sinonasal 98 

papilloma and 4 with IP-SNSCC) were retained for downstream scRNA-seq analyses. WES was 99 

performed in 18 patients (14 benign sinonasal papillomas and 4 IP-SNSCC), yielding 36 DNA 100 

samples (18 tumor-normal pairs). Seventeen patients were included in both datasets, whereas two 101 

patients were represented only in the scRNA-seq cohort and one IP-SNSCC patient only in the 102 

WES dataset. Clinicopathologic and demographic characteristics are summarized in Table 1 . The 103 

mean age at diagnosis was 63.9 years (s.d. 10.6), with a median of 69.0 years (interquartile 104 

range, 54.0 - 72.0). Patients with benign sinonasal papillomas had a mean age of 63.1 years, 105 

compared with 66.2 years for patients with IP-SNSCC. The cohort was predominantly male (15 106 

of 20, 75%), including 11 cases with IP (73.3%) and 4 with IP-SNSCC (80.0%). Self-reported 107 

race was White in 60% of patients (12 of 20), Black in 35% (7 of 20), and Asian in 5% (1 of 20). 108

Distinct cellular landscape of the IP and IP-SNSCC tumor microenvironment 112 

To uncover the molecular mechanisms driving malignant transformation, we performed the first 113 

known scRNA-seq analysis of tumors from patients with IP and IP-SNSCC ( Fig. 1a ). Unbiased 114 

clustering was applied to the combined single cell transcriptomes, identifying 25 distinct cell 115 

clusters ( Fig. 1b ). Although cluster proportions varied, each cluster was consistently observed 116 

across tumor samples rather than reflecting patient-specific subpopulations ( Fig. S1a-d ). 117 

Differential expression analysis revealed canonical marker genes specific to each cluster, 118 

delineating major cell populations and subpopulations, including ciliated epithelial cells, 119 

malignant epithelial cells, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, pericytes, T cells, B cells, mast cells, and 120 

myeloid cells ( Fig. 1c ). We next performed spatial phenotyping on formalin-fixed paraffin-121 

embedded (FFPE) tissue from IP and IP-SNSCC tumors using the PhenoCycler-Fusion system 122 

(Fig. 1d ). A panel of 25 markers was applied ( Fig. S2a ), and single-cell segmentation was 123 

carried out using StarDist to define nuclear and cell membrane boundaries ( Fig. S2b-d ). IP-124 

SNSCC tumors were relatively immune-rich compared to IP neoplasms ( Fig. 1e, f ), displaying a 125 

diverse immune infiltrate ( Fig. 1g ), consistent with prior reports describing sinonasal squamous 126 

cell carcinoma as an immunogenic tumor type 14,15 .127

Myeloid cells are enriched in the IP-SNSCC tumor microenvironment compared to IP 174 

Given the heterogeneous immune landscape revealed by scRNA-seq profiling, we next 175 

interrogated the immune composition of IP and IP-SNSCC neoplasms. Cluster proportion 176 

analysis comparing immune cell populations across samples showed a consistent trend towards 177 

increased myeloid cell representation in IP-SNSCC tumors ( Fig. 3a-g ), with marked enrichment 178 

of myeloid cell cluster 1 relative to sinonasal papillomas ( Fig. 3b ). By contrast, no notable trends 179 

or significant differences were observed in the proportions of B cells, mast cells, natural killer 180 

cells, CD8+ T cells, or regulatory T cells between IP and IP-SNSCC tumors ( Fig. 3h-l ). 181

We further characterized myeloid subpopulations identified in our unsupervised clustering 184 

analysis. Interestingly, expression of the G-protein coupled receptor 183 ( GPR183 ) and regulator 185 

of G-protein signaling 1 ( RGS1 ) emerged as a shared feature across multiple myeloid 186 

subpopulations ( Fig. 3m ). Expression of the alarmins S100A8 and S100A9 , was a defining 187

characterized by expression of the epidermal growth factor receptor ligand epiregulin ( EREG ), a 189

IDO1-high macrophages are enriched in IP-SNSCC 216 

Given the interface-proximal localization of macrophages in IP-SNSCC, we next interrogated 217 

whether this compartment also exhibited an immunoregulatory phenotype. Indoleamine 2,3-218 

dioxygenase 1 (IDO1) is a tryptophan-catabolizing enzyme expressed by activated myeloid cells 219 

and other components of the tumor microenvironment that functions as a metabolic immune 220 

checkpoint. IDO1 contributes to suppression of effector T-cell responses through local depletion 221 

of tryptophan and accumulation of immunosuppressive kynurenine metabolites. IDO1+ myeloid 222 

cells have been implicated in immune evasion across multiple solid tumors, including head and 223 

neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC). To determine whether IP-SNSCC lesions harbor 224 

localized IDO1-enriched immunoregulatory niches at the tumor interface, we assessed 225 

macrophage IDO1 expression as a function of distance from the lesional compartment. Mean 226 

macrophage IDO1 expression was higher in IP-SNSCC than in IP ( Fig. 4e ), and the fraction of 227 

IDO1-high macrophages was also increased (median, 21.0% versus 4.2%), representing an 228 

approximately fivefold difference between groups ( Fig. 4f ). Higher macrophage IDO1 229

Macrophage IDO1 expression is spatially patterned in IP-SNSCC 237 

As macrophages were stratified into core (0-50 μm), margin (50 -200 μm) and far (>200 μm) 238 

zones, IDO1 intensity distributions were compared across these regions. In IP-SNSCC, median 239 

macrophage IDO1 expression was highest in interface-adjacent zones, particularly the margin 240 

compartment ( Fig. 4g ). This spatial pattern was also evident at the individual-sample level, 241 

where IP-SNSCC specimens showing consistently higher IDO1 expression in the margin and far 242 

regions, whereas IP cases exhibited lower and more heterogeneous zonal values ( Fig. 4h ). 243 

Representative spatial maps from IP-SNSCC 336 further illustrate macrophage zone assignment 244 

across the whole section, IDO1-enriched macrophages within an interface-adjacent ROI, and the 245 

corresponding raw PhenoCycler ROI (Fig. 4i-k). Collectively, these data indicate that 246 

macrophage IDO1 expression in IP-SNSCC is spatially structured rather than uniformly 247 

distributed, with enrichment in interface-associated regions. 248

mechanism, we treated human SNSCC-derived cell lines (NC1 and NC6), IP-SNSCC-derived 275 

cells (NC4), and a non-sinonasal SCC cell line established from oral cavity cancer (UMSCC1), 276 

with recombinant human CXCL14 (rhCXCL14) (Fig. S6a-f ). Notably, NC1 and NC4 cells 277

mechanism 298 

Having established that CXCL14 promotes an immunoregulatory myeloid state in vitro , we next 299 

asked whether CXCL14-conditioned CD14+ myeloid cells suppress T-cell function, a previously 300 

unrecognized function of CXCL14. CD14 ⁺ monocytes from three independent healthy donors 301

Discussion 342 

In this study, we generated the first integrated single-cell transcriptomic and spatial proteomic 343 

map of malignant transformation from sinonasal inverted papilloma (IP) to IP-associated 344 

squamous cell carcinoma (IP-SNSCC). This multimodal analysis revealed progression as a 345 

coordinated process of epithelial, genomic and immune remodeling, implicating respiratory 346 

basal-like precursor states and recurrent CDKN2A loss and TP53 alterations together with the 347 

emergence of a spatially restricted immunosuppressive myeloid niche at the tumor-stroma 348 

interface. Within this niche, CXCL14-expressing tumor cells were spatially associated with 349 

macrophages and IDO1-enriched myeloid cells at the invasive front, distinguishing IP-SNSCC 350

Study considerations and limitations. 444 

The spatial analyses were performed on two-dimensional tissue sections, and architectural 445 

differences between papillary IP lesions and more solid IP-SNSCC tumor nests can influence 446 

distance-based measurements; nevertheless, distance-to-tumor and compartment-specific 447 

infiltration metrics are widely used in multiplex imaging studies and remain informative when 448 

interpreted alongside morphology 34,51 . In addition, the cohort size for high-plex spatial profiling 449 

was limited, restricting formal inference and motivating validation in larger, clinically annotated 450 

datasets. Furthermore, although our co-culture experiments provide functional support for a 451 

CXCL14-linked myeloid suppressive mechanism associated with IDO-pathway activity, they do 452 

not establish direct molecular regulation of IDO1 by CXCL14 or fully recapitulate the 453 

complexity of the intact tumor microenvironment. Additional mechanistic and in vivo studies 454 

will be needed to determine how CXCL14 shapes myeloid cell states within IP-SNSCC, to 455 

define the precise relationship between CXCL14 and IDO-pathway activation, and to evaluate 456 

whether this immunoregulatory mechanism can be therapeutically exploited. 457

Deriving cell-based and spatial features 549 

Cell-based and spatial feature derivation have been previously described 65 . Briefly, for each 550 

sample, a 39-length vector was computed with the average biomarker pixel intensity across the 551 

sample image for each biomarker and with the average biomarker pixel intensity within the cell 552 

segmentation masks for each biomarker. A 16-length vector was then computed with the fraction 553 

of each cell type present in the sample. This vector summed to 1. 554 

555 

Neighborhood matrix 556 

For each sample, we computed a “neighborhood matrix,” M, which is a matrix with shape 557 

(number of cell types) × (number of cell types), and each row sums to 1. Here, M is a 16 × 16 558 

matrix. Element m_ij is the fraction of cell type j within the k-nearest neighbors of cell type i, 559 

averaged across all cells of type i in the sample. In this work, we use k = 10, and nearest 560 

neighbors are computed using the centroid coordinates of each cell. 561

Somatic variant detection and annotation 724 

Whole-exome sequencing data were processed using the nf-core/sarek workflow (v3.3.2) 725 

executed with Nextflow v23.04.4 in a reproducible Singularity-based environment. Reads were 726 

aligned to the GRCh38 human reference genome using bwa-mem2. Somatic single-nucleotide 727 

variants (SNVs) and small insertions/deletions (indels) were identified in paired tumor-normal 728 

mode using matched PBMC-derived normal DNA with GATK Mutect2 v4.4.0.0 and Strelka 729 

v2.9.10. The workflow incorporated a panel of normals and population germline resources to 730 

reduce technical artifacts and likely germline calls. Somatic variants were functionally annotated 731 

using Ensembl VEP v110. For cohort-level analyses, annotated variants were summarized by 732 

variant classification, variant type, single-nucleotide substitution class, and number of consensus 733 

variants per sample. Recurrently altered genes were identified according to alteration frequency 734 

across the cohort. 735

Copy-number analysis 737 

Copy-number profiles were computed using superFreq v1.3.2. Gene-level copy-number events 738 

were categorized as gain (3 copies), amplification (≥4 copies), copy-neutral loss of 739 

heterozygosity (CNN-LOH), loss, or complete loss. Cohort-level copy-number summaries were 740 

calculated as the fraction of individuals harboring each event per gene, and integrated 741 

visualizations were generated to display the combined frequencies of copy-number and sequence 742 

alterations across the cohort. 743
