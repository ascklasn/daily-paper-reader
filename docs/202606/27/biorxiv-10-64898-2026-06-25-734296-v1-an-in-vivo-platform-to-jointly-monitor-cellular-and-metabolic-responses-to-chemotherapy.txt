# Abstract 32 

How drug treatments reshape immune and metabolic states within intact tumors remains difficult 33 

to study with existing methods. We introduce a spatial pharmacology platform that enables parallel 34 

analysis of multiple agents within a single tumor, linking local drug exposure to immune and 35 

metabolic remodeling. Using a microdevice for localized drug delivery, we created a large-scale 36 

paired CyCIF–MALDI dataset spanning 1.5 million cells across 27 MMTV-PyMT tumor sections 37 

and nine treatment programs, enabling integrated spatial pharmacology at unprecedented scale. 38 

Metabolic signatures robustly predict proteomic spatial neighborhoods establishing metabolism as 39 

a powerful predictor of tumor organization and immune phenotype. Within this framework, we 40 

identify a dominant metabolic axis defined by the myeloid polarization between CSF1R+ tumor-41 

associated macrophages and MPO+ infiltrating myeloid cells localized near regions of drug-42 

induced tumor cell death. Finally, we detect putative lipid-associated macrophage (LAM)-like 43 

populations within drug-resistant treatment regions. 44

# Main 45 

Metabolic conditioning of the tumor microenvironment (TME) is a central determinant of immune cell 46 

function and contributes to therapeutic resistance 1. Immune cells are disrupted by the metabolic TME 47 

through two mechanistically distinct axes: direct fate-altering signals that repolarize innate immune cells 48 

toward pro-tumor states, and nutrient competition that drives functional exhaustion. Tumors accumulate 49 

immunomodulatory metabolites that suppress antigen presentation and cytotoxic effector programs via 50 

acidosis‑driven lactate signaling 2,3 , adenosine–A2A receptor signaling 4,5 , and the kynurenine–AHR axis 6–8 .51 

In parallel, nutrient competition between tumor cells and immune cells constrains T‑cell glycolysis and 52 

IFN‑γ production in vivo, directly linking tumor bioenergetics to T‑cell dysfunction 9–11 .53 

Resolving immunometabolic states within intact tissue remains challenging. Bulk metabolomic assays 54 

require large cell numbers and average signals across heterogeneous microanatomical niches. In addition, 55 

cell isolation and fluorescence-activated cell sorting perturb cellular metabolism and introduce 56 

metabolomic artifacts 12–14 . Finally, metabolic phenotypes observed in standard culture conditions often 57 

diverge from in vivo behavior, even when partially mitigated by physiologic media formulations 15,16 .58 

Recent efforts to overcome these limitations using in situ or in vivo spatial metabolite imaging have 59 

provided valuable insights but studies either lack the paired proteomic context necessary for immune cell-60 

type distinction 17,18 , or are limited in spatial and molecular coverage to capture the diversity of 61 

immunomodulatory metabolism 19–21 . Critically, no prior study has resolved immune cell–type–specific 62 

metabolic remodeling across defined therapeutic perturbations within intact tumors. 63 

To investigate immune phenotypes in regions of therapeutic response and resistance, we pair intratumoral 64 

microdelivery of therapeutics with high-dimensional MALDI-MSI to obtain spatially resolved readouts of 65 

tumor microenvironment remodeling adjacent to each drug reservoir. This multiplex implantable 66 

microdevice (IMD) assay (MIMA) resolves treatment-specific immune and stromal signatures and has 67 

previously predicted rational drug combinations in vivo 22,23 and in patients 24–26 . We selected perturbations 68 

spanning distinct immune response and those with highest tumor killing capacity based on prior MIMA 69 

readouts. For example, Venetoclax recruits immature myeloid populations; Panobinostat promotes 70 

immunogenic cell death and increases antigen-presenting myeloid activity 22,27,28 ; the combination 71 

(panobinostat/venetoclax, PV) shows the strongest in vivo efficacy (Xu et al. , under review). 72 

We applied this in the MMTV-PyMT genetically engineered model of luminal-like breast cancer, which 73 

develops in an immunocompetent setting with macrophage-rich tumor microenvironments characteristic of 74 

human disease 29,30 . This enables spatially resolved, causally interpretable assessment of how pharmacologic 75

# Results 82 

An implantable microdevice enables controlled, multiplexed drug perturbation in intact tumors. 83 

We deployed an implantable microdevice (IMD) 31,22,24,25 bearing 18 spatially segregated release 84 

sites to perturb intratumoral regions within single tumors (Fig. 1a). Small-molecule agents were 85 

formulated in poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) for precise intratumoral release and loaded to 86 

approximate tissue-level exposures corresponding to systemic dosing (Methods). PEG-only 87 

control wells and untreated intratumoral regions served as local controls. IMD placement and 88 

retrieval were well tolerated by animals bearing orthotopic breast tumors, with the architecture 89 

surrounding release sites preserved on hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) sections, allowing 90 

downstream multi-omic profiling without tissue dissociation (Fig. 1b). 91 

Following a three-day exposure interval, tissues adjacent to each release site were sectioned 92 

serially for cyclic immunofluorescence (CycIF) 22 and matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization 93 

imaging mass spectrometry (MALDI-IMS), with an intervening H&E reference (Fig. 1b). We 94 

registered the modalities by thin-plate spline (TPS) 32 warping using conserved histologic features 95 

as landmarks, yielding a common coordinate framework for metabolites and single-cell 96 

phenotypes. TPS significantly outperformed alternative rigid and affine registration methods for 97 

both within-modality serial-section registration and cross-modality alignment of CyCIF-derived 98 

cell death regions with histologically labeled regions (paired t-test, P < 0.01; Extended Data Figs. 99 

1, 2). 100 

CycIF identifies drug-recruited immune cellular neighborhoods in situ. 101 

We profiled 20 sections across eight treatment programs with a validated 8-antibody CycIF panel 102 

for cell type identification (Fig 1c, Extended Data Fig. 3a). 14 sections were additionally imaged 103 

with an expanded 9-antibody panel to refine cell-state annotation 22,33 . After preprocessing, nuclei 104 

were segmented and single-cell intensities quantified. Marker positivity was gated using Gaussian 105 

mixture-modeling to minimize operator bias while accommodating non-Gaussian tails (Methods). 106 

This strategy is consistent with model-based gating in cytometry and multiplexed imaging 34,35 .107 

Adhering to canonical patterns of marker expression, we were able to label 1.48 million cells with 108 

cell type identities (Extended Data Fig. 3b). 109 

Spatial neighborhood analysis summarizes recurrent patterns of local cell type organization 36–38 .110

MALDI-IMS resolves spatially structured metabolic programs across tumor compartments. 119 

To determine the metabolic profiles of different immune neighborhoods, we performed spatial 120 

metabolomic profiling on 27 MMTV-PyMT tumor sections using MALDI‑IMS. After 121 

preprocessing, the dataset comprised 230,812 spots, which were normalized by total ion current 122 

(TIC), log-transformed, and z-scored across each metabolite (Methods). 84 high-quality small 123 

molecule m/z features were detected across all sections and retained for downstream analysis. 124 

Putative metabolite identities were assigned using high-precision matching of mass-to-charge 125 

ratios; orthogonal confirmation is provided where indicated in Methods (Extended Data Fig. 4). 126 

Six identified lipid signatures were labeled by empirical chemical formula. Isobaric species (e.g., 127 

citrate and isocitrate) are indistinguishable by MALDI-IMS and were therefore reported as a single 128 

peak. 129 

Tissue sections were organized by coordinated shifts in groups of co-abundant metabolites. To 130 

quantify spatial coupling patterns between metabolites, we constructed a spatial correlation 131 

network (Methods). This network resolved three robust spatially correlated metabolic programs, 132 

defined by correlations in the top decile of Bivariate Moran I statistics that were mutually 133 

conserved across all mouse replicates: (i) glutamine-nucleotide metabolic coupling, (ii) lipid 134 

metabolism, and (iii) purine catabolism. In addition, spatial anticorrelations between palmitic acid 135 

and both taurine and glutamine were conserved across all replicates. 136 

To interpret these metabolic programs, we examined metabolite abundance across tumor 137 

compartments (Extended Fig. 5a). A board-certified pathologist (SA) annotated the MALDI-IMS 138 

slide to delineate tumor, stroma, muscle, necrotic, and cystic regions. Registration of H&E-based 139 

tissue annotations to the MALDI-IMS coordinate frame uncovered pronounced compartmental 140 

differences in program abundance (Fig. 2a). 141

The largest component displayed a dual community structure with central hubs glutamine and 142 

glutamate. The two poles of this program show clear separation between tumor and stroma: the 143 

tumor pole reflects a proliferative metabolic program with features consistent with nucleotide 144 

biosynthesis/turnover, signaling intermediates, and antioxidant defenses; the stromal pole suggests 145 

catabolic/tissue-remodeling, enriched for nucleotide degradation products (e.g., inosine, 146 

hypoxanthine, xanthine, uridine), TCA intermediates (citrate/isocitrate, aconitate), amino-acid-147 

linked metabolites (aspartate, N-acetylaspartate), and membrane-precursor turnover (glycerol-3-148 

phosphate, PEA). In the adjacent muscle, elevated carnosine, anserine, and taurine recapitulated 149 

expected skeletal muscle signatures 39,40 . From this data, we speculate regional differences in 150 

glutamine utilization between metabolically active stromal and tumor areas, consistent with 151 

metabolic symbiosis between compartments 11,41 .152 

A second component indexed a lipid metabolic program centered on fatty-acid acquisition and 153 

remodeling in tumor areas. This program encompassed saturated and monounsaturated fatty acids, 154 

which are compatible with membrane biogenesis and energy storage, as well as polyunsaturated 155 

fatty acids, including arachidonic acid (AA), eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA), and docosahexaenoic 156 

acid (DHA). The presence of these polyunsaturated lipids suggests capacity for lipid-derived 157 

signaling (e.g., prostaglandins, resolvins) with potential consequences for inflammation and 158 

immune modulation in the local microenvironment 42–44 .159 

A third, minor metabolic component was enriched in apoptotic/necrotic regions and characterized 160 

by purine degradation products (guanine, adenine, and uric acid) along with an oxidative glucose 161 

by-product (D-gluconate), consistent with nucleic acid breakdown and a more oxidizing 162 

microenvironment 45 .163

profile (Fig 2b,c). We performed principal component analysis (PCA) on normalized metabolite 165 

intensities and batch-corrected the resulting embeddings using Harmony 46 to align samples in 166 

latent space, preserving shared biological structure while reducing inter-mouse technical variation 167 

(Extended Fig. 5b,c). We then performed Leiden clustering 47 to the corrected principal component 168 

scores, with silhouette analysis identifying 11 clusters as the optimal solution (Extended Fig. 5d,e). 169 

Focusing on the eight most abundant clusters (>1% of all spots), we annotated each based on its 170 

molecular class composition and enrichment of canonical metabolic pathways (Fig. 2d,e). 171

Spatial metabolic state predicts immune cellular neighborhoods across imaging modalities. 172 

We tested whether these metabolic profiles encoded reproducible information across modalities 173 

using a cross-modal classifier (Fig. 3a). To enable robust cross-modality classification, we 174 

restricted analysis to high-quality regions exhibiting accurate correspondence across all three 175 

modalities (MALDI-IMS, CycIF, and H&E) (Methods). 176 

As a positive control for cross-modality predictive performance, we trained a logistic regression 177 

classifier to predict pathologist-annotated histological regions from the H&E section 178 

corresponding to the MALDI data (Fig. 3b). Models were trained with class balancing and 179 

evaluated using leave-one-section-out cross-validation across 20 tissue sections to prevent 180 

overfitting from shared spatial patterns. The model accurately discriminated major histological 181 

compartments, with highest performance in muscle (ROC–AUC = 0.98, accuracy = 97%) and 182 

lowest in tumor (ROC–AUC = 0.88, accuracy = 81%), yielding a mean AUC of 0.93 across 183 

regions. These results demonstrate that metabolic profiles alone robustly capture canonical 184 

histological structure across modalities. 185 

Metabolic abundance was sufficient to predict immune cell proteomic phenotypes on a serial 186 

section (Fig. 3c,d). We trained a logistic regression classifier to predict the dominant CycIF 187 

neighborhood of each MALDI spot (Extended Data Fig. 6a,b). Using similar evaluation, the model 188 

most accurately classified myeloid-enriched neighborhoods, with the CycIF immune 189 

neighborhoods Pan-myeloid, Macrophage, and Non-macrophage averaging ROC-AUC 0.76 and 190 

accuracy 76%. By contrast, the metabolically heterogeneous CycIF Tumor neighborhood was less 191 

readily identified (ROC–AUC = 0.66, accuracy = 63%), highlighting the robustness of the 192 

identified immune-metabolic coupling (Extended Data Fig. 6c,d). 193

Effective drug treatment reorganizes the local immunometabolic landscape. 282

distance from the drug release site (Methods; Extended Fig. 10a,b). Venetoclax induced a strong 284 

but inconsistent immune cell recruitment proximal to the device, with immune cells comprising 285 

>30% of all cells within 500 μm across three experiments. In contrast, the Panobinostat/Venetoclax 286

induced by Venetoclax and PV treatment, subsequent analyses focused on these conditions. 289

# Discussion 303 

This study establishes an in situ pharmacology framework that links localized drug exposure to 304 

immune-cell states and metabolic organization within intact tumors. By coupling an implantable 305 

microdevice with cyclic immunofluorescence (CycIF), MALDI-imaging mass spectrometry 306 

(MALDI-IMS), and thin-plate-spline registration, we resolve agent-specific immune phenotypes 307 

alongside three reproducible metabolic programs—a tumor-biased proliferative axis, a stromal 308 

catabolic/tissue-remodeling axis, and a purine-rich dying cell program—with additional lipid-309 

remodeling features. The venetoclax-proximal increase in myeloid populations, and the PV 310 

combination therapy adjacent enrichment for tumor cell death and neutrophil infiltration together 311 

indicate that short-range drug exposure is sufficient to reorganize the local immune ecology. 312 

Importantly, these cellular shifts occur within distinct chemical neighborhoods defined by 313 

MALDI-IMS, positioning immunometabolism as both a readout and a target for rational 314 

combinations. 315 

The PUFA-enriched, CSF1R-high, CD206-high, MPO-low macrophage niche identified in our 316 

spatial metabolomic analysis is consistent with the lipid-associated macrophage (LAM) state 317 

described as an immunosuppressive TAM population in breast cancer. Prior studies have linked 318 

LAMs to fatty acid metabolism, TREM2 signaling, immune suppression, and resistance to immune 319 

checkpoint blockade 51–53 . Our findings extend this biology in two ways. First, we provide direct 320 

spatial metabolomic characterization of a LAM-like niche in intact mammary tumor tissue, 321 

demonstrating enrichment of long-chain PUFAs — including arachidonic acid, DHA, and EPA — 322 

within CSF1R-high macrophage regions. Second, through pharmacologic perturbation, we show 323 

that Venetoclax selectively enriches this PUFA-dominant macrophage state, whereas the PV 324 

combination instead promotes an opposing purine-catabolic, MPO-active immunogenic program, 325 

suggesting that persistence of a lipid-handling macrophage niche may contribute to Venetoclax 326 

resistance. 327 

Fatty acids DHA, EPA, and linoleic acid (LA) were all uniquely enriched in MALDI immune 328 

stroma proximal clusters, but not immune enriched MALDI immune cell death clusters. Despite 329 

growing interest in supplementing chemotherapies with dietary omega-3 fatty acids, the 330

Cell Segmentation 625 

Cell nuclei segmentation was performed on the CycIF round 1 DAPI stain using the CellProfiler 66 (v4.26) 626 

module IdentifyPrimaryObject with expected nuclei radius 4um to 12um. To improve segmentation, the 627 

DAPI image was altered for more accurate segmentation. First, the DAPI image was sharpened with two 628 

convolutional filtering steps. A Gaussian filter with kernel size 5uM was subtracted from the image. A 629 

Gaussian filter finds the spatially weighted average of kernel. Subtracting the average background of the 630 

image normalizes intensity so that there are no bright outlier regions. Next, the Laplacian of the Gaussian 631 

(LoG) of the image was subtracted. The Laplacian of the gaussian is a second order derivative operator 632 

which identifies rapid change in intensity from one pixel to its surrounding pixels (i.e. edge detection). 633 

Subtracting identified edges improves watershed segmentation by distinguishing individual cell boundaries. 634 

After image correction, the DAPI images were input to the CellProfiler pipeline. 635

above the second component. This approach follows established GMM-based gating methods used in 652
