# Abstract 

Spatial biology has entered a new era of multimodal profiling, with multiple, high-dimensional spatial omics types being measured on consecutive tissue slices, or co-assayed on the same slice. Interest then lies in statistical testing for spatial association between the features of the different modalities, to gain insight in biological processes. One major challenge is the multitude of bivariate combinations, leading to high computational demands. Another difficulty is the difference in spatial resolution between technologies, implying no one-to-one matching between the measurement spots of the two modalities, even after alignment. As a result, common statistical measures such as joint distributions and correlations are not defined, and tests need to rely on spatial vicinity only. Moreover, we argue that many existing bivariate association tests address an inappropriate null hypothesis, or make inappropriate assumptions, both implying absence of spatial autocorrelation in any of the features and leading to misleading conclusions. As a remedy, we modify tests for the detection of spatially variable genes (Moran’s I, Gaussian processes and generalized additive models (splines)) to derive bivariate tests across modalities with non-overlapping coordinate sets and provide variance estimators that do account for spatial autocorrelation. We develop inference methods for single sections as well as for replicated experiments with multiple sections, and compare their performance in nonparametric and parametric simulations. Finally, we apply the newly developed methods to two co-assayed spatial transcriptomics and metabolomics datasets from mouse and human. The full suite of tests is available from github.com/sthawinke/sbivar as the R-package sbivar .

# Author summary 

Spatial biology investigates molecules and cells in their spatial context in living tissues. For this purpose, recent technologies allow to detect different classes of biomolecules on the same or adjacent tissue sections. In this work, we focus on methods to detect colocalization of molecule pairs, which may provide clues on their biological function, e.g. involvement in the same pathways. Many existing statistical tests for identifying such colocalized pairs enforce spot-to-spot matching between tissue sections, thus failing to exploit the full spatial resolution of the data. # Introduction 1

Current biotechnology permits to study the molecular composition of biological tissue at 2

cellular resolution, yielding insights in the spatial organization of living tissues [1, 2]. 3

Yet each assay targets a single class of biomolecules, e.g. RNA, proteins or metabolites, 4

limiting our view on the entire biological processes. Therefore, different spatial omics 5

technologies are increasingly being applied to the same tissue, either on consecutive 6

slices [3, 4], or by measuring the different molecule types or modalities consecutively on 7

the same slice (co-assaying) [5–9]. Although single molecules can be identified using 8

hybridization [10], here we focus on technologies that quantify local molecular 9

composition on a predefined grid of spots using e.g. sequencing with Visium or mass 10 

spectroscopy with MALDI-MSI. A logical scientific aim is then to discover statistical 11 

associations between the different classes of biomolecules. A pivotal first step herein is 12 

to align the different images, whether taken on the same or consecutive slices, onto a 13 

common coordinate framework (CCF) to make corresponding regions of the original 14 

tissue overlap. This alignment problem is mostly solved manually, sometimes with the 15 

help of H&E stainings of the different images [3, 9, 11]. Given a CCF, the relative 16 

positions of the spots of the different modalities are known, allowing the two modalities 17 

to be analysed jointly. In this work, we focus on bivariate spatial association tests 18 

across modalities. They pose a unique analysis problem, as even after alignment there is 19 

no one-to-one mapping between the spots from different modalities, i.e. the coordinate 20 

sets are disjoint, due to differences in resolution and measurement area between the 21 

technologies capturing the different modalities. As a result, common statistical 22 

quantities such as correlations and joint distributions are undefined. Current practice is 23 

therefore to aggregate the spots of the modality with the best resolution and pair them 24 

with spots of the other modality to remove the disjointness [3, 8, 9], but this results in 25 

information loss. An additional challenge is computational, as the number of bivariate 26 

tests greatly exceeds the number of univariate tests applied to each modality separately. 27 

Finally, the null hypothesis should be chosen carefully. Analytical null distributions 28 

have been derived for bivariate versions of Moran’s I [12, 13] and Geary’s C [14], and for 29 

Lee’s L [15] for the case of joint coordinate sets under the randomization null 30 

hypothesis. This hypothesis means that each pair of observations (x,y) is equally likely 31 

to occur at any location, such that the corresponding null distribution can be 32 

approximated by permuting the measurement locations, as in some software 33 

implementations [16 –18]. Yet this randomization null hypothesis also implies an absence 34 

of spatial patterning or spatial autocorrelation (SAC) in any of the two variables 35 

involved in the test. Hence its rejection indicates either bivariate spatial association, or 36 

SAC in one of both variables, or any combination of the above. More formally, for two 37 

variables x and y the randomization null hypothesis entails all of the following: 38

permutation tests (e.g. in SpaGene [18]), as they assume exchangeability of 44 

observations [20], which is violated by SAC. 45 

We argue that it is preferable to have separate tests for univariate spatial patterning 46 

only, and for bivariate spatial association only. Many good tests for the former exist 47 

already, in particular for detecting spatially variable genes (SVGs) in transcriptomics, 48 

based on e.g. univariate Moran’s I ( MERINGUE by [16]), Gaussian processes ( SPARK 49 

by [21] and SpatialDE2 by [22]), generalized additive models ( spVC by [23]) and 50 

nonparametric tests for association between distance and covariance ( SPARK-X 51 

by [24]). For that reason, we focus on tests for bivariate association only, which need to 52 

accommodate for SAC in some way. We call the associated null hypothesis the 53 

independence null hypothesis, entailing only the first condition in Eq. 1. The modified 54 

t-test for the Pearson correlation coefficient tests this null hypothesis by estimating 55 

SAC for every variable separately and decreasing the degrees of freedom in the t-test 56 

accordingly [25 –27], but is only applicable to joint coordinate sets. Custom permutation 57 

schemes have been developed that leave the SAC intact and thus test the independence 58 

null hypothesis for any test statistic: the so-called ”random-shift” tests [28, 29]. Yet 59 

such tests can be computationally demanding and their p-values are discretized by the 60 

number of permutations, which can result in low power after multiple testing correction. 61 

The SpaceBF method explicitly accounts for SAC in a Bayesian framework [30], but 62 

relies on Markov chain Monte-Carlo (MCMC) sampling which may be too slow for large 63 

numbers of bivariate combinations. 64 

In this work, we evaluate existing methods for detection of bivariate spatial 65 

association using nonparametric and parametric simulations, and develop scalable new 66 

statistical tests for the independence null hypothesis of bivariate association across 67 

disjoint coordinates, available in the R-package sbivar from 68 

github.com/sthawinke/sbivar . We apply the new tests to spatial multi-omics 69 

datasets from mouse [9] and human [ ?] tissues. 70

Next we extend the methods described for single-image analysis to datasets with 176 

multiple images, e.g. tissue slices from different biological replicates [3, 9]. For the 177 

model based methods (bivariate GPs and GAMs), the ideal approach would 178 

accommodate all images in a single model, but this solution is computationally 179 

demanding and requires many additional assumptions on how e.g. spatial covariance 180 

parameters are allowed to vary across replicates. Here we take the pragmatic approach 181 

of extracting a single measure of association strength from each image, and using it as 182 

outcome in a linear model accounting for the design structure [39, 40]. This implies that 183 

variability is quantified across replicates rather than within a single image. Nevertheless 184 

it could be beneficial to inverse weigh the extracted association measure, bivariate 185 

Moran’s I or the correlation of evaluated spline surfaces ρxy , by their estimated variance 186 

in the linear model to improve the power. The bivariate Moran’s could also be scaled by 187 

its maximum absolute value for comparability. For the bivariate GPs, no measure of 188 

association strength is estimated, only a test statistic and corresponding p-value, so we 189 

do not use it for replicated analysis. 190

# Discussion 407 

It is long known that statistical tests should account for dependence between 408 

observations, e.g. autocorrelation in spatial or temporal data, to achieve valid statistical 409 

inference. Yet most published tests for bivariate association in spatial omics data ignore 410 

this principle. Pearson correlation is a workhorse measure often applied to test for 411 

bivariate spatial association, but standard formulae for its variance are invalid in 412 

presence of spatial autocorrelation (SAC). For other measures of bivariate spatial 413 

association, such as bivariate Moran’s I, bivariate Geary’s C and Lee’s L, exact null 414 

distributions have been derived under the randomization null hypothesis that also 415 

implies absence of spatial autocorrelation in any variable. Also naive permutation 416 

procedures, that indiscriminately scramble observations over measurement locations, 417 

test this omnibus null hypothesis, falsely suggesting bivariate association where only 418 

univariate spatial patterns are being picked up. 419 

Instead, bivariate association tests are needed that allow for SAC in both variables, 420 

while being sensitive only to true bivariate spatial association. The modified t-test 421 

accounts for the variance inflation of the Pearson correlation estimator under SAC, but 422 

relies on one-to-one spot matching and ignores neighbouring spots, which can lead to 423 

low power. Bivariate Moran’s I is an intuitive, nonparametric measure of spatial 424 

association that takes neighbourhood information into account and naturally generalizes 425 

to disjoint coordinate sets. Here we derive an analytical expression for its variance 426 

accounting for SAC, thus achieving good power against a range of bivariate spatial 427 

patterns while controlling type I error rate. Another method we describe relies on 428 

bivariate Gaussian processes (GPs), that model covariance between observations as a 429

function of distance, and as such naturally allow for disjoint coordinate sets. Yet they 430 

are mathematically and computationally demanding, scaling poorly with the number of 431 

spots despite past optimization efforts and GPU acceleration. Moreover, they provide 432 

no quantitative measure of association strength, only a p-value. A third method we 433 

introduced uses generalized additive models (GAMs) to describe univariate spatial 434 

patterns using splines as a function of space. They overcome the disjointness by 435 

evaluating the two spline surfaces in the same set of points and finding their correlation, 436 

along with its variance by propagating the uncertainty on the spline coefficients. The 437 

resulting correlation estimate is easy to interpret and visualize. Hence all valid inference 438 

methods work in similar fashion: first univariate spatial structures are estimated, and 439 

only then can bivariate association tests be performed. The test statistics of bivariate 440 

Moran’s I and GPs are calculated based on the full data and covariance matrices, 441 

resulting in memory and computing demands growing quadratically with the number of 442 

spots. These demands are exacerbated by the fact that for these two methods, the 443 

range of the spatial interaction is not estimated from the data, necessitating repeated 444 

testing at a prespecified set of ranges to achieve good power against all potential spatial 445 

patterns. GAMs, on the other hand, are adaptive to the scale of spatial patterns in the 446 

data and achieve a dimension reduction by evaluating the univariate spline surfaces on a 447 

fixed, smaller set of points, scaling better with the number of spots. Because of its 448 

nonparametric nature, bivariate Moran’s I struggles with heteroskedasticity as 449 

encountered in many omics data types, as is known for univariate Moran’s I [65, 66], 450 

whereas bivariate GPs and GAMs can account for heteroskedasticity when augmented 451 

with appropriate outcome distributions. 452 

As spatial omics technologies mature, replicated datasets consisting of multiple 453 

images of comparable tissue will become increasingly available. Since the principled 454 

approach of analysing all images through a single model may be computationally 455 

prohibitive, we propose to plug association measures calculated from single images into 456 

a linear model accounting for the experimental design as a pragmatic alternative. Since 457 

this quantifies variability across images and thus absorbs additional variability due to 458 

SAC, quantifying the variance of the image-wise estimates is unnecessary in this case. 459 

Yet it is important to render the estimates comparable before feeding them to the linear 460 

model, i.e. scaling the bivariate Moran’s I to its maximum value, which depends on the 461 

measurement grid. 462 

We demonstrated that many existing tests fail to control type I error in absence of 463 

bivariate spatial association. Yet comparing methods in term of power is harder as 464 

infinitely many types of bivariate spatial association exist, and additional, independent 465 

benchmarks are needed. Based on the results in this study we recommend applying 466 

both bivariate Moran’s I and GAMs and inspect the results visually (see Table S2 in S1 467 

Text for an overview of strengths and weaknesses). We have wrapped a suite of valid 468 

tests in the R-package sbivar available from github.com/sthawinke/sbivar . 469
