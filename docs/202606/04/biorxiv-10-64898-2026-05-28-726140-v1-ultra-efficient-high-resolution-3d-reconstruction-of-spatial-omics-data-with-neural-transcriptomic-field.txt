## Abstract 16 

Biological tissues are inherently three -dimensional (3D) ecosystems where spatial architecture 17 

dictates cellular function. While spatial omics technologies have revolutionized molecular 18 

profiling, they are largely restricted to isolated two -dimensional (2D ) tissue sections. Existing 19 

comput ational methods attempting to reconstruct 3D volumes from sparse slices rely heavily 20 

on local slice -to -slice interpolation, struggling to balance high -fidelity reconstruction, noise 21 

reduction, and atlas -scale efficiency. Here, we present Neural Transcripto mic Field (NTF), a 22 

deep learning framework employing multi -resolution hash -grid encoding and implicit neural 23 

representations. Unlike interpolation -based approaches that merely bridge adjacent 24 

observations, NTF learns a global, continuous 3D representation of the tissue. By modeling the 25 

underlying latent biological patterns, NTF intrinsically decouples true molecular signals from 26 

technical artifacts, naturally enabling robust denoising and high -fidelity reconstructions. This 27 

global field paradigm shatters tr aditional scalability limits: NTF achieves up to a 1,000× 28 

speedup over existing methods, notably reconstructing a 100 -million -cell scale 3D whole -29 

mouse embryo atlas in under 15 minutes. Furthermore , NTF can genera te super -resolved 30 

volumes from spars e input (e.g., utilizing only 10% of slices) and robustly extrapolating into 31 

unseen tissue regions. We demonstrate NTF’s versatility across diverse transcriptomic and 32 

proteomic datasets, capturing complex spatiotemporal dynamics in Drosophila and mouse 33 

embryogene sis, and mapping intra -tumoral functional gradients in human breast cancer. 34 

Ultimately, NTF provides an unprecedentedly fast, scalable, and robust computational engine 35 

for constructing the next generation of comprehensive 3D tissue atlases. 36

## Introduction 37 

Spatial omics technologies have transformed the study of tissue architecture by measuring 38 

molecular states while preserving spatial context  1-5. From the origin al spatial transcriptomics 39 

array design to modern sequencing -based and imaging -based platforms, the field has rapidly 40 

expanded in resolution, throughput and modality  2-6. This growth has created a pressing demand 41 

for computational methods that can move beyond descriptive two -dimensional (2D) analysis 42 

and recover spatial organization in three dimensions, where developmental gradients, laminar 43 

structures, invasive tumor fronts and long -range tissue interactions are often most apparent. 44 

True three -dimensional (3D) measurements are increasingly f easible, but they are still not 45 

routine across tissues, modalities and scales. Experimental advances such as STARmap, Open -46 

ST and Deep -STARmap show that direct 3D profiling can reveal spatial structures that are 47 

inaccessible in isolated 2D sections  7-9. Even so, thick -tissue measurements remain technically 48 

demanding, technology -specific and often constrained by panel size, throughput, tissue 49 

hand ling or cost  3-5,7-9. As a result, most practical studies still rely on serial or sparsely sampled 50 

planar sections and require computational reconstruction to recover missing spatial context. 51 

A broad ecosystem of compu tational methods has emerged for this purpose  10 . One class 52 

of methods focuses on alignment and integration of adjacent sections, including PASTE, 53 

PASTE2, STalign, GPSA and STAligner  11 -15 . A second class aims at explicit 3D reconstruction 54 

or atlas building from serial slices, exemplified by SpatialZ and the recent FEAST framework 55 

> 16 ,17

. These methods have substantially advanced the field, but they also expose a persistent 56 

trade -off. Alignment -centered approaches are effective for establishing correspondence across 57 

slices, yet they do not by themselves provide a continuous high -resolution representation. 58 

Reconstruction methods based on virtual -slice synthesis can bridge missing planes, but they 59 

are typically driven by local interpolation between observed sections, m ay inherit observed 60 

noise, and can become computationally heavy when applied to atlas -scale data  16 ,17 . With the 61 

advent of high -resolution platforms like Xenium and Visium HD , a single 2D slice routinely 62 

captures hundreds of thousands of spatial elements  18 -20 . Consequently, even a sparse set of 63 

input sections easily aggregates to millions of data points, and their subsequent high -resolution 64

To overcome the fundamental limitations of local slice -to -slice interpolation and meet the 68

NTF is a unified deep learning framework to reconstruct high -fidelity 3D spatial omics 70

the unique c omputational and biological complexities of spatial omics through a series of 72

local slice interpolation and enable continuous volume generation, we conceptu alize the tissue 74

Radius Filed, NeRF)  21 -23 , NTF learns a continuous coordinate -to -feature mapping, providing 76 

a biologically meaningful mathematical representation of the entire tissue architecture. Se cond, 77 

to address the massive computational bottleneck of atlas -scale data, NTF employs a78 

multiresolution hash -encoded architecture. This highly optimized data structure bypasses the 79 

heavy overhead of traditional volumetric rendering, allowing the model to process tens of 80 

millions of spatial coordinates with unprecedented speed. Third, to systematically decouple 81 

true biological signals from the inherited measurement artifacts prevalent in existing methods, 82 

NTF introduces a suite of specialized biological mod ules. It utilizes learnable section -specific 83 

embeddings to dynamically align independent spatial planes and correct batch effects; it 84 

explicitly models zero -inflation to address transcriptomic dropouts; and it captures cell -level 85 

variation to absorb hetero scedastic technical noise. Finally, to accurately reflect the physical 86 

limits of spatial assays, we integrate a Point Spread Function (PSF) -aware spatial sampling 87 

strategy that models finite acquisition blur. 88 

By capturing the underlying biological reality rather than raw observational noise, we 89 

show that NTF combines high reconstruction fidelity with unprecedented computational 90 

efficiency. Not only does it achieve up to a 1,000× speedup compared to existing methods, but 91 

it also maintains a remarkably lightw eight hardware footprint, consuming less than 10 GB of 92

Overview of the NTF model 114 

NTF conceptualizes spatial omics measurements as discrete samples drawn from an 115 

underlying continuous 3D molecular field (Fig. 1a, Methods). Within this framework, each 116 

spatial observation is defined by its 3D coordinate, its measured molecular profile, an d its 117 

section identity. To process this data, spatial coordinates are first passed through a118 

multiresolution hash -grid encoding, which is subsequently decoded by a lightweight multi -119 

layer perceptron (MLP) to predict expression values at arbitrary spatial locations. In 120 

fundamental contrast to virtual -slice generation pipelines that explicitly synthesize discrete 121

trained, this single, compact representation can be q ueried at any point in continuous space and 123 

rendered at any desired output resolution. 124 

Beyond this core continuous representation, NTF is natively engineered to decouple true 125 

biological signals from the profound technical confounders inherent in spatial as says (Fig. 1b). 126 

First, to systematically resolve the prevalent issue of zero -inflation in spatial transcriptomics, 127 

NTF features a dedicated dropout module that explicitly predicts the probability of a gene being 128 

undetected. Second, to account for uneven ca pture efficiency and technical noise across serial 129 

tissue sections, the architecture introduces learnable slice -specific variables: a bias component 130 

to correct for batch effects (section -dependent intensity shifts), and a variance module to absorb 131 

cell -lev el heteroscedastic measurement noise. Finally, to accurately reflect the physical 132 

limitations of spatial capture technologies (e.g., spots spanning multiple cells), NTF explicitly 133 

models the Point Spread Function (PSF). Rather than treating each coordinate as a134 

dimensionless perfect point, the model aggregates local 3D neighborhood signals to 135 

approximate the true physical capture volume before computing the loss against the observed 136 

2D measurements (Fig. 1a). 137

NTF achieves reconstruction fidelity with atlas -scale computational efficiency 185 

We first evaluated NTF on simulated data across increasing spatial scales, comparing its 186 

performance against SpatialZ and FEAST , two recent methods designed for 3D reconstruction 187 

from plana r spatial data  16 ,17 .. Across all tested scales, NTF consistently achieved the lowest 188 

root -mean -square error (RMSE) alongside the highest 3D structural similarity index (SSIM) 189 

and Spearman correlation (Fig. 2a , Supplementary Table 1 ). While SpatialZ served as the 190 

strongest numerical baseline, it remained slightly inferior to NTF, whereas FEAST exhibited 191 

lower overall reconstruction accuracy. These findings suggest that learning a continuous 192 

coordinate -based field is more effective at recovering the true latent spatial signal than relying 193 

on explicit slice interpolation. 194 

We next assessed computational scalability . When benchmarked on a single 40 GB A100 195 

GPU, SpatialZ required over 24 hours to process a dataset of 1 million cells, prohibiting its 196 

application to la rger scales. FEAST reduced runtime relative to SpatialZ but did not match the 197 

reconstruction accuracy of NTF . In stark contrast, NTF successfully reconstructed a 10 -million -198 

cell dataset in under 7 minutes (Fig. 2a). These results demonstrat e t hat NTF can maintain 199 

reconstruction accuracy while scaling to atlas -scale spatial datasets with practical runtimes. 200
