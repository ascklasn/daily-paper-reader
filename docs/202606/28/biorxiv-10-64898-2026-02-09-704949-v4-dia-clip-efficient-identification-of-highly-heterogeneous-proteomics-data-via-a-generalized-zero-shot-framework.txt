# Abstract 23 

Data-independent acquisition mass spectrometry has increasingly emerged as a cornerstone for 24 

characterizing highly heterogeneous biological systems, such as single-cell proteomics, metaproteomics, 25 

and spatial proteomics, offering unparalleled identification depth and quantification reproducibility. 26 

Current DIA analysis frameworks, however, require semi-supervised training within each run for peptide-27 

spectrum match (PSM) re-scoring, which is prone to overfitting and lacks generalizability across diverse 28 

species and experimental conditions. Here, we present πDIA-CLIP, a generalized framework shifting the 29 

DIA analysis strategy from semi-supervised training to zero-shot cross-modal representation learning 30 

through integrating dual-encoder contrastive learning and encoder-decoder architectures to establish a 31 

unified, high-precision representation for spectral features and peptides. Notably, the generalized zero-32 

shot nature of πDIA-CLIP facilitates an inference-only architecture, streamlining the analysis to achieve 33 

exceptional computational efficiency. Extensive evaluations across five distinct benchmarks demonstrate 34 

that πDIA-CLIP consistently outperforms existing tools, yielding an up to 44.6% increase in protein 35 

identification alongside a reduction in entrapment identifications reaching a maximal 52.5%. Furthermore, 36 

the enhanced identification depth facilitates the discovery of novel biomarkers and the elucidation of 37 

intricate cellular mechanisms. 38 

# Introduction 39 

Driven by the burgeoning demands of precision diagnostics and therapeutic development, proteomics has 40 

transitioned from a specialized technology to a high-throughput engine for biological discovery and 41 

clinical translation 1,2 . The ability to comprehensively characterize the proteome within highly 42 

heterogeneous biological contexts is now pivotal for identifying diagnostic biomarkers, monitoring 43 

treatment responses, and elucidating complex biological mechanisms 3-6 . To meet these rigorous 44 

requirements for depth and scalability, data-independent acquisition mass spectrometry (DIA-MS) has 45 

emerged as a cornerstone of high-throughput proteomics 7-10 , lauded for its high reproducibility 11 ,46 

sensitivity 12,13 , and high-throughput 14 . However, fully realizing the potential of DIA-MS is often 47 

hampered by the inherent complexity of its signal deconvolution, which remains a preeminent challenge 48 

in computational proteomics 15,16 . Unlike data-dependent acquisition (DDA) modes, DIA-MS employs 49 

isolation windows designed to encompass multiple m/z ranges, resulting in the concurrent fragmentation 50 

of multiple co-eluting precursor ions. This multiplexing gives rise to highly convoluted spectra where 51 

fragment-ion signals from disparate peptides are extensively interleaved, thereby confounding the 52 

definitive assignment of peptide-spectrum matches (PSMs) and limiting the depth of proteomic 53 

coverage 7,17 .54

Over the past decade, MSFragger-DIA 18 , PECAN 19 , Spectronaut 20 , and MaxDIA 21 , have profoundly 55 

advanced the field of DIA proteomics, establishing the standard for reproducible proteome profiling. To 56 

analyze DIA-MS data, peptide-centric approaches rely on reference spectral libraries to query DIA-MS 57 

data with a standardized computational workflow: retention time (RT) calibration, extracted ion 58 

chromatogram (XIC) generation, peak-group scoring, PSM re-scoring, and false discovery rate (FDR) 59 

estimation. To distinguish true target peptides from decoy hits, earlier PSM re-scoring strategies relied on 60 

semi-supervised machine learning algorithms (e.g., Percolator 22 , mProphet 23 or XGBoost 24 ). These 61 

algorithms are predicated upon sophisticated feature engineering, which integrates diverse statistical 62 

metrics such as RT alignment, peak-shape correlation, and ionic intensity distributions to enhance 63 

identification confidence. However, the reliance on hand-crafted feature engineering inherently restricts 64 

the capacity to capture complex data nuances, thus compromising the sensitivity for identifying low-65 

abundance proteins within complex background noise. Deep learning-based approaches, such as 66 

AlphaDIA 25 , DreamDIA 26 , DIA-NN 27 , Alpha-XIC 28 and DIA-BERT 29 leverage deep neural networks to 67 

extract high-dimensional features from raw DIA-MS signals without manual intervention, bolstering 68 

PSMs re-scoring performance and delivering substantially deeper proteome coverage. 69 

Despite these advancements, existing frameworks still necessitate semi-supervised learning to repetitively 70 

optimize re-scoring models for each individual DIA-MS analysis run. By fundamentally assigning 71 

pseudo-labels to unannotated PSMs for iterative self-updating, this “run-specific” semi-supervised 72 

learning paradigm poses an inherent risk of overfitting. Specifically, biased initial annotations toward 73 

specific typologies drive the model into a detrimental, self-reinforcing loop, while uniform sampling 74 

strategies systematically marginalize rare species, inherently underfitting sparse, low-abundance targets. 75 

Beyond the risk of overfitting, the high computational cost required for repetitive optimization in semi-76 

supervised learning framework severely bottlenecks high-throughput data analysis. Moreover, 77 

contemporary scoring metrics analyze peak groups in isolation, failing to capture the semantic 78 

associations between amino acid sequence and spectral data. These limitations highlight a critical need for 79 

a multi-modal framework capable of generalized PSM identification within sparse, high-dimensional 80 

space in a zero-shot manner, thereby circumventing the temporal burden of semi-supervised learning 81 

paradigm. 82 

To circumvent these long-standing bottlenecks, we present πDIA-CLIP (Data-Independent Acquisition 83 

with Contrastive Learning Integrated Proteomics), a generalized, end-to-end architecture for zero-shot 84 

PSM re-scoring, where "π" stands for the π-HuB project, a global initiative for proteomics research 30 .85 

Diverging from traditional methodologies, πDIA-CLIP represents the first implementation of cross-modal 86 

contrastive learning in DIA-MS analysis, aligning discrete peptide sequences with corresponding 87

# Results 106 

# Architecture and Analysis Workflow of πDIA-CLIP 107 

πDIA-CLIP features a unified end-to-end architecture synergizing cross-modal contrastive learning 108 

framework 31 with a dual-architectural paradigm comprising both dual-encoder and encoder-decoder 109 

architecture (Supplementary Information Fig. S1). The design rationale centers on the functional 110 

decoupling of cross-modal alignment and high-dimensional feature refinement. Specifically, the dual-111 

encoder component utilizes a transformer-based sequence encoder and a specialized spectral encoder to 112 

project peptide sequences and XIC signals into a shared latent manifold for establishing foundational 113 

semantic correspondences. Complementing this global alignment, the encoder-decoder architecture serves 114 

as a discriminative engine, engineered to decode the intricate, non-linear dependencies between sequences 115 

and spectral signatures. This synergistic design couples the broad generalization of the dual-encoder with 116 

the high-resolution sensitivity of the encoder-decoder, facilitating precise PSM adjudication across 117 

heterogeneous datasets. 118

To circumvent the “run-specific” training constraints inherent in conventional DIA analysis, πDIA-CLIP 119 

employs a supervised contrastive learning pre-training strategy to internalize robust representations of 120 

peptide-spectrum correspondences (Fig. 1a). We curated a large-scale training dataset by processing DIA-121 

MS data through DIA-NN, yielding an extensive collection of over 28 million high-confidence PSMs. 122 

This dataset encompasses four diverse species( Homo sapiens , Mus musculus , Saccharomyces cerevisiae ,123 

and Escherichia coli ) and was acquired across five distinct MS platforms (e.g., Astral, TripleTOF; 124 

Supplementary Information Table 1). To bolster the discriminative performance of πDIA-CLIP against 125 

deceptive signals, entrapment PSMs are incorporated as negative sample during pre-training. This 126 

approach compels πDIA-CLIP to learn the subtle spectral nuances required to distinguish true targets 127 

from entrapments, ensuring accurate identification in a zero-shot manner. By pre-training on this large-128 

scale dataset, πDIA-CLIP captures the intrinsic peptide-spectrum correspondences, fundamentally 129 

transcending the constraints of traditional feature engineering. Crucially, this entrapment-integrated pre-130 

training strategy empowers πDIA-CLIP with zero-shot inference capabilities, enabling deep and accurate 131 

PSM identification with exceptional computational efficiency. To facilitate widespread academic and 132 

clinical adoption, πDIA-CLIP is deployed across a versatile ecosystem (Fig. 1a), supporting localized 133 

execution via a standalone workstation client, high-throughput processing through a scalable inference 134 

API, and automated down-stream interpretation integrated into LLM-driven agent analysts. 135 

Leveraging the πDIA-CLIP, we established a comprehensive, peptide-centric workflow for end-to-end 136 

DIA-MS data analysis (Fig. 1b). This pipeline is designed to be software-agnostic, where RT calibration 137 

step can be performed by mainstream DIA identification tools. Subsequently, πDIA-CLIP extracts 138 

precursor and fragment XICs, performing PSM re-scoring and quantification within a zero-shot scenario. 139 

By imbuing the standardized workflow with extensive prior knowledge internalized during large-scale 140 

pre-training, πDIA-CLIP transcends biases inherent in individual experimental runs to ensure consistent, 141 

high-fidelity identification across varying experimental conditions. 142

# Zero-Shot Inference Empowers Computational Efficiency 225 

The advent of high-throughput MS, exemplified by the Orbitrap Astral, facilitates unprecedented data 226 

generation 12,36 . Despite the enhanced identification depth enabled by sophisticated deep learning 227 

architectures, the necessity for intensive training or optimization remains a substantial bottleneck. This 228 

lag between data acquisition and processing speed increasingly restricts the scalability of modern 229 

proteomic workflows. 230 

πDIA-CLIP bridges this computational gap by shifting the DIA analysis strategy from repetitive semi-231 

supervised training to a generalized zero-shot representation, offering a unified, inference-only solution 232 

for PSM re-scoring. The computational efficiency between πDIA-CLIP and existing deep learning tools 233 

was benchmarked using HeLa cell lysates analyzed with 0.5h chromatographic gradient (PRIDE ID: 234 

PXD005573). In this experiment, more than 26,000 MS/MS spectra were searched against a spectral 235 

library containing more than 4,000,000 precursors. To ensure hardware parity, all evaluations were 236 

conducted on a high-performance server equipped with 8 NVIDIA A100 (80GB) GPUs and dual Intel 237 

Xeon Platinum 8336C processors (2.30 GHz, 56 cores/112 threads). By obviating the computational 238 

burden of iterative optimization, πDIA-CLIP completed the entire workflow within 13 minutes, 239 

representing an 8-fold and a more than 100-fold acceleration in run time relative to AlphaDIA (1.7 h) and 240 

DIA-BERT (22.9 h), respectively (Supplementary Information Table 2). Furthermore, a granular 241 

assessment of PSM re-scoring and FDR estimation further underscored this efficiency, with πDIA-CLIP 242 

requiring only 2.8 minutes for these tasks. This performance contrasted sharply with AlphaDIA (31.7 min) 243 

and DIA-BERT (5.2 h) despite relying on high-performance GPU acceleration. This profound speedup 244 

stems from the zero-shot architecture of πDIA-CLIP, which requires only a single, highly efficient 245 

forward inference. This approach fundamentally bypasses the multiple computationally expensive epochs 246 

of model training and parameter updating mandated by existing models. Remarkably, πDIA-CLIP 247

# Unlocking the Metaproteome via Zero-Shot Generalizability 256 

Metaproteomics has emerged as a transformative tool for deciphering microbiome-host interactions and 257 

complex ecological networks by capturing community-wide functional profiles 37,38 . In practice, the wide-258 

window acquisition of DIA-MS in metaproteomics yields highly chimeric spectra with overlapping 259 

fragment ions and composition-dependent interference, presenting a significant computational challenge 260 

for accurate profiling across diverse samples. Characterizing such immense taxonomic diversity within 261 

highly heterogeneous datasets necessitates robust generalizability, ensuring reliable identification of 262 

unseen taxa. To evaluate the generalizability of πDIA-CLIP across heterogeneous and previously unseen 263 

biological contexts, we benchmarked πDIA-CLIP utilizing a mixed metaproteomic cohort (PXD054415) 264 

comprising 32 species at equal cell counts 39 . The community spans immense taxonomic distances, 265 

including representatives from Archaea and Eukaryota alongside 25 bacterial strains and five 266 

bacteriophages. Since validated protein sequences for the Archaea, Eukaryota, and bacteriophages were 267 

unavailable in UniProt, library-free DIA-MS searching of our evaluation was restricted to a FASTA 268 

database of the 25 bacterial strains sourced from the original study 39 .269 

Comparative benchmarking on the PXD054415 cohort revealed superior identification generalizability for 270 

πDIA-CLIP at both precursor and protein levels, despite no prior exposure to these specific biological 271 

species. Total identifications of πDIA-CLIP reached approximately 11,000 precursors and 5,000 proteins, 272 

representing increases of at least 2.3% (Fig. 4a) and 2.0% (Fig. 4b) over existing tools, respectively. 273 

Species-wise analysis confirmed species-agnostic identification gains across the majority of microbial 274 

taxa (Fig. 4c). The improvements appeared particularly pronounced among low-abundance proteins, as 275 

evidenced by average 0.7% deviation in quantitative values and 13% reduction in standard deviation 276 

(Supplementary Information Fig. S4a). Furthermore, assessment of quantification stability revealed 277 

average 29.3% decrease in median CV values for πDIA-CLIP across all evaluated taxa, highlighting 278 

enhanced reproducibility in complex metaproteomic contexts (Supplementary Information Fig. S4b). 279

emerged as a transformative tool in various fields like tumor microenvironment research and clinical 288 

precision medicine 40-42 . To evaluate the practicality of πDIA-CLIP in these complex biological scenarios, 289 

a spatially resolved proteomics dataset generated by integrating laser capture microdissection (LCM) with 290 

liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) is utilized to profile the proteomic landscape of 291

# Discussion 358 

This study introduces πDIA-CLIP, a cross-modal framework establishing novel identification approach in 359 

data-independent acquisition proteomics by transitioning from semi-supervised training models to a 360 

unified, generalized zero-shot inference architecture. By obviating the need for repetitive optimization, 361 

this approach eliminates the inherent risk of overfitting within constrained local datasets, while the 362 

inference-only architecture facilitates exceptional computational efficiency across heterogeneous 363 

biological scenarios, including metaproteomics, spatial proteomics, and single-cell proteomics. 364 

Mechanistically, πDIA-CLIP involves the first implementation of cross-modal contrastive learning within 365 

DIA-MS, where a dual-encoder framework aligns peptide sequences and multi-dimensional XIC signals 366 

within a shared latent space. Ablation studies further underscore the critical role of contrastive learning in 367 

fostering the generalizability of πDIA-CLIP, while simultaneously revealing substantial scope for 368 

architectural refinement to further enhance identification depth and sensitivity (see Supplementary 369 

Information Notes). Furthermore, by leveraging global prior knowledge from millions of PSMs, πDIA-370 

CLIP ensures precise zero-shot identification and exceptional robustness across diverse instrumental 371 

platforms and biological scenarios, representing a foundational step toward truly scalable and 372 

generalizable proteomics informatics. 373 

Systematic benchmarking across heterogeneous cohorts, including complex multi-species consortium, 374 

metaproteomic microbial communities, clinical breast cancer specimens and ultra-low-input single-cell 375

Although the conventional FDR threshold serves as a golden standard, it fails to represent an absolute 383 

demarcation between authentic and spurious identifications. Due to inherent statistical fluctuations, there 384 

remain some authentic target signals in proximity to this threshold. Integrating expert experience to 385

# Re-scoring and Quantification Procedure of πDIA-CLIP 428 

As illustrated in Figure S1, πDIA-CLIP is composed of three primary functional components: initial 429 

encoding phase, contrastive learning alignment stage, and specialized decoder for PSM re-scoring. 430 

The initial encoding phase utilizes a dual-encoder architecture. Within a training batch of size B ,431 

precursor ions undergo transformation into feature representations Pଵ, P ଶ, … , P ୆. Besides, to handle the 432 

intrinsic relationship between precursor XICs and fragment XICs, πDIA-CLIP employs dedicated XIC 433 

encoding layers to separately process precursor XICs, fragment XICs, and concatenated XICs. These 434 

resulting features are then combined and processed to a representation of spectrum features Sଵ, S ଶ, … , S ୆.435

The secondary stage involves a contrastive learning framework designed to align precursor and spectrum 436 

features within a unified, shared latent space. Optimization of πDIA-CLIP focuses on maximizing the 437

Derivation of the PSM score utilizes a specialized decoder architecture. Encoded precursor and spectrum 440 

features are fed into a transformer-based decoder to generate a [B, dim_model] feature. In parallel, 441 

physical co-elution characteristics are explicitly incorporated through the calculation of Pearson 442 

Correlation Coefficient (PCC) matrix, subsequently projected into a [B, dim_model] feature via a multi-443 

layer perceptron (MLP). Fusion of these two features occurs through element-wise summation, followed 444 

by a final MLP with a sigmoid activation function. This process yields a calibrated PSM score ranging 445 

from 0 to 1. 446 

Calculation of quantitative abundance for each identified precursor relies on the arithmetic mean of Top-447 

K areas of fragment XICs. While K conventionally equals 6, πDIA-CLIP adaptively selects the top-6 or 448 

all available fragment ions with non-zero areas, ensuring the inclusion of only informative signals in the 449 

final quantitative result. 450

# Analysis of Single-cell DIA-MS Data 507 

To standardize and analyze single-cell data across disparate search engines, we mapped identified 508 

precursors to a common human spectral library, followed by protein quantification through precursor-509 

level averaging and outlier rejection. To account for technical variation in total signal, cell-specific 510 

protein abundances were normalized to a scale of one million units, and filtered to retain only proteins 511 

with normalized abundance exceeding 1.0 in at least two cells. 512 

The resulting high-dimensional expression matrices were log-transformed and subjected to Independent 513 

Component Analysis (ICA) using the FastICA algorithm (random_state=3984) to identify the primary 514 

axes of cellular variation. Four independent components were extracted to serve as the basis for 515 

unsupervised hierarchical clustering via Ward’s method. The optimal clustering resolution was 516 

determined by assessing dendrogram linkage distances, with a threshold of 6 applied to partition cells into 517 

distinct subpopulations. To visualize these high-dimensional relationships in a lower-dimensional 518 

manifold, t-SNE algorithm (perplexity=75, random_state=254) was performed on the ICA component 519 

space. 520

# Benchmark setting 521 

The benchmarking process included a comparative evaluation against several state-of-the-art software 522 

tools. Specifically, for the HeLa cell lysates and complex multi-species mixture, the evaluation was 523 

performed using DIA-NN (v2.0), MaxDIA (v2.6.7.0), and MSFragger-DIA (v4.1). Meanwhile, for the 524 

metaproteomics, spatial proteomics, and single-cell proteomics datasets, we utilized DIA-NN (v1.7.12), 525 

MaxDIA (v2.6.7.0) and MSFragger-DIA (v4.1). Reference proteomes for H. sapiens , S. cerevisiae , E. 526 

coli , and M. musculus were retrieved from the UniProt database. 527 

For library-based search workflows, DIA-NN utilized spectral libraries predicted by DIA-NN (v1.8.1) 528 

based on the respective FASTA files, whereas MaxDIA employed its native library prediction engine. 529 

The predicted search space was constrained to peptide lengths ranging from 7 to 30 amino acids with 530 

precursor charge states between 1+ and 4+. Crucially, the MBR functionality remained disabled to ensure 531 

a standardized assessment of identification performance across all platforms. Analyses using MSFragger-532 

DIA were conducted via the DIA-SpecLib-Quant workflow, with mass tolerances set to 10 ppm for 533 

precursors and 20 ppm for fragments. For all searches, carbamidomethylation of cysteine was considered 534 

as fixed modification. To ensure a standardized assessment of identification depth, protein-level 535 

identification was performed with q < 1.0, with a protein deemed identified upon at least one constituent 536 

peptide meeting the detection threshold. 537 

Quantification assessments utilized precursor-level data across all evaluated platforms. Specifically, DIA-538 

NN processing exclusively employed the direct quantification mode without MBR functionality. 539 

Precursor quantities were extracted from the 'Precursor.Quantity' column in DIA-NN results and the 540 

'Intensity' column within evidence.txt files of MaxDIA. For protein-level quantification, a uniform 541 

approach involved calculating the arithmetic mean of constituent peptide intensities following the 542
