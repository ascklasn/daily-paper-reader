Abstract 7

Genomic epidemiology workflows typically require expert curation of multiple specialized tools, 8

extensive manual parameter tuning, and access to heterogeneous compute infrastructure. While 9

standard generative AI models often hallucinate in complex biological domains, we introduce 10 

biomeStat: an autonomous AI agent that functions as a strict deterministic orchestrator. By 11 

automatically writing code to execute established bioinformatics tools in sandboxed 12 

environments, biomeStat dynamically provisions compute resources (CPU and GPU) and 13 

guarantees reproducibility, making it immediately useful for scientists without requiring 14 

command -line expertise. 15 

To demonstrate the platform, we performed a fully autonomous genomic epidemiology and 16 

structural analysis of 1,000 Dengue virus (DENV) genomes sampled from 16 Asian countries 17 

between 2000 and 2025. The agent seamlessly orchestrated phylogenetic reconstruction (IQ -18 

TREE, TreeTime), Bayesian phylodynamics (BEAST2 via NVIDIA H200 GPU), selection 19 

pressure analysis (HyPhy), and structural mapping (PyMOL). The analysis was completed in 20 

under 24 hours of wall -clock time, revealing endemic stability (R_e  ~1.0) and identifying 1,869 21 

candidate immune escape sites structurally colocalized with B -cell and T -cell epitopes. 22 

Furthermore, the agent validated 176 highly conserved drug target residues across the viral 23 

replication complex, confirming that resistance -associated positions for emerging antivirals JNJ -24 

1802 and NITD -688 remain absolutely conserved across all four serotypes. By bridging the gap 25 

between natural language intent and deterministic computational execution, biomeStat reduces 26 

weeks of expert effort into a single -session analysis with full methodological transparency. 27

# Introduction 37 

Genomic epidemiology is at the core of how we track viral evolution, infer transmission 38 

networks and monitor immune escape [1]. However, the actual task of applying phylogenetic and 39 

phylodynamic models to emerging viruses such as dengue, SARS -CoV2, influenza etc. , faces 40 

severe computational and bioinformatic s bottlenecks [2]. This chokepoint is often worse in 41 

resource constrained research centres especially in the Global South [3-5]. Traditional Bayesian 42 

phylogenetic tools, which provide the most accurate epidemiological insights, scale 43 

exponentially with dataset size (O(N2)), rendering them computationally intractable for massive 44 

datasets like the millions of genomics sequenced during the COVID19 pandemic [2, 6]. This 45 

scalability crisis forces researchers to either wait weeks for convergence or rely on heuristic 46 

approaches that sacrifice statistical certainty .47 

Beyond sheer data volume, complex evolutionary dynamics and uneven sampling compound 48 

these computational hurdles. For example, early in the SARS -CoV -2 pandemic, the virus’s low 49 

genetic diversity led to “flat trees” with unresolved polytomies, making transmission inference 50 

mathematically difficult [6]. Furthermore, events like recombination in dengue and SARS -CoV -51 

2 variants, or reassortment in influenza, violate the assumptions of strict bifurcating trees and 52 

require computationally heavy network or structured coalescent models [7]. Additionally, 53 

unstandardized amplicon sequencing pipelines had introduced systematic errors into millions of 54 

SARS -CoV -2 consensus sequences in public databases, generating spurious phylogenetic 55 

branches that corrupted downstream evolutionary analyses and required reprocessing of 4.47 56 

million genomes through a purpose -built amplicon -aware pipeline to correct [8]. In the field, 57 

real -time sequencing of outbreaks like Ebola and Zika generates data that demands resource -58 

intensive error -correction algorithms simply to distinguish sequencing noise from true mutations 59 

[9, 10 ]. Consequently, executing a complete genomic epidemiology workflow requires 60 

integrating numerous specialized tools, extensive parameterization, and managing disparate 61 

computational resources (from CPU tasks to GPU -intensive MCMC chains), which typically 62 

demands weeks of expert bioinformatician effort [2]. The scale of this challenge was recently 63 

underscored when systematic amplicon -driven errors were discovered to have corrupted millions 64 

of SARS -CoV -2 phylogenies globally, requiring reprocessing of 4.47 million genomes with 65 

purpose -built assembly tools [8].66 

67 

Here we introduce biomeStat (www.biomestat.com) , an AI agent designed to autonomously 68 

execute bioinformatics workflows by selecting appropriate tools, configuring parameters, 69 

interpreting intermediate results, and dynamically scaling compute resources. The agent ma de 70 

real -time decisions about tool selection, parameter optimization, error handling, and resource 71 

allocation without human intervention during execution. 72 

To validate this approach, we tasked biomeStat with a complete genomic epidemiology analysis 73 

of 1,000 DENV genomes from Asia spanning 25 years (2000 –2025). The workflow produced 74 

phylodynamic estimates of effective reproductive number ( 𝑅 𝑒 ), serotype -specific demographic 75 

histories, a comprehensive map of immune escape candidates, and identification of conserved 76 

drug targets, all within a single analysis session. 77

Dataset Assembly 85 

We retrieved 1,000 complete DENV genome sequences from NCBI GenBank using the Entrez 86 

API. The authors set the sequence curation criteria as: complete genomes, all serotypes, from 87 

Asia, collected from human samples, between 2000 -2025. 88 

Sequences were stratified to ensure geographic and temporal representativeness: a maximum of 89 

200 sequences per country across 16 Asian nations (Vietnam,  𝑛 = 200 ; Singapore,  𝑛 = 172 ;90

2000 –2024, with peak representation in 2006 –2008 and 2015 –2019. Serotype composition was: 94 

DENV -1 ( 𝑛 = 371 , 37.1%), DENV -2 ( 𝑛 = 304 , 30.4%), DENV -3 ( 𝑛 = 235 , 23.5%), and 95 

DENV -4 ( 𝑛 = 90 , 9.0%). 96 

Lineage Assignment 97 

Serotype -specific lineage classification was performed using Nextclade v3 [11 ] with reference 98 

datasets for each serotype. Lineages were assigned following the nomenclature proposed by Hill 99 

et al. [11 ], which provides a four -level hierarchy (serotype, genotype, major lineage, minor 100 

lineage) analogous to the Pango system used for SARS -CoV -2. 101 

Multiple Sequence Alignment 102 

Whole -genome alignment was performed using MAFFT v7 [12 ] with default parameters. To 103 

study specific viral proteins (Envelope, NS1, NS3, NS4A, NS4B and NS5), we first identified 104 

their exact locations within each serotype's genome. We then extracted these specific genetic 105 

sequences, translated them into amino acids, and aligned them using the highly accurate MAFFT 106 

L-INS -i algorithm [12 ].107 

Maximum Likelihood Phylogeny 108 

Phylogenetic inference was performed using IQ -TREE 2 [13 ] under the GTR+F+G4 substitution 109 

model [14 , 15 ] with 1,000 ultrafast bootstrap replicates [16 ]. The analysis yielded a tree with log -110 

likelihood of  −359 ,017 , total tree length of 7.55 substitutions/site, and 56.2% of branch length 111 

internal to the tree, indicating well -resolved deep structure. 112

Molecular Clock and Temporal Analysis 113 

Root -to -tip regression and time -scaled phylogeny construction were performed with TreeTime 114 

[17 ] , yielding an estimated substitution rate of  9.16 × 10 −5 substitutions/site/year across the full 115 

dataset. 116 

Bayesian Phylodynamics 117 

Birth -Death Skyline (BDSKY) 118 

A Birth -Death Skyline model [18 ] was implemented in BEAST2 v2.7.8 [19 ] on the full 1,000 -119 

taxon dataset. The MCMC chain was run for  10 7 states with five -time intervals (2000 –2005, 120 

2005 –2010, 2010 –2015, 2015 –2020, 2020 –2025). GPU acceleration was provided by the 121 

BEAGLE library [20 ] running on an NVIDIA H200 GPU (141 GB VRAM). Convergence was 122 

assessed by effective sample size (ESS) criteria. 123 

Per -Serotype Bayesian Skyline Plot (BSP) 124 

Serotype -specific demographic reconstructions were performed using the Bayesian Skyline Plot 125 

coalescent model within BEAST2 for each of the four serotypes independently (DENV -1: 371 126 

taxa; DENV -2: 304 taxa; DENV -3: 235 taxa; DENV -4: 90 taxa). Each MCMC chain was run for 127 

10 7 states under an HKY+G4 model with a strict molecular clock. Population size was estimated 128 

in five intervals per serotype. A 10% burn -in was applied to all posterior samples. 129 

Selection Pressure Analysis 130 

Site -specific selection was quantified using the HyPhy package. Pervasive selection was 131 

estimated by FUBAR (Fast Unconstrained Bayesian Approximation for inferring selection) [21 ],132 

and episodic diversifying selection was detected by MEME (Mixed Effects Model of Evolution) 133 

[22 ]. Additionally, within -serotype Tajima’s D [23 ] was computed for E, NS1, NS3, NS4, and 134 

NS5 to characterize population -level selection. 135 

Immune Escape Mapping 136 

To predict where the virus is actively mutating to evade the immune system, we mapped 137 

candidate immune escape sites by integrating structural, immunological, and evolutionary data 138 

across the viral proteins. 139 

To structurally visualize population -level sequence variability, position -specific Shannon 140 

entropy values calculated from the 1,000 -genome multiple sequence alignment were mapped 141 

onto the 3D crystal structure of the DENV -2 Envelope (E) glycoprotein complexed with the 142 

broadly neutralizing human antibody EDE1 C10 (PDB ID: 4UT9). Similarly, to map T -cell 143 

immune evasion, predicted MHC Class I and II epitopes and episodic escape mutations were 144 

mapped onto the structure of the NS5 RNA -dependent RNA polymerase and Methyltransferase 145 

domains (PDB ID: 6IZZ). Using PyMOL (Schrödinger, LLC), entropy values were substituted 146 

into the temperature factor (B -factor) column of the PDB files to render surface variability, 147 

allowing direct spatial correlation between mutational hotspots, immune epitopes, and 148 

functionally constrained viral domains. 149 

Rather than randomly analyzing mutations across the entire genome, we restricted our search to 150 

regions most likely to be targeted by the host immune response. To avoid the severe 151

We identified B cell epitopes by computationally analyzing the inherent physicochemical 155 

properties required for antibody binding. Specifically, we calculated local hydrophilicity using 156 

the Hopp -Woods scale [26 ] and predicted surface accessibility using the Emini fractional 157 

probability model [27 ]. By establishing strict statistical thresholds for these metrics, we 158 

effectively flagged surface -exposed, water -accessible regions, accurately mirroring the logic of 159 

platforms like BepiPred 3.0 [24 , 28 ] .160 

To find potential T cell epitopes, MHC Class I and Class II presentation sites were identified by 161 

applying the binding affinity rules derived from the NetMHCpan 4.1 and NetMHCIIpan 4.0 162 

neural networks [25 ]. We computationally scanned our viral alignments to locate conserved 163 

anchor residues and sequence profiles that satisfy the strong -binding definitions (e.g., predicted 164 

binding rank ≤2%) established by these models. This allowed us to map the boundaries of 165 

peptides most likely to be naturally presented by diverse human leukocyte antigen alleles [29 ,166 

30 ] .167 

To measure how much these specific immune -targeted regions mutate across our Asian Dengue 168 

isolates, we calculated the Shannon entropy [31 ]at every single amino acid position. High 169 

entropy flags a hotspot where the virus actively tolerates or encourages amino acid variability, 170 

whereas low entropy indicates a structurally conserved residue. 171 

When identifying true escape mutants because high variability alone does not prove immune 172 

escape (it could simply be random genetic drift ), we required proof of evolutionary adaptation. 173 

Because absolute cutoff values fail to capture the extreme structural variability between different 174 

Dengue proteins, we implemented a dynamic, protein -specific heuristic. We operationally 175 

defined a 70th percentile composite propensity score and a 6 -residue minimum length constraint 176 

because absolute thresholds fail across highly heterogeneous viral proteins. 177 

This dynamic, protein -specific threshold successfully flagged the most structurally viable 178 

antibody targets. Crucially, any potential false positives generated by this structural screening 179 

were subsequently filtered out by our rigorous evolutionary criteria (FUBAR/MEME) [21 ] ,180 

ensuring that only sites under active positive selection were ultimately classified as immune 181 

escape candidates. 182 

We identified true immune escape sites by looking for a specific signature: when a mutation 183 

inside an epitope was both highly variable across strains and statistically proven to be under 184 

positive selection, we concluded the virus was actively retaining this mutation to evade host 185 

immune defenses. 186

Codon Usage Analysis 197 

Because dengue virus must replicate efficiently in two drastically different environments namely, 198 

the Aedes aegypti mosquito vector and the human host, it faces conflicting evolutionary 199 

pressures on its genome [38 ].200 

To evaluate how the virus balances these conflicting demands, we calculated the Codon 201

Structural Colocalization of Mutational Hotspots and Immune Epitopes: 290 

To determine if the functionally identified escape mutations physically disrupt antibody binding, 291 

we mapped the population -level Shannon entropy onto a 3D co -crystal structure of the DENV E 292 

protein complexed with a broadly neutralizing antibody. The structural mapping revealed that 293 

mutational variability is not randomly distributed across the viral surface. Instead, high -entropy 294 

mutation hotspots strongly colocalize with the physical docking footprint of the EDE1 C10 295 

neutralizing antibody. This spatial ove rlap confirms that the functional sequence variation 296 

identified in our population -level analysis directly alters the physical contact points required for 297 

neutralizing antibody attachment, providing structural validation of B -cell immune escape. 298 

299 

Furthermore, while the NS5 protein contained the highest absolute number of escape sites, the 300 

incidence of these individual mutations remained extremely low within the population (over 70% 301 

of escape sites remained >99% conserved). Structural mapping on the NS5 prote in resolved this 302 

apparent paradox (Supplementary figure 1) . We found that 43.8% of the NS5 surface is targeted 303 

by MHC Class I and II T -cell epitopes. However, the virus's ability to mutate these regions is 304 

severely restricted by the critical functional domains of the Methyltransferase (MTase) and 305 

RNA -dependent RNA Polymerase (RdRp). Escape mutations mapped directly to regions 306 

overlapping these vital catalytic engines, indicating that while episodic escape from T -cells 307 

occurs, it incurs a massive fitness cos t. This restricts the global spread of these mutations, 308 

explaining the strong purifying selection (highly negative Tajima's D) observed in NS5. 309

# Discussion 445 

Dengue virus infects an estimated 390 million people annually, with over 70% of the clinical 446 

burden concentrated in tropical and subtropical Asia [52 -54 ]. Genomic surveillance is now 447 

central to tracking viral evolution, monitoring immune escape, and guiding vaccine and antiviral 448 

development [1, 11 ], yet a complete genomic epidemiology workflow requires integrating 449 

numerous specialized tools - from sequence alignment and phylogenetic inference to Bayesian 450 

phylodynamics, epitope prediction, and conservation mapping - each with distinct 451 

parameterization and compute requirements. Orchestrating these tools manually for a dataset of 452 

1,000 genomes typically requires weeks of expert bioinformatician time. 453 

Recent studies have highlighted the difficulty autonomous AI agents face when navigating 454 

complex biological databases like NCBI due to a lack of deterministic retrieval layers [55 ]. By 455 

utilizing biomeStat, we overcame this bottleneck; the agent programmatically translated natural 456 

language criteria (human host, Asian origin, 2000 –2025 collection, complete genomes) into 457 

strict, deterministic API queries (NCBI E -utilities/Biopython), ensuring rigorous and 458 

reproducible dataset construction without the errors typical of non -deterministic LLM retrieval. 459 

Autonomous pipeline validation. biomeStat compressed this workflow to hours through three 460 

core capabilities. First, the agent autonomously selected tools from a curated container registry 461 

and configured complex model parameters (GTR+G4 substitution models, MCMC chain 462 

lengths) based on the inherent characteristics of the dataset, without human prompting. Second, 463 

recognizing that BEAST2 MCMC analyses require GPU -accelerated likelihood computation, 464 

biomeStat autonomously escalated from a CPU tier to an H200 GPU and configured the 465 

BEAGLE library for CUDA parallelization. Third, when the agent encountered version -specific 466 

software errors (such as deprecated variable types or mathematical constraints inside BEAST2), 467 

it diagnosed the bugs and corrected them in real -time rather than halting execution. 468 

Concordance with established dengue biology. The biological outputs of the pipeline align with 469 

published data. The effective reproductive number (Re) hovered near 1.0 across all five -year 470 

intervals from 2000 to 2025 (Figure 3), consistent with the endemic equilibrium of dengue in 471 

Asia [9]. The slight decline from the earliest to the most recent interval should be interpreted 472

As evidenced by our profoundly negative Tajima's D values (Figure 4) coupled with high 495 

segregating site counts (Supplementary Table 2) (e.g., S=886 for DENV -3 NS5), these recent 496

sampled before purifying selection can purge them from the population [7].498

validated epitopes. This supports the use of this agentic AI orchestrated workflow as a good 507 

addition to immune epitope mapping methods. 508

Our combined functional and structural analysis provides a clear physical basis for DENV 519

mutations precisely at neutralizing interfaces on the Envelope protein. Because the E protein is 521 

the primary target of host neutralizing antibodies, its structural flexibility at these specific contact 522

reactive, sub -neutralizing antibodies generated against these variable antigenic loops can 524 

exacerbate disease severity during secondary heterotypic infections. Conversely, the internal 525 

NS5 protein is subjected to an intense evolutionary tug -of -war (Supplementary Figure 1) . While 526 

it is broadly targeted by T -cells, NS5 has almost no structural flexibility due to its vital 527 

polymerase and methyltransferase functions. Any episodic mutations that successfully evade T -528 

cell recognition incur severe fitness penalties, trapping these mutants at low population 529 

frequencies and making the conserved regions of NS5 ideal, stable targets for small -molecule 530 

antiviral development. 531 

These findings are directly relevant to clinical trials now underway. VIS513, a humanized pan -532

in Phase 1 [63 ] and is now being evaluated alongside molnupiravir in the ADAPT Phase 2 534

protein escape sites identified here will be important for anticipating potential resistance to such 536 

mAb -based therapeutics. More broadly, any drug or vaccine targeting these hyper -variable loci 537 

would face rapid mutational evasion, reinforcing the need to direct therapeutic development 538 

toward the conserved functional domains identified in our drug target analysis (Figure 7). 539

uniformly constrained domains in the study. 547

# Limitations 587 

Several limitations should be noted. First, immune escape candidates were identified using 588 

surrogate thresholds based on Shannon entropy, surface accessibility, and evidence of 589 

diversifying selection, rather than direct epitope prediction via BepiPred 3.0 or NetMHCpan 4.1. 590 

While BiomeStat could integrate these tools as additional containerized modules, the surrogate 591 

approach was chosen to enable rapid, alignment -level screening across all six proteins 592 

simultaneously; validation of prioritized candidates with structure -based B -cell and HLA -593 

restricted T -cell epitope predictors remains a logical next step. Second, the BEAST2 analyses 594 

used strict clock models for computational tractability; relaxed clock models may provide more 595 

accurate rate estimates. Third, sampling density varied substantially across countries and years, 596 

which may bias phylodynamic estimates. Fourth, the 10M -state MCMC chains, while producing 597 

reasonable ESS values, are shorter than the 50 –100M states often recommended for BDSKY 598 

analyses. While a traditional, deep retrospective analysis might employ 50 -100M MCMC states, 599 

our objective was to simulate a rapid -response outbreak scenario. By utilizing an H200 GPU and 600 

terminating the chains at 10M states once critical parameters reached reasonable ESS 601 

convergence, biomeStat successfully generated an actionable phylodynamic snapshot (including 602
