ABSTRACT 1Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is characterized by complex lung architecture and 2spatially heterogeneous remodeling, which have hindered integrated analysis of cell-3intrinsic activity and intercellular communication during disease progression. Here we 4profiled six IPF lung specimens comprising more than 630,000 cells using the Xenium 55k panel and developed an epithelial-centered neighborhood profiling framework based 6on the local cellular composition around each epithelial cell. This approach captured 7fibrosis-associated variation in epithelial niches without requiring predefined 8histological regions. Pseudo-temporal continuum inference of these profiles 9reconstructed a continuous axis that reflected the spatial progression of fibrotic 10 remodeling from relatively preserved alveolar regions to fibrotic and airway-like 11 remodeled regions. Within this spatial dataset, we mapped coordinated changes in 12 epithelial states, local microenvironments, epithelial intracellular pathway activities, and 13 directional interactions with neighboring cell types along the same axis. Our findings 14 provide a spatial framework that generates testable hypotheses for progressive epithelial 15 niche remodeling in IPF. 16

INTRODUCTION 1Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is a chronic, progressive, and ultimately fatal 2interstitial lung disease of unknown cause 1. The prognosis remains poor, with median 3survival of approximately 2–5 years after diagnosis 1–3 . A deeper understanding of the 4mechanisms driving remodeling is therefore required to identify disease- modifying 5targets. 6A major obstacle to understanding IPF pathogenesis is the spatial complexity of 7the IPF lung, which comprises regionally specialized epithelial, mesenchymal, 8endothelial, and immune niches. In IPF, this complexity is amplified by spatially 9heterogeneous remodeling that is difficult to reproduce in vitro or in experimental 10 models 4,5 . Among the pathological changes in IPF, epithelial remodeling is particularly 11 prominent 6–8 . Normal alveolar type 1 (AT1) and type 2 (AT2) cells are progressively 12 lost, while KRT5 -/KRT17 + aberrant epithelial cells emerge near fibroblastic foci 8–10 , and 13 honeycomb cysts are lined by airway-like epithelium 11 . These observations suggest a 14 directional transition from preserved alveolar epithelium to aberrant epithelial states and 15 airway-like metaplasia. However, how this transition is coordinated with changes in the 16 surrounding microenvironment remains incompletely understood. 17 Single-cell transcriptomics and spatial profiling have identified IPF-associated 18 cell states and multicellular niches 7,10,12–15 . However, most spatial niche analyses 19 describe tissue architecture as discrete structural states, leaving their relationship to 20 fibrotic progression uncertain. Although airspace-based analyses have inferred fibrotic 21 stages from individual airspace units, they provide limited access to cell-level 22 transcriptional activity and cell–cell communication within the same spatial dataset 13 .23

4Thus, a framework that links tissue architecture, epithelial remodeling, intracellular 1programs, and intercellular signaling within spatial data is needed. 2Here, we analyzed six human IPF tissue samples using Xenium 5k spatial 3transcriptomics. We developed epithelial-centered neighborhood profiling to quantify 4the cellular composition surrounding each epithelial cell and used trajectory inference to 5reconstruct a pseudo-temporal continuum of fibrotic remodeling from local tissue 6architecture. We then integrated this continuum with cell-level pathway activity analysis 7and spatially resolved ligand–receptor scoring to examine the transition of epithelial 8intracellular programs and cell–cell communication within the same dataset. This 9framework provides a spatially anchored view of epithelial remodeling in IPF and 10 identifies candidate signaling axes that may be associated with fibrotic progression. 11 12

9Given that fibrotic remodeling in IPF proceeds unidirectionally within the local 1epithelial microenvironment, yet distinct histological stages coexist within the same 2specimen, we reasoned that epithelial-centered neighborhood profiles could be ordered 3along a pseudo-temporal continuum of fibrotic progression. We adapted the logic of 4pseudotime analysis, which infers dynamic biological processes from snapshot single-5cell data containing mixed cellular states. Here, instead of gene expression, we used 6cell-type composition vectors derived from each epithelial-centered neighborhood 7profile as input, allowing continuity to be inferred from differences in local cellular 8composition. As the input and interpretation differ from conventional pseudotime 9analysis, we refer to this framework as “pseudo-temporal continuum inference” (Fig. 10 3a). To focus on fibrotic remodeling, we excluded bronchiolar and alveolar duct classes, 11 which reflected bronchiolar and alveolar duct structures, and the epithelial-rich class, 12 which contained little non-epithelial microenvironmental information. Using near-13 normal alveolar, pre-fibrotic, fibrotic, and metaplastic classes, we inferred trajectory 14 structure with scFates 26 and designated profiles assigned to the near-normal alveolar 15 class as the root to assign directionality (Fig. 3b, Extended Data Fig. 7a). Each epithelial 16 cell was assigned a continuum position (Extended Data Fig. 7b), and projection of these 17 values onto tissue coordinates revealed a spatially continuous distribution of fibrotic 18 remodeling across samples (Fig. 3c, d, Extended Data Fig. 7c–f). DISCUSSION 4In this study, we analyzed six IPF tissue samples using Xenium 5k panel-based spatial 5transcriptomics and developed an epithelial-centered framework to characterize fibrotic 6remodeling. Pseudo-temporal continuum inference based on epithelial-centered 7neighborhood profiling allowed us to model fibrotic remodeling as a continuous process 8anchored to the local cellular environment of epithelial cells. This continuum reflected 9histopathologic progression and provided a common axis for evaluating epithelial 10 subtype composition, neighboring cell populations, epithelial intracellular pathway 11 activity, and inferred interactions between epithelial cells and their surrounding cells 12 during fibrotic progression. Although the findings should be interpreted as hypothesis-13 generating, they provide a spatially anchored framework for understanding the 14 multicellular coordination of fibrotic remodeling in IPF. 15 Previous spatial and single-cell studies of pulmonary fibrosis have often defined 16 disease progression at the sample or region level, and even niche-based analyses 17 typically considered all cell types together, resulting in stage-like rather than continuous 18 representations of remodeling 12,13,15,24 . By reducing the spatial heterogeneity of IPF into 19 epithelial-centered neighborhood profiles representing different phases of fibrotic 20 remodeling, our approach incorporated continuity into the analysis of epithelial 21 microenvironments. This framework enabled pathway activity and cell–cell interactions 22 to be evaluated within the same spatial dataset along a shared remodeling axis, without 23 relying on separate datasets or region-level annotations. Integrating 3these cell-level interaction scores with the pseudo-temporal continuum further allowed 4us to examine how epithelial–neighbor interactions varied along the fibrotic remodeling 5trajectory. 6Analysis of epithelial intracellular pathway activity suggested that Androgen and 7Hypoxia pathway activities decreased along the continuum. Sample preparation 6Lung resection was performed as therapeutic management for lung cancer associated 7with IPF at Kyushu University Hospital. Resected specimens were processed as 8formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tissue blocks. From these FFPE specimens, 9we retrospectively selected four patients whose fibrotic portion was pathologically 10 diagnosed as showing a usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP) pattern. Clinical 11 characteristics, surgical procedures, and resection sites are summarized in 12 Supplementary Table 1. For each block, fibrotic areas were identified by pathologists 13 and respiratory physicians. After review of the entire resected specimen, we 14 microdissected regions representing a spectrum of fibrotic remodeling, including 15 relatively preserved alveolar architecture, fibroblast focus-rich areas, and 16 honeycombing, for subsequent Xenium profiling. Based on histopathologic assessment, 17 samples were categorized as fibroblastic focus-rich (FR_s1, FR_s3, FR_s4), 18 honeycombing-dominant (HC_s1, HC_s3), or representing the interface between 19 relatively preserved alveolar architecture and fibrotic remodeling (AF_s2). This study 20 was performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and was approved by the 21 Institutional Review Board of Kyushu University Hospital (approval numbers 23495-22 00). The requirement for written informed consent was waived, and an opt-out method 23 was used because of the retrospective nature of the study. 24

Imputation 14 Imputation refers to predicting the expression of genes that were not directly measured 15 by leveraging gene–gene covariance learned from reference single-cell RNA-seq 16 datasets. As 19 imputation inherently relies on model assumptions and reference data, it can be 20 susceptible to library- and reference-specific biases.
